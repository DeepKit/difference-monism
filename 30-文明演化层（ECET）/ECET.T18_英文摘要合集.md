## A01: Entry Point — What ECET Is, What It Is Not, and Where Its Value Lies

**Abstract**

This document serves as the entry point for all readers of the ECET framework. It identifies three categories of problems that ECET addresses: (1) structural problems of misaligned responsibility and power, where decision-making authority exists but accountability is diffuse; (2) cross-domain collaboration problems, where engineers, lawyers, politicians, and administrators cannot communicate about the same system using a shared conceptual language; and (3) future technology governance problems, where AI and high-speed automation create accountability vacuums that existing legal and institutional frameworks cannot address. The document argues that existing frameworks—subsidiarity principles, accountability gap research, AI ethics declarations—have identified these problems but failed to provide an operationalizable, cross-domain solution. ECET's contribution is not the discovery of new problems but the systematic, structural, and operationalizable integration of existing insights into a unified governance framework.

**Keywords**: accountability gap, shadow sovereignty, cross-domain governance, AI accountability, operationalization

## B03: Three Axioms — Energy Constraint, Adaptive Selection Constraint, Incompleteness Constraint

**Abstract**

This document establishes the foundational axioms of ECET. Three constraints are identified as irreducible and jointly sufficient for characterizing the evolutionary dynamics of any system—physical, cognitive, or social. The energy constraint holds that any system must operate within available resource limits; the adaptive selection constraint holds that any system's behavior must produce positive adaptation or be eliminated; the incompleteness constraint holds that no cognitive system can completely describe its environment (following Gödel's incompleteness theorems and Turing's halting problem). From these three axioms, three governance principles are derived: the responsibility symmetry principle (decision authority must be held by those who bear consequences), the anti-finalization principle (no structure can claim permanent validity), and the AI attribution principle (AI decision authority requires a human organizational bearer). The document also provides lightweight mathematical formalization including difference equations for bias-creativity dynamics and stability conditions for closed-loop systems.

**Keywords**: energy constraint, adaptive selection, incompleteness, responsibility symmetry, anti-finalization, AI attribution

## B05: Deviation and Creativity — The Productive Role of Bias

**Abstract**

This document develops ECET's theory of productive deviation. Contrary to the dominant paradigm of bias elimination, ECET argues that deviation is not a defect but raw material for creativity. The relationship between deviation level and creative output follows an inverted-U curve: zero deviation produces no variation and thus no creativity; maximum deviation produces instability and thus no adaptive value; moderate deviation produces the highest creative output. This mechanism is formalized through the equation $P_t = \gamma \cdot B_t \cdot (1 - B_t)$, where optimal deviation occurs at $B^* = 0.5$. The document classifies deviations into cognitive, behavioral, and systemic types, and derives implications for AI alignment: the goal should not be zero-deviation systems but systems with calibrated deviation budgets and independent verification mechanisms.

**Keywords**: productive deviation, inverted-U curve, creativity mechanism, deviation budget, AI alignment

## B07: Closed-Loop Evolutionary Dynamics — The Three-Layer Cycle

**Abstract**

This document presents ECET's model of closed-loop evolutionary dynamics, structured as three nested cycles: the execution layer (action → immediate feedback), the learning layer (pattern recognition → model update), and the evolutionary layer (rule modification → structural change). The three layers operate at different timescales and require different intervention mechanisms. Failure modes are analyzed for each layer: execution layer failures include feedback delay and signal noise; learning layer failures include overfitting and underfitting; evolutionary layer failures include lock-in and premature convergence. The document argues that human oversight is most critical at the evolutionary layer, where rule modifications can produce irreversible structural changes. This provides the theoretical basis for ECET's position that AI systems require human participation in rule-level decisions.

**Keywords**: closed-loop dynamics, three-layer cycle, execution layer, learning layer, evolutionary layer, human oversight

## B09: Testable Hypotheses — H1 through H10

**Abstract**

This document provides the scientific interface for ECET by deriving ten testable hypotheses from the three axioms. The hypotheses cover: H1 (deviation budget hypothesis: systems with non-zero deviation budgets adapt faster in novel environments), H2 (FREEZE rate hypothesis: the relationship between uncertainty acknowledgment and output quality follows an inverted-U curve), H3 (responsibility symmetry stability hypothesis: organizations with higher decision-consequence symmetry survive longer under external pressure), H4 (independent verification hypothesis: systems with verification-generation separation have lower error rates in safety-critical tasks), H5 (closed-loop speed hypothesis: feedback loop speed and system performance follow an inverted-U curve), H6 (scale boundary hypothesis: single-layer responsibility structures become unstable above a scale threshold), H7 (shadow sovereignty fragility hypothesis: systems with high decision-consequence separation are more likely to collapse under external shocks), H8 (incompleteness acknowledgment hypothesis: systems that acknowledge their limitations achieve higher long-term user trust), H9 (evolutionary layer intervention hypothesis: AI systems with human participation in rule modification maintain value alignment better over time), and H10 (anti-finalization hypothesis: institutional designs with explicit revision mechanisms adapt faster after environmental change). Each hypothesis includes operationalized definitions, verification directions, and explicit falsification conditions.

**Keywords**: testable hypotheses, falsification conditions, deviation budget, FREEZE rate, responsibility symmetry, shadow sovereignty

## C11: AI Agent Safety Architecture — Constraint-Driven Alignment

**Abstract**

This document applies ECET's three axioms to AI agent safety architecture, proposing a constraint-driven alignment strategy based on the principle that output verification is superior to process inspection. The core architecture implements a three-layer constraint system: resource constraints (energy constraint), behavioral contracts (adaptive selection constraint), and uncertainty handling (incompleteness constraint). The operational mechanism is a seven-step cycle: receive request → resource check → contract validation → generation → output verification → FREEZE/PASS/FAIL judgment → feedback. The document argues that the fundamental problem with current AI alignment approaches is their focus on inspecting the generation process rather than verifying outputs against independently specified contracts. ECET's contribution is the theoretical justification for why independent verification is structurally superior: it implements the adaptive selection constraint by creating a筛选 mechanism genuinely independent of the generation source.

**Keywords**: AI safety, constraint-driven alignment, output verification, behavioral contracts, FREEZE mechanism, ODD framework

## C13: Shadow Sovereignty and Governance Principles

**Abstract**

This document develops ECET's governance theory, centered on the concept of shadow sovereignty: structures where decision authority exists but accountability is diffuse or absent. Shadow sovereignty is not a modern invention—the East India Company, bureaucratic delegation chains, and financial derivative structures all created similar accountability vacuums. AI systems represent the latest and most extreme form, characterized by higher speed, larger scale, and more ambiguous boundaries. From ECET's three axioms, three governance principles are derived: (1) responsibility symmetry—decision authority must be held by those who bear consequences; (2) anti-finalization—no structure can claim permanent validity or irrevocable authority; (3) AI attribution—AI systems are not sovereign subjects and must have a human organizational bearer whose dissolution terminates the AI's decision authority. Three operational boundaries are specified: subject identification (dual-layer principal structure with responsibility tracing chains), execution mechanism (multi-layer audit with closed-loop accountability for adjudicators), and scale boundary (hierarchical responsibility triggered above impact thresholds). The document uses structural judgment language throughout, avoiding moral appeals.

**Keywords**: shadow sovereignty, responsibility symmetry, anti-finalization, AI attribution, governance principles, operational boundaries

## C15: Intellectual History — ECET's Origins and Contributions

**Abstract**

This document traces the intellectual genealogy of ECET and clarifies its original contributions. ECET draws from six major intellectual sources: ASTO (Adaptive System Theory of Organization, the immediate predecessor, now repositioned as a "starting point" rather than a parallel theory), Darwinian evolution (adaptive selection mechanism), Gödel's incompleteness theorems (logical foundation for the incompleteness constraint), Kuhn's paradigm theory (framework for understanding theoretical transitions), thermodynamics (physical foundation for the energy constraint), and accountability gap research (empirical motivation for governance applications). ECET's original contributions are identified as: (1) the first systematic operationalization of responsibility symmetry as a structural stability condition rather than a moral claim; (2) the three-layer closed-loop architecture as a unified model for cognitive, organizational, and AI systems; (3) the cross-domain governance framework that provides a common language for engineers, lawyers, politicians, and administrators; and (4) the derivation of AI attribution principles from physical constraints rather than ethical preferences.

**Keywords**: intellectual history, ASTO, Darwin, Gödel, Kuhn, original contributions, cross-domain framework

## C17: Simulation Verification Report — ABM Validation of H1, H2, H4

**Abstract**

This document provides initial empirical support for three ECET hypotheses through agent-based modeling (ABM) simulations. Experiment 1 (H1 validation) varies global constraint strength $C \in [0,1]$ across 50 agents over 1000 time steps, finding a robust inverted-U relationship between constraint strength and creative output, with peak performance at $C \approx 0.4$–$0.6$. Experiment 2 (H2 validation) compares deviation-utilization strategies against deviation-elimination strategies across environments with varying volatility, finding that utilization strategies significantly outperform elimination strategies in high-volatility environments while showing no significant difference in low-volatility environments. Experiment 3 (H4 validation) varies agent cognitive precision from 1.0 to 0.05, finding that exploration diversity increases monotonically with simplification while adaptive fitness follows an inverted-U curve, with peak fitness at precision $\approx 0.4$–$0.6$. All three hypotheses receive empirical support. Limitations include small agent populations, simplified interaction rules, and parameters derived from theoretical reasoning rather than empirical calibration.

**Keywords**: agent-based modeling, simulation verification, inverted-U curve, deviation utilization, cognitive simplification, H1, H2, H4

## One-Page Summary: ECET at a Glance

**Full Name**: Evolutionary Constraint Existential Theory (演化约束存在论)

**Core Claim**: Any system—physical, cognitive, or social—operates under three irreducible constraints. These constraints, rather than being limitations to overcome, are the structural conditions that make existence, adaptation, and creativity possible.

**Three Axioms**:
1. Energy Constraint: Systems must operate within available resource limits
2. Adaptive Selection Constraint: Non-adaptive structures are eliminated
3. Incompleteness Constraint: No cognitive system can completely describe its environment

**Three Governance Principles** (derived, not assumed):
1. Responsibility Symmetry: Decision authority belongs to those who bear consequences
2. Anti-Finalization: No structure can claim permanent validity
3. AI Attribution: AI decision authority requires a human organizational bearer

**What Makes ECET Different**:
- Derives governance principles from physical constraints, not ethical preferences
- Provides a cross-domain common language for engineers, lawyers, politicians, and administrators
- Operationalizes existing insights (subsidiarity, accountability gap research) into actionable boundary conditions
- Addresses AI governance through structural analysis rather than moral appeals

**What ECET Is Not**:
- Not an ethics declaration
- Not an engineering manual
- Not a political position
- Not a claim to have discovered new problems

> ECET's value lies not in the novelty of its ideas but in being the first framework to systematically, structurally, and operationally integrate existing insights into a cross-domain governance framework.
