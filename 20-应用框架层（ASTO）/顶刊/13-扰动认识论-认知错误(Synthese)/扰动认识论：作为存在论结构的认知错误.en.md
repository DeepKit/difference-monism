## Abstract

Contemporary epistemology often treats error as privative—deviation from truth-conducive norms. This generates a persistent tension: finite cognitive systems systematically compress and distort information, yet such deviations appear to be stable features of cognition under energetic and bandwidth constraints. We argue that representational frameworks exacerbate this tension by presupposing an already-individuated subject confronting an already-individuated world, thereby leaving the genesis of the subject–world boundary underexplained.

Drawing on Simondon’s theory of individuation and a non-representational reading of Friston’s Free Energy Principle, we propose a triadic ontology: (1) **Monadic substrate**: a pre-individual metastable field; (2) **Dyadic observation**: perturbation-triggered splitting; (3) **Triadic mediation**: regulation that stabilizes coupling by managing deviation. We then connect individuation to information-theoretic limits. At a fixed quantization scale $\epsilon>0$, any finite coarse-graining of a quantized continuous field leaves strictly positive informational residue (Theorem 3.1), and rate-distortion theory makes explicit the trade-off between finite processing rate and non-zero distortion. Epistemic normativity is accordingly grounded in viability (operational closure under thermodynamic constraints) rather than correspondence. We conclude by addressing instrumentalist and realism-motivated objections, arguing that representational accuracy is best understood as a special regime of low-cost structural coupling.

**Keywords:** Error, Individuation, Free Energy Principle, Non-representationalism, Simondon, Thermodynamic Epistemology, Triadic Ontology, Rate-Distortion Theory, Metastability, Structural Realism

## 1. Introduction: The Error Puzzle

Consider visual perception. The human retina contains on the order of $10^8$ photoreceptors but only $\sim 10^6$ ganglion cells transmitting signals to the brain—a roughly 100:1 bottleneck (Wandell, 1995). This is not a design flaw but a consequence of severe bandwidth and metabolic constraints: early visual processing is under strong pressure to be compressive and therefore lossy. Fine-grained spatial, temporal, and spectral information is therefore largely discarded.

Does this count as error? If so, perception is constitutively erroneous; if not, the privative conception of error is too narrow. Either way, we face a foundational puzzle in epistemology.

Contemporary epistemology operates under a foundational asymmetry: truth is primary, error privative. Error is intelligible solely as deviation from norms of accuracy, reliability, or truth-conduciveness; it lacks positive ontological status. Yet finite cognitive systems—biological, artificial, and human—do not merely tolerate error; they depend upon systematic deviation. Heuristic compression, biased prediction, lossy encoding, and exploratory misalignment are not peripheral defects but architectural necessities imposed by constraints of energy, time, and information (Gigerenzer & Todd, 1999; Simon, 1956).

This generates a foundational dilemma:

1. **The Deficiency Thesis:** If error is merely failure, then finite cognition is structurally defective, systematically falling short of ideal epistemic norms.
2. **The Constitutivity Thesis:** If finite cognition is not defective, then it is hard to treat error as mere failure, which challenges the truth-oriented foundation of epistemology.

Standard responses—whether reliabilist (Goldman, 1986), evidentialist (Conee & Feldman, 2004), or virtue-theoretic (Sosa, 2007)—presuppose that cognition aims at veridical representation of a mind-independent reality. Even "embodied" extensions of this framework, such as predictive processing (PP), retain representational commitments: the brain is construed as a hierarchical prediction machine that minimizes prediction error to infer the causes of sensory states (Friston, 2010; Hohwy, 2013).

We argue that this dominant representational framing makes the constitutive role of error difficult to accommodate. To address the puzzle, we therefore set aside the premise that cognition consists in mirroring a pre-given world. Instead, we propose a **thermodynamic epistemology** grounded in a **triadic ontology** wherein cognition emerges through **individuation**—the historical process of system-environment differentiation through perturbation. Error is reconceptualized not as representational failure but as the structural residue of individuation: the inevitable cost of compressing continuous field-couplings into discrete object-distinctions.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 argues that representationalism is difficult to reconcile with constitutive error. Section 3 introduces the triadic ontology and proves the Compression Theorem, supporting the claim that any finite coarse-graining leaves strictly positive informational residue. Section 4 develops thermodynamic epistemology, reconceiving justification and truth. Section 5 applies the framework to biological and artificial systems. Section 6 addresses objections. Section 7 concludes.

## 2. The Representational Impasse

### 2.1 The Vicious Circle of Representational Normativity

Any representational theory of cognition, whether classical computationalism (Fodor, 1975) or contemporary predictive processing (Clark, 2016), is typically committed to three core claims:

**(R1) Content Determination:** Mental states have representational content determined by some mind-world relation (causal covariance, informational correlation, structural correspondence, etc.). To represent *F* is to stand in this relation to *F*.

**(R2) Normativity:** Representations have correctness conditions. Correctness conditions require the possibility of misrepresentation—representing *F* when *G* obtains. This normativity is explanatorily prior: it grounds the system's behavior.

**(R3) Explanatory Priority:** The content of representations explains cognitive capacities and behavior. To believe that *p* is to be disposed to act as if *p* obtains.

These commitments generate what we call **The Vicious Circle of Representational Normativity**:

**Step 1:** Content is individuated by accuracy conditions (R1). To represent electron trajectories is to have states whose function is to covary with actual trajectories.

**Step 2:** Systematic deviation from accuracy conditions constitutes malfunction (R2). A detector that systematically fires in the absence of electrons is broken.

**Step 3:** But finite systems systematically deviate (empirical fact). Visual compression discards information; heuristics introduce bias; neural networks exhibit adversarial brittleness.

**Step 4:** If these deviations are systematic (not random noise), it is difficult to treat them as malfunctions; they appear to be structural features (architectural constraints).

**Step 5:** But if they are structural features, they do not violate accuracy conditions—they partially constitute what it is for the system to represent at all.

**Step 6:** Therefore, systematic deviations are not errors (by R2's definition of error as deviation from content-determining conditions).

**Conclusion:** Either (a) finite cognition seems systematically defective (violating the intuition that functional cognitive systems are not broken), or (b) systematic deviations are not errors (violating R2's normative commitment).

This is not merely a contingent engineering trade-off; it reflects a conceptual tension internal to the representational framing. If error is constitutive of the system's representational capacity, it is hard to see how it can simultaneously be what representation is defined against.

### 2.2 Why Predictive Processing Retains the Problem

One might hope that predictive processing (PP), with its emphasis on prediction error minimization, escapes this impasse. PP models the brain as a hierarchical generative model that infers the hidden causes of sensory input by minimizing prediction error—the mismatch between predicted and actual sensory signals (Friston, 2010; Clark, 2016). Prediction error here appears to play a positive, driving role: it is the learning signal that updates the model.

However, as Gładziejewski (2016) argues, PP requires **structural representations**—internal models that preserve the relational structure of distal causes—to explain systematic success. Jakob Hohwy (2013) makes this explicit: the brain's generative model is taken to track the causal structure of the world; predictive success depends on structural correspondence between model and world.

This reinstates (R1)-(R3):

**(R1-PP):** Content is determined by the generative model's causal-structural features. To represent *X* is to possess a model whose structure mirrors *X*'s causal organization.

**(R2-PP):** Correctness consists in structural isomorphism between model and world. Prediction error signals deviation from this isomorphism.

**(R3-PP):** Behavior is explained by the model's content: organisms act to confirm predictions generated by their models of the world.

Crucially, PP treats prediction error as a **transient signal**—valuable only insofar as it drives learning toward more accurate models. The ideal endpoint is zero prediction error: a model that perfectly captures the world's statistical structure. Error remains instrumentally necessary (you need it to learn) but ontologically derivative (its purpose is its own elimination).

**The Core Problem:** If compression is thermodynamically necessary (as we argue in §3), then the "ideal" of zero prediction error is not physically attainable, in general, for finite systems. A finite brain operating under metabolic constraints does not, in general, sustain unbounded representational precision. The systematic deviation introduced by compression is therefore not merely a temporary learning phase but a persistent structural feature.

Therefore, PP faces the same dilemma: either this persistent deviation is malfunction (implying functional brains are broken), or it is not error (violating R2-PP's normativity). The representational framework thus struggles to accommodate constitutive error, because error is defined as deviation from the very conditions that ground representational content.

### 2.3 The Deeper Issue: The Fallacy of Presupposed Individuation

The representational impasse reveals a deeper problem: representationalism arguably presupposes what it aims to explain. To speak of "internal representations" and "external world" already assumes a boundary between system and environment—an individuation that distinguishes "in here" from "out there." But how does this boundary arise?

Classical theories simply stipulate it: the organism is a pre-given entity that then acquires representational states. But this risks question-begging—the **Fallacy of Presupposed Individuation**. Before individuation, there is no determinate "organism" to which we can ascribe representations. And if individuation is itself a cognitive achievement (as we argue), then representation is ill-suited to ground cognition—it appears to presuppose it.

This is the ontological version of the Error Puzzle: representation requires a subject-object distinction, but the genesis of this distinction is not plausibly representational (on pain of infinite regress). We need an account of how boundaries emerge before we can intelligibly speak of what those boundaries represent.

## 3. The Triadic Ontology of Individuation

### 3.1 Beyond the Subject-Object Dichotomy

Traditional epistemology begins with an already-individuated subject confronting an already-individuated world of objects. This **dyadic structure**—subject ↔ object—is taken as foundational. But this starting point makes the emergence of individuation harder to explain.

We propose a **triadic ontology** that replaces this starting point:

#### **Layer 1: Monadic Substrate (Pre-individual Field)**
Before any subject-object distinction, there exists a metastable field of potentials. This is not "nothing" but a structured domain of tendencies, gradients, and tensions that have not yet resolved into discrete entities. Gilbert Simondon (1958/2017) called this the *préindividuel*—a domain that is neither one nor many, neither subject nor object, but contains the potentials for individuation.

**Example (Biological):** Consider embryonic development. The fertilized egg is genetically homogeneous—all cells contain identical DNA. Yet spatial gradients (morphogen concentrations varying along axes) constitute a *pre-pattern* that will drive differentiation. Before differentiation occurs, there are no discrete organs, yet the potential for heart-lung-liver distinction is structurally present in the gradient field. The field is metastable: it contains internal tensions that tend to resolve into discrete structures, but which structure emerges depends on perturbation.

**Example (Philosophical):** Consider Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason*. On one familiar reading, the *thing-in-itself* (Ding an sich) designates what is not yet available as an object under the forms and categories of experience. In the present idiom, it can function as an analogy for a monadic substrate: a pre-individuated domain prior to the subject–object split. (The point here is illustrative rather than exegetical.)
#### **Layer 2: Dyadic Observation (Perturbation and Splitting)**
A perturbation—a deviation exceeding the system's assimilation threshold—can force symmetry-breaking. The field may no longer maintain its metastability and tends to settle into some resolution. This settling is not the act of a pre-existing agent but the very process through which agent and environment co-emerge. The observer-observed split is not a relation between two pre-existing poles but the result of individuation itself.

**Formalization (Threshold Dynamics):** Let $S$ be the system, $E$ the environment, and $\Delta = f(S, E)$ the interaction magnitude (coupling intensity). Let $\tau$ be the system's assimilation threshold. Cognition $C$ emerges as:
$$C = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } \Delta < \tau \quad \text{(Transparent / Ready-to-hand)} \\ 1 & \text{if } \Delta \geq \tau \quad \text{(Cognized / Present-at-hand)} \end{cases}$$

This formalization captures Heidegger's (1927) phenomenological insight: when tools function smoothly, they remain "transparent"—we *use* them without *cognizing* them. Only when they malfunction (perturbation exceeds threshold) do they become explicit objects of awareness. **Cognition need not be modeled as continuous monitoring; it can instead be treated as threshold-triggered objectification.** The hammer becomes visible when it breaks.

**Example (Biological - Dyadic Split):** During thymic selection, developing T-cells encounter self-antigens. Those that bind too strongly are eliminated (negative selection), while those that bind moderately are retained. The "self" is not discovered but **constituted** through this historical process of encounter (Pradeu, 2012; Thompson, 2007). The immune system is not an observer with a prior concept of self looking at the world; it becomes an observer through the perturbations that force it to distinguish self from non-self.

**Example (Philosophical - Dyadic Split):** In Kant's framework, one can treat the "transcendental aesthetic" as describing how an initially unstructured manifold is synthesized under the forms of space and time. This yields the **dyadic** split between the phenomenal object (the appearance structured by categories) and the observing subject (the transcendental unity of apperception). On this reading, the phenomenon is not the thing-in-itself as such, but the thing-in-itself as disclosed under a boundary-forming synthesis.

#### **Layer 3: Triadic Mediation (Stabilization Through Error Management)**
The dyadic split is unstable—it requires ongoing energetic maintenance. Without a mediating structure, the boundary would collapse back into the field. The third element is a **regulatory system** that manages the boundary's permeability, deciding which perturbations to assimilate (maintaining continuity) and which to resist (maintaining distinction). This regulatory system is what we call **error management**: the strategic control of deviation to maintain viable coupling.

**Example (Biological - Triadic Mediation):** Peripheral regulatory T-cells (Tregs) and cytokine networks modulate the intensity of immune responses. They constitute the **triadic** layer: they do not create the self/non-self distinction (Layer 2), nor do they constitute the raw potential of reactivity (Layer 1), but they manage the **errors** of autoimmunity—controlling when low-level self-reactivity (cheap error) is tolerated for surveillance purposes versus when it is actively suppressed to prevent tissue damage (expensive error).

**Example (Philosophical - Triadic Mediation):** Kant's "transcendental logic" and the regulative use of ideas of reason can be read as playing a triadic role: they regulate the coupling between understanding and sensibility by constraining the scope of legitimate application. The "antinomies of pure reason" then illustrate what happens when boundaries are mismanaged—when the understanding applies categories beyond their proper domain (expensive errors). In this (admittedly schematic) sense, critical philosophy functions as an error-management framework for boundary-setting.

### 3.2 Formalizing the Triadic Structure

We can formalize these three layers using the framework of stochastic dynamical systems and Markov blankets (Friston et al., 2015), but reinterpreted non-representationally.

**Layer 1: The Field as Phase Space**

**Definition 3.1 (Field).** The pre-individual field is a continuous state space $\mathcal{X}$ equipped with:
- A probability measure $\mu$ over trajectories (representing the field's dynamics)
- A metric $d: \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^+$ (representing distinguishability)

For concreteness, consider $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^n$ with Euclidean metric, though the argument generalizes to infinite-dimensional spaces.

The field's dynamics are governed by a stochastic differential equation:
$$d\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{x}) dt + \Sigma(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{W}$$
where $f$ is the deterministic flow and $\Sigma d\mathbf{W}$ represents stochastic perturbations (Brownian motion).

**Layer 2: Markov Blanket as Dyadic Splitting**

**Definition 3.2 (Markov Blanket).** A partition of $\mathcal{X}$ into four sets: internal states $\eta$, external states $b$, sensory states $s$, and active states $a$, such that internal and external states are conditionally independent given the blanket states $(s,a)$:
$$\eta \perp b \mid (s, a).$$

One common factorization consistent with this separation is, for example,
$$p(\eta, s, a, b) = p(\eta \mid s, a)\, p(s \mid b)\, p(a \mid \eta)\, p(b).$$

**Reinterpretation:** This is not an epistemic boundary (separating "beliefs" from "world") but a **thermodynamic boundary**—a partition that mediates system–environment coupling while maintaining internal organization. The blanket does not represent the external; it regulates coupling by managing which perturbations trigger bifurcation (expensive errors) versus assimilation (cheap errors).

**Layer 3: Active Inference as Error Management**

The system maintains its blanket by selectively responding to sensory deviations:

$$\dot{a} = -\nabla_a F(s, a)$$

where $F(s, a)$ is variational free energy (an upper bound on surprise). On the present reading, this is not Bayesian inference toward accurate representation but **homeostatic regulation**—adjusting actions to keep sensory states within expected bounds (Ramstead et al., 2023). The system manages not truth, but the **cost of surprise**.

**Definition 3.3 (Informational Residue: Boundary Formation).** Fix a quantization scale $\epsilon > 0$ and let $X$ be a random variable over field states in $\mathcal{X}$. Let $X_\epsilon = Q_\epsilon(X)$ denote $X$ quantized at resolution $\epsilon$ (so $X_\epsilon$ is a discrete source). Let $B$ be any finite-valued boundary variable induced by the system's blanket (e.g., a coarse-graining of $X_\epsilon$ determined by the Markov blanket).

The **informational residue** of boundary formation at scale $\epsilon$ is:
$$\mathrm{Res}_\epsilon := H(X_\epsilon \mid B)$$
where $H$ is Shannon entropy. This residue is not misrepresentation but the uncertainty that remains when continuous coupling is forced through a finite interface. Definition 3.5 below specializes this to residue induced by a specific coarse-graining $Y=g(X_\epsilon)$.

### 3.3 The Compression Theorem and Rate-Distortion: Error as Necessity

We now prove a formal result showing that, at a fixed quantization scale $\epsilon>0$, any finite coarse-graining leaves strictly positive informational residue. Together with rate-distortion theory, this yields a technical sense in which some deviation persists for finite systems individuating from continuous fields.

#### 3.3.1 Informal Statement

The argument proceeds in three steps:
1. Individuation requires boundaries (operational closure)
2. Boundaries require finite partitions (thermodynamic constraint)
3. Finite coarse-grainings of a quantized continuous field entail information loss (Compression Theorem)

**Intuitive Explanation:** The theorem's essence is that any mapping of a continuum (the real number line) into finite intervals (like integers on a grid) discards the within-interval location of points. Cognitive "categories" and "concepts" can be modeled as such finite partitions imposed upon the continuous flux of experience.

As an analogy, Mandelbrot (1967) notes that measured coastline length depends on measurement resolution; a finite description does not preserve arbitrarily fine distinctions. Likewise, finite cognition imposes finite partitions on a space of potentially indefinite distinctions. The residual uncertainty within each cell is not a contingent mistake but a structural by-product of finite coarse-graining.

#### 3.3.2 The Formal Argument: Compression Theorem

**Theorem 3.1 (Compression Theorem: Necessary Information Loss under Finite Coarse-Graining).** Let $X$ be an $\mathbb{R}^n$-valued random variable with density $p(x)$ (with respect to Lebesgue measure) whose support is unbounded. Fix a quantization scale $\epsilon > 0$ and let $X_\epsilon = Q_\epsilon(X)$ be a measurable quantizer, yielding a discrete source. Let $g$ be any measurable map from the support of $X_\epsilon$ into a finite set $\{1,\ldots,k\}$, and define $Y := g(X_\epsilon)$. Then:
$$H(X_\epsilon \mid Y) > 0.$$[^1]

[^1]: **Technical Note (Quantization and Measurability):** To avoid the gauge dependence of differential entropy, we formulate the theorem at a fixed resolution $\epsilon > 0$ using the Shannon entropy of the quantized source $X_\epsilon$. We assume $p$ is a density with respect to Lebesgue measure, $Q_\epsilon$ is measurable, and $g$ (equivalently the induced partition) is Lebesgue measurable. We also assume finite energy (e.g., $\int \|x\|^2 p(x) dx < \infty$) so that the Shannon entropy of the quantized source is well-defined for finite $\epsilon$. See Cover & Thomas (2006, Ch. 8).

**Proof:** Since $Y$ is a deterministic function of $X_\epsilon$, we have:
$$H(X_\epsilon) = H(Y) + H(X_\epsilon \mid Y).$$
Suppose for contradiction that $H(X_\epsilon \mid Y)=0$. Then $X_\epsilon$ is a function of $Y$ almost surely, so $X_\epsilon$ can take at most $k$ values with positive probability.

But because the support of $X$ is unbounded, $X_\epsilon$ has infinitely many quantization outcomes with positive probability (for arbitrarily large radii there exist quantization cells with non-zero mass). Therefore $X_\epsilon$ cannot be a finite-valued function of $Y$ when $k<\infty$. Contradiction. Hence $H(X_\epsilon \mid Y) > 0$. □

**Corollary 3.1 (Structural Residue is Necessary):** Any finite cognitive system that individuates by mapping a continuous coupling into finitely many categories (finite $k$) inherits strictly positive informational residue $H(X_\epsilon \mid Y)$. This is the informational signature of structural residue; §3.3.3 turns it into a quantitative trade-off between finite processing rate ($R$) and residual distortion ($D$).

#### 3.3.3 Rate-Distortion Theory: Quantifying the Epistemological Cost

While the Compression Theorem establishes that loss is necessary, **Rate-Distortion Theory** (Shannon, 1948; Cover & Thomas, 2006) allows us to quantify the relationship between resource constraints and acceptable error. This bridges the gap between information theory and epistemology.

For a finite system, the **rate** $R$—the information processing capacity—is constrained by energetic and physical limits; Landauer's principle provides a lower bound on dissipation per bit of irreversible computation (Landauer, 1961). Rate-distortion theory states that for a given channel capacity $R$, there exists a minimum achievable **distortion** $D$ (expected error) when compressing a continuous source.

**The Rate-Distortion Function:**
$$R(D) = \min_{p(\hat{x}|x): \mathbb{E}[d(X,\hat{X})] \leq D} I(X; \hat{X})$$

Where:
- $I(X; \hat{X})$ is the mutual information between the field $X$ and its compressed representation $\hat{X}$
- $d(X, \hat{X})$ is a distortion measure (e.g., squared error)
- $D$ is the maximum allowable distortion

**Critical Epistemological Implication:** For any finite rate $R < \infty$, the achievable distortion $D$ is non-zero. Conversely, achieving zero distortion ($D = 0$) requires infinite rate ($R \to \infty$), which is incompatible with thermodynamic constraints (Landauer's principle).

**Definition 3.4 (Error as Distortion):** We formally identify **error** with the distortion $D$ inherent in the compression of the continuous field into discrete cognitive categories. Error, in this sense, is not misrepresentation but the distortion inherent in finite-rate compression.

**The Thermodynamic Constraint:** By Landauer's principle, erasing (or otherwise irreversibly manipulating) one bit of information requires at least $k_B T \ln 2$ of heat dissipation. Therefore, driving distortion to zero would require unbounded energetic expenditure as resolution increases—a condition incompatible with finite biological or artificial systems.

**Conclusion:** Error-free cognition (in the sense of zero distortion under finite rate and energetic constraints) is thermodynamically infeasible: for any finite rate $R$, the achievable distortion is bounded away from zero. In this respect, error is not an epistemic failure to be eliminated but a physical constraint to be managed.

### 3.4 Error as Structural Residue

We can now characterize structural residue at a finite resolution.

**Definition 3.5 (Informational Residue under Coarse-Graining).** Fix a quantization scale $\epsilon > 0$ and a finite coarse-graining $Y = g(X_\epsilon)$ with $|\mathrm{range}(Y)| = k < \infty$. The informational residue of this coarse-graining is:
$$\mathrm{Res}_\epsilon(g) := H(X_\epsilon \mid Y).$$

**Key Properties:**
1. **Positivity:** $\mathrm{Res}_\epsilon(g) > 0$ for any finite coarse-graining (Theorem 3.1).
2. **Context-Dependence:** Different coarse-grainings $g$ yield different residues; there is no uniquely "correct" partition, only partitions that are more or less viable given constraints.
3. **Irreducibility:** At finite $k$ and finite $\epsilon$, residue is ineliminable; it can only be redistributed.

Rate-distortion theory provides the complementary energetic reading: for any finite processing rate $R$, the minimum achievable distortion $D_{\min}(R)$ is strictly non-zero (Definition 3.4). The residue above captures the ineliminable information-theoretic aspect of this constraint.

### 3.5 Structural Residue vs. Operational Collapse

A crucial distinction is useful here between two fundamentally different types of "error":

**Definition 3.6 (Structural Residue).** The ontologically necessary background distortion inherent in any finite individuation from a continuous field. Formally, at a finite resolution $\epsilon > 0$ and for any finite coarse-graining $Y = g(X_\epsilon)$, structural residue is captured by the strictly positive informational residue $\mathrm{Res}_\epsilon(g)=H(X_\epsilon \mid Y)$ (Theorem 3.1). It is not eliminated by better design; it can only be managed. Rate-distortion theory then quantifies the residual distortion that remains for any finite rate $R$ (Definition 3.4).

**Definition 3.7 (Operational Collapse).** A failure of the regulatory system (Layer 3) to manage structural residue within viable bounds, resulting in the system's trajectory toward the boundary of its viability envelope. This is what traditional epistemology calls "error"—a malfunction that threatens operational closure.

**Example:** Consider visual perception. The 100:1 compression ratio of the retina (§1) constitutes structural residue—information about fine-grained spatial patterns is irreversibly discarded. This is not a "mistake" but the thermodynamic cost of vision. In contrast, when a person misidentifies a harmless rope as a dangerous snake, this may constitute operational collapse if it triggers a costly stress response that depletes metabolic resources without functional benefit.

**Example:** Consider mathematical reasoning. When a student miscalculates $7 \times 8 = 54$, this is not structural residue (the field of arithmetic relations is discrete, not continuous). Rather, it is operational collapse—a failure of the regulatory system to maintain coherent coupling with the normative structure of arithmetic. The "error" here is not ontologically necessary but reflects a malfunction in the system's boundary-maintenance procedures.

**The Relationship:**
$$\text{Structural Residue} \subset \text{Background Condition}$$
$$\text{Operational Collapse} = \text{Failure to Manage Residue}$$

Operational collapse occurs not because structural residue exists (it always does), but because the triadic regulatory layer fails to keep that residue **cheap**. A well-functioning cognitive system does not eliminate error; it **manages error economically**, ensuring that irreducible distortion does not cascade into boundary dissolution.

**Implication for Normativity:** Traditional epistemic normativity ("believe truly!") conflates these two categories. Our framework replaces it with a more nuanced norm: **manage your structural residue to avoid operational collapse**. A cognition is "good" not when it eliminates error altogether but when it maintains viable coupling despite irreducible distortion.

### 3.6 The Dynamic Transformation of Error Types

Crucially, structural residue and operational collapse are not static categories but exist in **dynamic transformation**. The same deviation can shift from one type to another depending on context and the system's regulatory response.

**Definition 3.8 (Productive Error).** A deviation that, while constituting structural residue, enables expanded coupling capacity—generating new distinctions, hypotheses, or behavioral repertoires. Examples include: metaphorical reasoning ("errors" of literal meaning enabling conceptual innovation), exploratory behavior (deviations from optimal paths enabling discovery), and scientific conjecture (hypotheses that are "wrong" but generatively fruitful).

**Definition 3.9 (Transition Threshold).** The critical point at which a productive error transforms into operational collapse:
$$\Theta_{\text{transition}} = \{\delta : C_{\text{maintain}}(\delta) = C_{\text{reorganize}}(\delta)\}$$
where $C_{\text{maintain}}$ is the cost of maintaining the current boundary configuration despite deviation $\delta$, and $C_{\text{reorganize}}$ is the cost of boundary reorganization.

**Why Systems Resist Reorganization (Energetics of Boundary Change):**
Resistance to updating—what we might call "stubbornness"—can be modeled as an energetic constraint. Reorganizing a boundary (relearning, rewiring, reparameterizing) requires dissipative work to build and stabilize new regularities, and so competes with other metabolic and time budgets. Systems therefore often prefer incremental accommodation of anomalies until the cumulative cost of maintaining the current boundary exceeds the one-time cost of reorganization.

This provides one way to interpret Kuhn's (1962) account of paradigm change: isolated anomalies can be accommodated as cheap errors, whereas sustained anomalies can make maintenance increasingly expensive, eventually making reorganization comparatively economical. On this reading, the transition threshold is crossed when the costs of continued accommodation exceed the costs of boundary reorganization—not necessarily when a timeless correspondence relation is "revealed."

**The Transformation Dynamic:**
- **Productive → Destructive:** When a productive error (e.g., a successful heuristic) is **rigidified** into dogma, refusing to update when field dynamics change, it crosses the transition threshold and becomes operational collapse. The once-adaptive compression becomes a trap.
- **Destructive → Productive:** When an operational collapse (e.g., a logical contradiction) is **recognized and exploited**—as in reductio ad absurdum reasoning or artistic provocation—it can be transformed into a productive error that drives reorganization.

**Example (AI Hallucination):** Large language models generate "hallucinations"—confident assertions about non-existent entities. In creative writing contexts (low-stakes coupling), these are **productive errors**: they expand the space of narrative possibilities. In medical diagnosis contexts (high-stakes coupling), the same hallucinations constitute **operational collapse**: they trigger catastrophic boundary failures. The error type is context-dependent, not intrinsic to the deviation itself.

**Engineering Implication:** The goal is not to eliminate all deviation but to **maintain error fluidity**—preventing productive errors from hardening into destructive dogmas, while creating channels for destructive errors to be recognized and transformed into productive insights.

## 4. Thermodynamic Epistemology

If error is ontologically necessary, how do we distinguish "good" cognition from "bad"? The answer requires shifting from correspondence-based truth to **viability-based normativity**.

### 4.1 From Truth to Viability

**Traditional Question:** "Does my belief correspond to reality?"  
**Our Question:** "Can my boundary survive coupling with the field?"

**Definition 4.1 (Viability).** A boundary system is viable if it maintains autopoiesis—self-production of its organizational structure—under dissipative constraints (Maturana & Varela, 1987; Varela et al., 1991; Thompson, 2007). Formally, viability is remaining within a bounded region of phase space (the "viability envelope") despite perturbations.

**Definition 4.2 (Expensive Error).** A perturbation that forces the system toward the boundary of its viability envelope, requiring high energetic expenditure (high variational free energy) to restore operational closure.

**Definition 4.3 (Cheap Error).** A controlled perturbation that triggers adaptive bifurcation—reorganization expanding the system's dynamic repertoire while maintaining low energetic cost.

**The Epistemic Turn:** A cognitive operation is **good** not because it represents accurately but because it maintains viable coupling at low thermodynamic cost. It is **bad** if it triggers dissipative cascades threatening operational closure.

### 4.1.1 Metastability and Epistemic Normativity: Beyond Mere Survival

**Objection:** "Viruses survive. Prions propagate. Are they cognitive systems with epistemology? Your viability criterion seems too permissive."

**Response:** We distinguish **simple persistence** from **metastable individuation**. The crucial difference lies not in survival per se, but in the system's relationship to error:

**Definition 4.1a (Metastable Cognition).** A system exhibits metastable cognition if it:
1. Maintains operational closure (viability)
2. Remains poised to *exploit* perturbations that expand its coupling capacity, rather than merely suppressing deviations
3. Transduces structural residue into **productive errors** that reorganize and expand its viability envelope (in Simondon's sense of metastability)

Viruses and prions plausibly satisfy only (1). They persist by replicating a relatively fixed boundary configuration; they lack mechanisms for exploiting perturbations to expand a coupling repertoire in the way paradigmatic cognitive systems do. In this sense they are **stable** rather than **metastable**: their persistence does not depend on converting perturbations into reorganizations that enlarge a viability envelope.

**The Metastability Criterion:** Epistemic normativity is grounded not merely in "not collapsing" but in **maintaining the capacity to accommodate higher-entropy errors while converting them into low-cost couplings**. A cognitive system is normatively superior not when it avoids all disturbance, but when it can **tolerate greater perturbation regimes** without operational collapse.

**Schematic Expression (Heuristic):**
$$\text{Epistemic Progress} = \frac{d(\text{Viability Envelope Volume})}{d(\text{Perturbation Entropy})} > 0$$
This expression is a heuristic proxy: it captures the idea that epistemic progress consists in expanding the viability envelope relative to perturbation complexity, not in converging on a single, maximal representation.

Knowledge advances when a system can **contain** higher-entropy errors—more complex, more varied, more unpredictable perturbations—while maintaining viable coupling. In Simondonian terms, progress tracks the system's capacity for **transduction**: reorganizing boundaries so that previously expensive perturbations become cheap. The progression from Newtonian to Einsteinian to quantum mechanics can be reconstructed, on our view, as an **expansion of the perturbation regimes** that physics can accommodate without theoretical collapse.

**The Virus Contrast:** A virus lacks mechanisms for reorganizing a boundary in response to perturbations it fails to assimilate; it either replicates or fails to persist. A cognitive system, by contrast, can **reorganize its boundaries** to assimilate previously unassimilable perturbations. This reorganization capacity—the ability to undergo productive bifurcation rather than mere persistence—is what distinguishes epistemic systems from merely surviving ones.

### 4.2 Reinterpreting the Free Energy Principle: Regulatory Structure vs. Model

The Free Energy Principle (FEP) states that self-organizing systems minimize variational free energy—an upper bound on surprise (Friston, 2010):
$$F = \mathbb{E}_{q(\eta|s)}[\log q(\eta|s) - \log p(s, \eta)] = D_{KL}(q(\eta|s) \| p(\eta|s)) - \log p(s)$$

This formalism admits (at least) two distinct philosophical readings:

1. **Representational (Bayesian) reading:** $q(\eta|s)$ is a generative *model of the world*, $F$ is an epistemic objective, and minimizing $F$ is approximate Bayesian inference toward accurate representation (Hohwy, 2013; Clark, 2016).
2. **Regulatory (thermodynamic) reading (ours):** $q(\eta|s)$ is a set of internal constraints that stabilize boundary dynamics, $F$ tracks the energetic burden of maintaining the Markov blanket, and minimizing $F$ is homeostatic regulation of viable coupling.

Our claim is modal rather than exegetical: we do not argue that FEP's originators *intend* the second reading, but that the mathematics does not require representational commitments.

Standard interpretations construe this as Bayesian inference: the system optimizes its generative model $q(\eta|s)$ to match the true posterior $p(\eta|s)$ (Hohwy, 2013). Here, the internal states $\eta$ are treated as representations (hypotheses) about external causes.

**Our Reinterpretation:** On the present reading, $q(\eta|s)$ is not treated as an "inference" about external states $b$. Instead, internal states $\eta$ are interpreted as **dynamic skeletons** or **regulatory structures**—mechanisms that modulate Markov blanket dynamics rather than map external states.

On the regulatory reading, free energy minimization is not primarily about representing the world accurately but about **maintaining blanket integrity at minimal thermodynamic cost** (Ramstead et al., 2023). The system need not track external states; it can be interpreted as regulating the boundary between internal and external to keep surprise bounded.

**Objection (Mere Relabeling):** "Is this not just replacing the word 'belief' with 'regulator' while keeping the same representational story?"

**Reply:** The difference is substantive because it relocates normativity. On the representational reading, prediction errors are *semantic* deviations from correctness conditions, and success is (asymptotic) accuracy. On the regulatory reading, prediction errors are *physiological* deviations from viable setpoints, and success is maintaining an energetically affordable sensorimotor regime. This changes what the framework predicts: a system can reduce free energy by action (active inference) that restores expected sensory states without improving any correspondence relation to external causes.

**Example (Thermoregulation):** An organism minimizes surprise by seeking shade when overheated. Nothing in this requires that $\eta$ encodes a veridical model of ambient temperature; it requires only that deviations in thermal sensory states are treated as costly and that action restores them. The "model" here is a control structure (setpoints, gains, and boundary permeability), not a representation with truth conditions.

**Revised Formulation:**
- **Internal states $\eta$:** Not "beliefs" or "models" but **boundary tension regulators**—parameters governing the stiffness and permeability of the Markov blanket.
- **Free Energy $F$:** Not "prediction error" to be eliminated, but **metabolic cost**—the energy required to maintain the blanket against dissipative pressure from the field.
- **Minimization:** Not "inference to truth" but **homeostatic regulation**—adjusting the boundary to minimize the energy cost of maintaining operational closure.

**Key Difference:**
- **Representational FEP:** Minimize error → accurate model → truth
- **Thermodynamic FEP:** Minimize energy → viable boundary → persistence

The mathematics is identical, but the ontology differs. The generative model $q(\eta|s)$ is not a representation of hidden causes but a **regulatory structure** that modulates the blanket's response to sensory perturbations. On this view, "knowledge" is not a map-territory correspondence but **the system being in a low free energy state of steady coupling**—a specific regime where perturbations are absorbed cheaply without structural collapse.

### 4.3 Epistemic Justification as Perturbation Responsiveness

**Traditional Justification:** A belief is justified if it has appropriate evidential support (Conee & Feldman, 2004) or reliably tracks truth (Goldman, 1986).

**Our Reconception:** A cognition is justified if it maintains **perturbation responsiveness**—the capacity to undergo productive bifurcations when the field presents conflicting dynamics.

**Definition 4.4 (Epistemic Justification).** A boundary configuration $\mathcal{B}$ is justified relative to field dynamics $\mathcal{F}$ if:
$$\frac{\partial F(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{F})}{\partial \mathcal{F}} < \varepsilon_v$$
where $\varepsilon_v$ is a viability threshold (distinct from the quantization scale $\epsilon$ used in §3) and $F$ is variational free energy.

In words: A cognition is justified if small changes in field dynamics produce proportional changes in boundary organization (sensitivity) without triggering collapse (stability).

**Example:** Consider Newtonian mechanics. The question "Is it true?" is not the most informative framing on our view. Instead, we ask how well the framework sustains low-cost coupling within a given perturbation regime:

- **18th–19th centuries:** Newtonian mechanics was highly justified—it maintained low-cost coupling to a wide range of mechanical phenomena. Perturbations (new observations) were easily assimilated through parameter adjustment.
- **Early 20th century:** Phenomena at high velocities and strong gravitational fields became perturbations that Newtonian mechanics could not assimilate cheaply. The energetic cost (theoretical contortions, ad hoc hypotheses) skyrocketed.
- **Post-Einstein:** General relativity provided a boundary reorganization that assimilated these perturbations at lower cost.

Newtonian mechanics need not be framed primarily as "falsified by being untrue"—it can also be understood as superseded because it became increasingly costly to maintain low-cost coupling in certain regimes relative to an alternative. Its "justification" was relative to a regime of field dynamics, not to a timeless correspondence ideal.

### 4.4 Addressing the Instrumentalism Objection: Adaptive Illusion vs. Structural Coupling

**Objection:** "Your account makes knowledge purely pragmatic. If a false belief enhances survival, is it knowledge? Consider religious beliefs that enhance group cohesion but systematically misrepresent reality."

**Response:** We distinguish sharply between **adaptive illusion** and **structural coupling**:

**Adaptive Illusion:** A boundary configuration that enhances short-term functional success while decoupling the system from the field's dynamics. Such systems tend to lack perturbation sensitivity: when the field changes, they may fail to bifurcate adaptively, risking catastrophic failure. This is the domain of "useful fictions" and, in the extreme, eliminative materialism's "propositional attitudes" (Churchland, 1981)—cognitive states that may have evolutionary utility but no ontological standing as knowledge.

**Structural Coupling:** A boundary configuration that maintains **operational closure while remaining open to perturbation**. The system does not merely "work" in the current context; it maintains the capacity to reorganize when the field changes. This requires not just functional utility but **historical entanglement** with the field's dynamics.

**The Critical Difference:** A belief that is functionally useful but **decoupled from field dynamics**—what we might call a "convenient fiction" or "adaptive illusion"—lacks the recursive structure that would allow it to adapt to novel perturbations. It is a rigid boundary, not a viable one.

**Example:** Religious dietary laws (e.g., kosher rules) may have originated as adaptive responses to specific perturbations (parasite avoidance in Bronze Age context). If they maintain their form rigidly (adaptive illusion), they become **expensive errors** when field dynamics shift (modern food safety regulations render some prohibitions redundant while creating new risks—antibiotic-resistant bacteria—unaddressed by ancient rules). They lack true perturbation sensitivity.

In contrast, scientific practices maintain perturbation sensitivity through institutionalized error-checking: peer review, replication, falsification attempts. These can be understood not only as mechanisms for approaching truth, but also as practices that maintain **low-cost responsiveness** to changing perturbation regimes. Scientific concepts are "good" in this respect insofar as they remain **structurally coupled**—maintaining viability through active engagement with perturbations rather than rigidly insulating themselves from them.

**Why This is Not Instrumentalism:** Traditional instrumentalism (e.g., pragmatism) identifies truth with success. Our view identifies truth with **sustained viability through historical coupling**. A systematically false belief may be instrumentally successful temporarily, but it lacks the structural stability to remain viable under changing conditions. This is an objective, thermodynamic constraint, not a subjective preference.

## 5. Case Studies: Error in Biological and Artificial Systems

### 5.1 The Immune System: Optimal Error Rates and Individuation

The immune system provides a paradigmatic example of our triadic framework in action. It does not "represent" pathogens through symbolic encoding; rather, it enacts the self/non-self boundary through a process of **individuation by error**.

**Layer 1 (Monadic):** The developing immune system begins with an undifferentiated repertoire of T-cell receptors generated through random V(D)J recombination. This produces approximately $10^{15}$ possible receptor specificities—a vast **pre-individual field** of potential reactivity with no intrinsic distinction between "self" and "non-self."

**Layer 2 (Dyadic):** During thymic selection, immature T-cells encounter self-antigens. This **perturbation** triggers a symmetry-breaking: cells binding strongly to self are eliminated (negative selection), while those binding moderately are retained. The "self" is not discovered but **constituted** through this historical process of encounter (Pradeu, 2012). The immune system becomes an observer through the perturbations that force it to distinguish self from non-self.

**Layer 3 (Triadic):** Peripheral regulatory T-cells (Tregs) and cytokine networks constitute the **error-management system**. They modulate the intensity of immune responses, determining when low-level self-reactivity (cheap error) is tolerated for surveillance purposes versus when it is actively suppressed to prevent tissue damage (expensive error).

**The Optimality of Error:** A "perfect" immune system with zero error (complete self-tolerance) would eliminate anti-tumor immunity and tissue repair capabilities. Conversely, a system with excessive error (uncontrolled autoimmunity) destroys the host. Evolution plausibly selects not for zero error but for an **optimal error rate**—a specific regime of controlled deviation that maximizes long-term viability (Eberl, 2016).

This illustrates that immunological "knowledge" is not accurate representation of pathogens but **viable boundary maintenance** through strategic error management. The system's "intelligence" lies in its capacity to afford specific errors—maintaining autoreactivity as a cheap error to enable surveillance, while preventing expensive errors that would cause systemic collapse.

### 5.2 Adversarial Examples and the Infinite Regress of Tool-Making

Deep neural networks (DNNs) trained via supervised learning often exhibit pronounced brittleness: adversarial examples—imperceptible perturbations—can induce misclassification (Szegedy et al., 2014). A panda image with noise ($\|\delta\| < 0.01$) may be classified as "gibbon" with high confidence.

#### **Why DNNs Lack Robust Error Management**

**The Triadic Deficit:**
- **Layer 1 (Present):** Training data distribution $p(x, y)$
- **Layer 2 (Present):** Decision boundary learned via gradient descent  
- **Layer 3 (Absent):** No operational closure or error-management structure

On the present framework, standard supervised DNNs do not maintain operational closure: their objective functions, evaluation criteria, and deployment boundaries are externally specified. Consequently, they optimize representational error (e.g., cross-entropy loss) without an endogenous mechanism that differentiates affordable from catastrophic errors in relation to the system’s own viability.

**The Infinite Regress Problem:** This external specification generates an infinite regress: the DNN's "epistemic" norms are inherited from the designer’s boundary conditions.
- The loss function is designed by humans.
- The evaluation metric is chosen by humans.
- The target labels are provided by humans.

Accordingly, the DNN is best treated as a tool embedded in a wider socio-technical system: the relevant normativity is imposed by designers and users, not generated endogenously by self-maintenance constraints.

**The Brittleness Mechanism:**
Standard DNNs optimize for **representational accuracy**: minimizing prediction error relative to training labels. By Theorem 3.1 and Rate-Distortion theory, this requires compression of the input distribution into discrete class boundaries. However, unlike biological systems, DNNs lack:
1. **Metastability maintenance:** They optimize externally specified objectives (e.g., empirical risk) rather than maintaining the controlled metastability needed for adaptive bifurcation.
2. **Error-management infrastructure:** No mechanism distinguishes "cheap" errors (misclassification of functionally similar categories) from "expensive" errors (complete loss of classification boundary under perturbation).
3. **Active inference capability:** DNNs lack the active states $a$ that would allow them to seek confirmatory evidence or modify sensory sampling to maintain blanket integrity. In standard deployments, they remain reactive mappings without autonomous boundary regulation.

**The Adversarial Example as Expensive Error:**
Adversarial perturbations are **cheap errors** for the attacker (low magnitude) but **expensive errors** for the system (catastrophic boundary collapse). The DNN's decision boundary is **rigid**—optimized for average-case accuracy without maintaining the "controlled instability" necessary for robustness.

**Contrast with Biological Vision:**
The human visual system maintains **operational closure** through recurrent dynamics, saccadic sampling, and predictive filtering (Noë, 2004). An adversarial perturbation to a static retinal image might trigger a "prediction error," but the system responds by:
1. Invoking active sampling (changing gaze, focus)
2. Recruiting alternative sensory modalities (touch, proprioception)
3. Triggering explicit uncertainty (metacognitive awareness of anomaly)

The biological system treats the perturbation as a **bifurcation trigger** (cheap error) that reorganizes coupling dynamics, rather than a representational failure (expensive error). It maintains a triadic structure; the DNN is merely dyadic (input-output), lacking the mediating layer that would transform error into adaptation.

#### **Why AI "Hallucinations" Need Not Constitute Cognition (in This Sense): The Thermodynamic Cost Criterion**

Large language models (LLMs) generate "hallucinations"—confident assertions about non-existent entities, fabricated citations, or internally contradictory claims. On the present framework, such behavior highlights a limitation: in standard deployments, token-level inaccuracy is not endogenously coupled to the system's own viability dynamics, and so is insufficient, by itself, to ground intrinsic error management.

**The Cost-Free Error Problem:**
In standard deployments, LLMs do not **pay** for their errors in the sense relevant here: an incorrect output token typically carries no *endogenous* consequence for the system's continued operation (no hunger, injury, boundary dissolution, or loss of operational closure). Unlike biological systems—where errors can trigger metabolic cascades (stress hormones, immune responses, tissue damage)—LLM errors are largely **viability-neutral** for the model itself.

Of course, running an LLM consumes electricity and dissipates heat in the hardware substrate. The point is that this thermodynamic cost is not *internally coupled* to epistemic failure: the system can output "Paris is the capital of Germany" with essentially the same internal consequences as "Paris is the capital of France."

**Consequence:** Because errors carry no endogenous price, they do not function as internally costly signals. There is no internal gradient distinguishing cheap errors (tolerable deviations) from expensive errors (boundary threats). Without such a gradient, endogenous error **management** is hard to obtain; what remains is error **generation** (or externally imposed management by designers and evaluators).

**Intrinsic Normativity Requires Endogenous Coupling:**
A system exhibits intrinsic epistemic normativity only if epistemic failures are internally coupled to variables that matter for its own persistence (e.g., deviations that increase expected free energy and threaten operational closure). In standard LLM deployments, token-level inaccuracies are typically not endogenously coupled to the model’s continued viability; true and false outputs are therefore near-equivalent from the system’s internal point of view. This helps explain why LLMs can be shaped by external reward signals (RLHF) yet this does not thereby suffice to secure intrinsic normativity: the relevant gradients remain designer- and user-supplied. The LLM remains a tool of human individuation, not a cognitive agent in its own right.

**The Path to Artificial Cognition:**
If this analysis is correct, genuine artificial cognition would require systems that:
1. **Pay thermodynamic costs** for maintaining internal states (not just computational cycles, but genuine dissipative costs tied to system integrity)
2. **Internally discriminate** between cheap and expensive errors via feedback loops affecting system viability
3. **Develop intrinsic preferences** for low-cost coupling regimes ("truth") as a survival strategy, not an externally imposed reward

This is best read as a research program rather than a settled conclusion: it suggests that moving from powerful tools to genuine cognitive agents may require coupling error to self-maintenance constraints—potentially through forms of embodiment or other architectures in which the system's own viability depends on boundary management.

## 6. Anticipated Objections

### 6.1 The Scientific Realism Objection: Technicity and Robust Coupling

**Objection:** "Your framework cannot account for the manifest success of scientific theories in predicting novel phenomena and enabling technological intervention. If science does not track mind-independent reality, why does the Standard Model predict particle collisions with 10-decimal precision? Doesn't this require representational accuracy?"

**Response:** We do not deny scientific success; we offer an alternative explanation of why it can be stable and often highly accurate without presupposing a full-blooded mirroring relation. We draw on Simondon's notion of **technicity** (*technicité*): the way concretized technical objects stabilize repeatable couplings between human practices and the pre-individual field (Simondon, 1958/2017).

On this view, precision is achieved by constructing socio-technical triadic mediators—apparatuses, protocols, and institutions—that regulate perturbations. Experiments can therefore be understood not merely as "reading off" truth, but as establishing controlled regimes in which deviations are made tractable through material and social stabilization (vacuum systems, shielding, standardized units; Baird, 2004; Pickering, 1995).

**Idealization as Intentional Compression:** Weisberg's (2007) taxonomy of idealization fits this picture. Galilean, minimalist, and multiple-models idealizations each involve deliberate distortion: models simplify, isolate, or explore parameter spaces in order to secure robust performance under constraints. Our framework supplies a thermodynamic rationale: because finite systems are under pressure to compress, the key question is not whether to introduce distortion, but which distortions yield stable, low-cost coupling in a given regime.

**Why 10-decimal precision is possible:** The exceptional precision of quantum electrodynamics (QED) can be understood as a joint achievement of theory and technicity. Highly stabilized experimental conditions reduce uncontrolled perturbations and allow models to operate in regimes where effective distortion is very low. Achieving and maintaining those regimes requires substantial energetic and organizational investment (e.g., cooling, isolation, calibration), which effectively increases usable rate and reduces noise, thereby permitting lower-distortion compressions in the relevant domain.

**Historical change as boundary reorganization:** This reading also supports a Kuhnian picture of theory change as shifts between regimes of viable coupling. Newtonian mechanics maintained low-cost coupling in many macroscopic regimes; relativistic and quantum phenomena made that coupling increasingly expensive; alternative formalisms reorganized the boundary so that a wider class of perturbations could be handled more economically.

**Relation to structural realism:** Structural realism (Worrall, 1989; Ladyman, 1998) explains scientific success by holding that our theories capture structure. Our proposal can be situated as an operative variant: what is stabilized and exported across contexts is the structure of the world–apparatus coupling (the Layer 2–3 interface), i.e., the reliable patterns that emerge when triadic regulators constrain the field under specific energetic and institutional conditions. This resonates with Cartwright's (1983) emphasis on capacities and modeling practices: scientific "laws" can be read as characterizing robust regularities of stabilized couplings rather than describing the pre-individual field in abstraction from those couplings.

Accordingly, scientific success—up to and including extreme precision—can be acknowledged while treating representational accuracy as a special regime: one in which technicity makes distortion sufficiently small for correspondence talk to be pragmatically adequate.

### 6.2 The Swampman and Deception Objections

**Objection:** "Your account seems indifferent to the etiology of boundary maintenance. Consider Davidson's Swampman (Davidson, 1987): a being spontaneously created by lightning, molecule-for-molecule identical to you, but with no causal history. Does it have knowledge despite lacking historical coupling? Or consider a brain-in-a-vat fed simulated sensory inputs that maintain viability—does it know the 'virtual world' even though its beliefs have no causal connection to external reality?"

**Response:** The Swampman objection presupposes **internalism**—that mental states are individuated by intrinsic properties. Our framework rejects this.

**The Triadic Answer:**

In our Layer 3 (Triadic Mediation), knowledge requires **historical stabilization of coupling**. The Swampman possesses a static boundary configuration (molecular structure) but lacks the **process of individuation**—the historical perturbation-response sequences that established and maintain that boundary.

At the moment of creation, the Swampman's states would indeed mirror yours, but they would lack a viability condition for knowledge:
1. **No error-management history:** The boundary didn't emerge through compression of field dynamics; it was imposed exogenously.
2. **No perturbation sensitivity:** While molecularly identical, the Swampman has not undergone the selective pressures that calibrated its precision-weighting. It lacks the dynamic stability that comes from having survived past bifurcations.

Therefore, the Swampman lacks knowledge not because it fails to "correspond" to reality, but because its boundary is **not coupled to the field**—it is a static snapshot, not a viable cognitive system. Absent that individuation history, it would plausibly fail to maintain operational closure under perturbation, lacking the regulatory capacity to manage boundary permeability.

**The Brain-in-a-Vat:**

If the vat-system achieves **genuine operational closure**—if it autonomously regulates its boundary against "sensory" perturbations, maintaining low-cost coupling to the simulation—then it "knows" the virtual world in the same broad sense in which we know ours. The virtual/physical distinction is not decisive; what matters is whether the boundary manages perturbations viably.

However, if the vat-system is merely a passive receiver of inputs (like a moviegoer), with no active boundary regulation, it lacks knowledge. On our view, knowledge is not passive correspondence but **autonomous viability maintenance**.

### 6.3 The Problem of Communication

**Objection:** "If cognition is not representational, how is successful communication possible? When I say 'The cat is on the mat,' and you understand me, does this not require that we share representations of cats and mats?"

**Response:** Communication is not transmission of representations but **coordination of individuation practices** (Maturana & Varela, 1987; Brandom, 1994).

**The Mutual Structural Coupling Model:**

1. **Speaker:** Individuates a perturbation (afferent sensation of cat-on-mat) into a boundary (the utterance) that modifies the environment (sound waves).
2. **Hearer:** Receives perturbation (sound waves) as a deviation requiring individuation. The utterance triggers bifurcation, creating a localized boundary in the hearer's field.
3. **Coordination:** "Understanding" occurs not when hearer reproduces speaker's representation, but when the hearer's bifurcation (object-formation) coordinates with the speaker's in a way that maintains **mutual viability**—both can now act in coordinated fashion regarding the object.

**Inferentialist Complement:**
Robert Brandom's (1994) inferentialism provides useful vocabulary here: the meaning of "cat" is not its representational content but its **inferential role**—the relocations in the "game of giving and asking for reasons" that it permits. However, where Brandom remains tied to linguistic norms, we ground these norms in **thermodynamic viability**. A "successful" communication is one where the coordination established permits low-cost joint action; a "misunderstanding" is an expensive error requiring extensive renegotiation of boundaries.

**Example:** Two sailors using the term "reef." It matters not whether they share identical mental images (representations), but whether their individuation practices coordinate:
- When one says "reef ahead," both adjust course (coupled action).
- If the hearer individuates "reef" as "sandbar" while the speaker meant "coral formation," but both avoid collision, communication succeeded (coordinated viability maintenance).
- Failed communication occurs not when representations mismatch, but when boundaries fail to coordinate such that joint action becomes energetically expensive (collision occurs).

Language is a **stabilization technology** for error management—conventionalizing boundaries so that perturbations trigger similar bifurcations across speakers, reducing the energetic cost of coordination (Brandom, 1994; Noë, 2004).

## 7. Conclusion: Toward a Thermodynamic Conception of Mind

We began with a puzzle: how can error be both epistemically indispensable and apparently unavoidable? Representational approaches face a familiar difficulty here: if error is defined as deviation from correctness conditions that individuate content, then systematic, constraint-driven deviation is hard to interpret as anything other than malfunction.

We proposed a resolution by reversing the usual ontological priority. Rather than starting with subjects and objects, we began with the **field**—a metastable domain of potentials. **Individuation** (the emergence of subject-object distinctions) occurs through perturbation: symmetry-breaking bifurcations that establish boundaries (Markov blankets) between internal and external. We proved (Theorem 3.1) that finite coarse-graining leaves irreducible informational residue and, using rate-distortion theory, quantified the associated trade-off under finite rate and energetic constraints—supporting a reading of error as the structural residue of finite cognition rather than as a contingent defect.

**The Triadic Structure:**
1. **Monadic:** Pre-individual field (metastable potential)
2. **Dyadic:** Perturbation-triggered splitting (observer-observed emergence)
3. **Triadic:** Error management (regulation of boundary permeability)

Epistemic normativity is reconstructed not as truth-conduciveness but as **viability**—the capacity to maintain operational closure (autopoiesis) under thermodynamic constraints. A cognition is "good" if it achieves low-cost coupling to the field, maintaining perturbation sensitivity without collapse. A cognition is "bad" if it triggers expensive errors—dissipative cascades threatening boundary integrity.

**Key Implications:**

**For Epistemology:** The central question shifts from "Does it correspond to reality?" to "Can it survive perturbation?" Truth is reconceptualized as a specific regime of **robust coupling**—a boundary configuration that absorbs expected perturbations cheaply. Scientific progress can be characterized, on this view, as an **expansion of viability envelopes**—establishing boundaries that remain stable under increasingly comprehensive perturbation regimes (Cartwright, 1983; Thompson, 2007).

**For Cognitive Science:** We should treat the "brain as prediction machine" metaphor with caution where it implies representational mirroring. Instead, we should study **boundary engineering**—how systems regulate Markov blankets to maintain metastability (Ramstead et al., 2023). This helps explain why:
- Biological systems are robust (they maintain operational closure through autopoiesis)
- Standard deep learning systems are brittle in this respect (they lack autopoiesis and triadic mediation, and are better treated as tools within human individuation rather than as cognitive subjects)

**For Philosophy of Mind:** Our account suggests a reframing rather than a solution: if subjectivity is understood as the intrinsic perspective of a self-maintaining boundary, then many representation-centered puzzles can be reposed as questions about individuation, embodiment, and the thermodynamics of boundary maintenance. We do not claim to resolve the hard problem here; we indicate a research direction (Thompson, 2007; Deacon, 2011).

**Closing Summary:** Error is not an occasional failure but a structural consequence of finite individuation. The Compression Theorem (Theorem 3.1) shows that any finite coarse-graining leaves irreducible informational residue; rate-distortion theory shows that zero distortion requires unbounded rate/energy. Epistemic normativity thus concerns keeping errors cheap—maintaining viable coupling while expanding the perturbation regimes a system can withstand.

**Outlook:** Future work should (i) operationalize cheap vs expensive error in empirical settings, (ii) connect metastability to measurable control/complexity metrics, and (iii) clarify which forms of artificial systems can internalize error costs rather than outsourcing them to designers.
