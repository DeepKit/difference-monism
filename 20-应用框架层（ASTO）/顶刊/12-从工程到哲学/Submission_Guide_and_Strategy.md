# Submission Guide: Targeting *Philosophy & Technology*

**Target Journal**: *Philosophy & Technology* (Springer)
**Current Editor-in-Chief**: Check current listing (historically associated with Luciano Floridi's school of thought).
**Impact Factor**: High (Top tier in Philosophy/Tech intersection).

---

## 1. Pre-Submission Checklist (投稿前自查)

Before you upload, ensure your manuscript signals "Professional Scholar" + "Practicing Engineer":

*   [ ] **Word Count**: Aim for **7,000 - 9,000 words**. If currently shorter, expand Section 3 & 4 with more concrete engineering examples. If longer than 10k, trim the "Generalization" section.
*   [ ] **Abstract**: Must be **< 250 words**. It needs to be punchy. The "Engineering Turn" hook must appear in the first 3 sentences.
*   [ ] **Keywords**: Use standard taxonomy terms. Suggested: *AI Alignment, Epistemology of Simulation, Software Engineering, Structural Realism, Trust*.
*   [ ] **Anonymization**: Ensure the main PDF does not contain your name or affiliation (Double-blind review requirement).
*   [ ] **Formatting**: Springer uses a specific citation style (usually Chicago or APA). Use LaTeX or Word templates provided by Springer.

## 2. The Cover Letter Strategy (附信策略)

The Cover Letter is your sales pitch to the Editor. It will not be seen by reviewers, so you can be more direct about your background here.

**Key Narrative**: "I am an engineer who discovered a philosophical crisis in the trenches, not a philosopher speculating from an armchair."

### Draft Cover Letter

> Dear Editor,
>
> I am pleased to submit my manuscript, "**The Engineering Turn in Epistemology**," for consideration in *Philosophy & Technology*.
>
> While much of the current literature on AI Alignment focuses on theoretical ethics or high-level policy, this paper takes a different approach. Drawing on my background as a software architect leading the development of **Output-Driven Development (ODD)** frameworks, I argue that the velocity of Generative AI is forcing a fundamental epistemological shift in how we build and trust software.
>
> The paper extracts a novel **Triadic Ontology (ASTO)** directly from engineering mechanisms like Mutation Testing and Artifact Sealing. It argues that we are moving from an era of "Transparency" (understanding process) to an era of "Perturbation" (verifying boundaries).
>
> Given *Philosophy & Technology*'s commitment to investigating the "technological condition," I believe this analysis of the *ontology of the IDE* will resonate strongly with your readership. It bridges the gap between the philosophy of science (Hacking, Popper) and the hard constraints of modern CI/CD pipelines.
>
> I confirm that this work is original, has not been published elsewhere, and is not under consideration by another journal.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Your Affiliation/Role]

## 3. Reviewer Selection Strategy (推荐审稿人)

You will often be asked to suggest reviewers. This is strategic. You want people who understand **Technology** and **Philosophy**, not pure metaphysicians who will hate the engineering terms.

**Ideal Profiles (Look for people like these):**
1.  **Technical Philosophers**: Scholars who cite *Luciano Floridi*, *Peter-Paul Verbeek*, or *Don Ihde*. They understand "Technological Mediation."
2.  **Philosophy of Science**: Scholars working on *Models and Simulations* or *Experiments*. They will appreciate the "Mutation Testing" argument.
3.  **Critical AI Scholars**: People writing about *Algorithmic Governance*.

**Avoid**: Pure Continental Philosophers (too poetic) or Pure Analytic Logicians (too rigid).

## 4. Addressing "Reviewer 2" (应对刁钻批评)

Be prepared for these likely criticisms and have your defenses ready (pre-empt them in the paper if possible):

### Criticism A: "This is just engineering best practice, not philosophy."
*   **Defense**: Acknowledge it is engineering, but argue that **engineering *is* doing philosophy** via other means. Engineering mechanisms (like Sealing) are answers to philosophical questions (like "What is Being?"). You are *extracting* the philosophy, not inventing it.

### Criticism B: "You are anthropomorphizing the AI (e.g., 'Blue Team')."
*   **Defense**: Clarify that you are using functional definitions. When you say AI "decides," you mean it collapses a probability distribution, not that it has a soul.

### Criticism C: "This promotes a 'Black Box' society."
*   **Defense**: You are not *promoting* it; you are *diagnosing* it. The velocity of AI makes the Black Box inevitable. Your paper offers a way to *survive* the Black Box (via boundary control), rather than hopelessly demanding it become transparent.

## 5. Post-Submission Timeline (时间线)

1.  **Submission**: Day 0
2.  **Editor Check**: 1-2 Weeks. (If rejected here, it means the topic didn't fit. Re-submit to *Techné* or *AI & Society*).
3.  **Review**: 3-6 Months. (This is normal. Be patient).
4.  **Decision**:
    *   **Accept**: Rare on first try.
    *   **Minor Revisions**: Great! Fix typos and small clarity issues.
    *   **Major Revisions**: Most common for good papers. They want you to engage more with existing literature. **Do it.**
    *   **Reject**: Don't panic. Read the comments. If they say "not philosophical enough," add more citations to Heidegger/Simondon. If they say "too technical," simplify the engineering examples.

## 6. Backup Journals (备选方案)

If *Philosophy & Technology* says no, try these:

1.  ***AI & Society***: Very receptive to social/structural impacts of AI.
2.  ***Techné: Research in Philosophy and Technology***: The official journal of the Society for Philosophy and Technology. Very rigorous but welcomes practitioner views.
3.  ***Minds and Machines***: Focuses on AI and cognitive science. Good for the "Epistemology" angle.
