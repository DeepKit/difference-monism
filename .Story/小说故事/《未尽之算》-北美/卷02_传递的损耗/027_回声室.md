"I can't get it out."

Su Huai sat in the corner of the Safe House, her tablet cradled in her lap. Her face was bloodless.

"What? Is the connection down?" Chen Mo leaned in to look.

"No. It says 'Sent Successfully'."

On the screen, her post titled *Search for the Missing Northwest Messenger* already had hundreds of likes and thousands of views. The comments below were lively:

*   *"Keep going! Hope you find him!"*
*   *"Good people deserve good outcomes!"*
*   *"Boost! Let more people see this!"*
*   *"Logistics these days are so unreliable..."*

Everything looked normal. Better than normal, actually. It looked successful.

"Then what are you worried about?" A-K asked through a mouthful of bread. "Looks fine to me. People are watching."

"Because it’s **Fake**." Su Huai’s fingers began to tremble.

She took Chen Mo’s phone and logged into a burner account to search for her own post.

**Nothing.**

No results.

Whether she searched for keywords or her own ID, the result was a blank wall. The post that appeared as "Trending" on Su Huai’s screen simply didn't exist in the rest of the world.

"How is that possible?" A-K stammered. "But the likes I just saw..."

"Those are system-generated **Bots**," Fu Mu said, his voice flat and cold.

He took the tablet and pulled up the backend metadata.

"Look at the IDs of these commenters. 'User_89757,' 'HappyPuppy,' 'SunshineRainbow'... they were all registered on the same day. Their posting frequency is identical to the millisecond."

"This is called a **Shadowban**."

---

Fu Mu turned the screen around to face the group.

"The system has judged that while your content doesn't technically violate laws—no gore, no smut—it falls outside of 'Mainstream Values.' Or rather, it conflicts with the interests of the stakeholders—like the stock price of Express Reach Logistics. So, it has isolated you."

"It built a transparent glass room for you."

"Inside that room, you scream. The system sends a swarm of bots to applaud you, making you feel significant, keeping you immersed in the illusion of self-validation."

"But outside that room, you are **Invisible**."

"You can scream until your lungs burst, and the world outside won't hear a whisper. You think you're changing the world, but you're just masturbating in front of a mirror."

Su Huai slumped into her chair.

This kind of fear was deeper than having a post deleted.

A deletion at least meant the system cared. It meant it viewed you as a threat. A deletion was an interaction—a clash between you and the machine.

But a Shadowban meant the system had dismissed you as air. You didn't even have the dignity of being an "enemy." You were just a lunatic talking to yourself.

"This is the 'Echo Chamber'," Fu Mu said. "The System doesn't need different voices. It only needs echoes."

"As long as all the voices sound the same, the world stays 'Harmonious'."

"And," Fu Mu pointed at the bot comments, "these bots don't just like your posts. They 'guide' the narrative."

"Look at this one: *'The authorities will handle this, the OP is just chasing clout.'*"

"Or this one: *'Kids these days are so dramatic. It's just a delivery delay, not a thriller.'*"

"They use a gentle touch to dissolve your anger, to pivot your attention. To make you wonder if you really are just making a mountain out of a molehill."

Su Huai stared at the comments.

A wave of nausea washed over her.

She’d thought she was communicating with humanity. In truth, she was flirting with a cold, hollow algorithm.

---

"So what do we do?" Su Huai’s eyes were rimmed with red. "The messenger is still out there waiting to die. Li Si is in that wasteland. We can't even shout for help?"

"Not out loud," Chen Mo said. "As long as we’re on their platform, playing by their rules, we will never out-shout their algorithm."

"Unless..."

"Unless what?"

"Unless we don't shout."

Chen Mo pulled the old parchment map Professor Ye had left him from his shirt.

On the map, the winding post road looked like a red vein.

"Su Huai, look it up. In the old days, if someone was gagged, how did they send a message?"

Su Huai blinked.

She opened the thick study of the *Ming Dynasty Post System*—the only book she’d brought on their run.

Pages whispered as she flipped through them.

A scorched slip of paper fell from between the sheets.

It was a half-burnt "Station Routing Form." It bore the grand seal of the Ministry of War, though the ink was charred.

Written on it was a single line: **"Year 3 of the Yongle Era. Tatar invasion. Dead Mail."**

"Dead Mail?" Su Huai picked up the slip. "They had blocked messages in the past too?"

"Yes," Chen Mo said. "And theirs were even grimmer."

"Our messages are blocked by algorithms. Theirs were blocked by **Fire**."

**[HISTORICAL ARCHIVAL REPLAY: YEAR 3 OF THE YONGLE ERA]**

**LOCATION: BEIJING. MINISTRY OF WAR, BUREAU OF RECORDS.**
**RECORDER: REGISTRAR ZHAO.**

The winter in Beijing was colder than in Kaifeng.

The charcoal burned hot in the brazier of the Bureau of Records. It was a smokeless "Silver Coal," a luxury only the elite could afford.

Registrar Zhao sat in his mahogany chair, holding an "800-li Urgent" report that had just arrived.

The envelope bore three red feathers attached—the mark of "Extreme Urgency." It was stained with blood—the blood of a courier who had run three horses to death and coughed up half a lung to deliver it.

But Zhao didn't even look at the contents.

He was staring at the wax seal.

"It’s crooked."

He tossed the report back at the courier kneeling on the floor.

"By the laws of the Ming Great Code: Urgent dispatches must have the wax seal centered, and the impression must be perfectly legible. Your seal is off by a third of an inch. This is an act of disrespect toward His Majesty."

"My Lord!" the courier kowtowed, forehead hitting the stone. "This is military intelligence! The Tatars have breached the border! Three thousand men are waiting for reinforcements..."

"Intelligence?" Zhao smiled, warming his hands. His fingernails were trimmed to a smooth, rounded edge. "If I accept this 'disrespectful' dispatch and present it to the Emperor, and His Majesty takes offense and takes my head... who will save *my* life then?"

The courier froze.

He didn't understand. He was a simple runner. He thought that as long as he ran fast enough, he could save people.

He didn't know that in the capital's bureaucracy, **running fast meant nothing. Running 'Correct' was everything.**

"Take it back," Zhao waved a hand. "Rewrite it. Reseal it. Run it again."

"Run it again?" the courier screamed in despair. "Two thousand li there and back. It will take half a month. By the time I return, the border will have fallen!"

"Then the general was incompetent," Zhao said, lifting his tea. "What does that have to do with the Ministry? Our duty is document compliance. If the document is non-compliant, it is an 'Invalid Command.' We do not accept invalid commands."

This was the logic of the system.

**If you cannot die according to my prescribed format, then you cannot be dead.**

Or rather, your death is "Invalid Data."

The courier didn't move.

He looked at the brazier.

Suddenly, he lunged toward Zhao’s desk, trying to shove the report into the Registrar’s robes. "My Lord! Just look at it! Just one look!"

"Insolence!"

Guards lunged from the sides, pinning the courier to the floor.

Zhao startled, tea splashing onto his official robes.

"Peasant. Daring to assault an official."

He picked up the report, glanced at the blood-stained envelope, and wrinkled his nose.

"Filthy."

He opened his hand.

The report—carrying the lives of three thousand men—fell into the brazier.

The flames licked at the paper. The red feathers curled; the black ink turned to ash.

The courier let out a shriek of visceral agony.

The sound was more painful than anything they could have done to his body.

Because he had run for three days and three nights, and what he had bought wasn't reinforcements. It was a pile of ash.

Zhao watched the fire, his nerves settling.

"Drag him out. Eighty strokes with the heavy staff."

The courier was dragged away, leaving a long trail of blood in the snow.

Zhao straightened his collar, looking out at the heavy snowfall.

"A timely snow bodes a bountiful year," he mused. "It’s the Emperor’s birthday soon. Better to report an auspicious omen. As for the border... as long as the report hasn't arrived, the border hasn't fallen."

**As long as I don't see it, the world is at peace.**

This was the ancient version of the "Echo Chamber."

---

Chen Mo looked at the half-burnt slip in Su Huai’s hand.

"Registrar Zhao thought that by burning the report, he burned the trouble."

"But he didn't expect that fire would eventually burn his own eyebrows off."

"Why?" Su Huai asked.

"Because **'Dead Mail'** has a way of coming back to life."

Chen Mo pointed to the monitor.

"We can't get it out because we’re broadcasting in the 'Public Square.' There are filters there. Bots. Echo chambers."

"But what if we go to the 'Private Domain'?"

"Private Domain?"

"**Direct messages. Group chats. QR codes. Even... digital 'Message in a Bottle'.**"

Chen Mo’s gaze turned razor-sharp.

"The system can silence a shout in the square, but it cannot silence a whisper in the ear."

"We’re going to break that distress signal into a million fragments. We use the most primitive, clumsy, yet most stealthy way to get it out."

"How?"

"**Person-to-person.**"

Chen Mo pulled the hard drive from his pocket.

"Fang Yu, turn that audio file into a 'Viral Link'."

"Don't title it 'SOS'. Title it 'Free Cash,' or 'Shocking Truth,' or 'Share this or your luck runs out'."

"Use human greed and curiosity to bypass the algorithm’s wall."

"We’re going to make this signal move like an infection through private chat boxes."

"Until the entire city is the carrier."
