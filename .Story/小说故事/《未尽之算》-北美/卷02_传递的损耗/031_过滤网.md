> **《未尽之算》·第31集·过滤网**

The Inner Hall of the Bureau of Records, Ministry of War.

Registrar Zhao did not burn the letter immediately.

He placed the black box—the one Li Si had patched with his own blood—on his desk, admiring it as if it were a fine piece of art.

"Li Si," he said, his long fingers drumming rhythmically against the red wax seal. "You think you are saving the country?"

"No. You are causing the state unnecessary stress."

He opened the box.

Inside was a document saturated with sweat and dried salt. The handwriting was jagged, the ink bled into the paper in a way that felt ferocious.

It was the blood-written report of Juyong Pass Commander Wang Deren:

**"Fifty thousand Tatar horsemen at the border. Rations within the pass only sufficient for three days. Send reinforcements immediately, or the pass falls and all within perish. Your servant, Wang Deren, submits this in blood."**

Zhao sighed.

Fifty thousand riders. Three days of rations.

This was serious—truly. In ordinary times, such a letter would shake the capital to its foundations, sending the Six Ministries into a frenzied panic.

But what day was it today?

Today was the Emperor's Birthday Festival. His Majesty was hosting a banquet for the officials—watching plays, enjoying the fireworks.

If this letter were delivered now, it would be like tossing a dead rat into a pot of fine soup.

The Emperor would be unhappy. The Grand Secretary would be unhappy. The Minister of War would be unhappy. Every official in the court would be unhappy.

And if everyone else is unhappy, I won’t be happy either.

Zhao stood and walked to the window.

Outside, fireworks bloomed, illuminating the night sky over Beijing. The multicolored flashes reflected off his face, making his expression shift in the flickering light.

"A golden age," he mused.

In this golden age, the distant clash of swords at the border was nothing but a faint buzzing of mosquitoes—utterly insignificant.

The System’s primary directive is **Stability**.

Any information that threatens stability is **Noise**.

My duty is to filter that noise.

I’m not "suppressing" a report. I’m performing "Information De-noising."

I am protecting the Emperor’s mood, the peace of the capital, and the illusion of this prosperous age.

As for Juyong Pass...

Zhao turned and walked toward the corner.

There sat a massive mahogany chest bound with a yellow seal: **"PENDING."**

He opened the chest.

It was already filled with boxes and documents of all kinds.

Some were from Liaodong, some from Datong, some from Xuanfu. Some read "Famine," some read "Plague," some read "Mutiny."

They had all been "Refrigerated."

Inside this chest, time stood still. Those cries for help, those screams of agony, those roars of fury—they were all sealed within this dark, silent volume.

Zhao tossed Wang Deren’s blood-report into the box.

"If the pass falls in three days, it is the will of Heaven."

He closed the lid and snapped the copper lock shut.

"If it doesn't fall, then I have saved the Emperor the expense of a military mobilization."

"Doing nothing is the highest form of politics."

He sat back in his chair and took a sip of his tea, which had long since gone cold.

The tea was cold, but his heart felt warm.

For he believed he had done the right thing. He had sacrificed the Truth for the Sake of the Big Picture.

This was the logic of the **Filter Net**.

It filters out more than just impurities; it filters out untimeliness and inconvenient suffering.

---

The Modern Day. The Archives of the Bureau of Social Security.

"Found it."

Chen Mo stared at the monitor and let out a long breath.

After three hours of brute-force hacking, he’d finally breached an outer server belonging to "Vision PR." He hadn't reached the core database, but he’d found the "Digital Corpse" of the journalist, Wang Wu.

"What is this?" Su Huai leaned in.

The screen displayed a complex flowchart.

**[INTERNAL PUBLIC OPINION RESPONSE FLOWCHART]**

1.  **Keyword Scraping**: The system automatically scans the network for "Sensitive Keywords" (e.g., Chemical Plant, Pollution, SOS).
2.  **Risk Assessment**: Information is scored based on the sender’s reach (followers), the "Heat" of the post (shares), and the content’s volatility (images/videos included).
3.  **Automated Response**:
    *   *Low Risk*: Throttling, folding, Shadowbanning.
    *   *Mid Risk*: Post deletion, account lockout.
    *   *High Risk*: **MANUAL INTERVENTION**.

Wang Wu’s distress audio had been tagged "High Risk."

"What does 'Manual Intervention' mean?" Su Huai asked.

"It means physical elimination."

Chen Mo opened a folder titled "Recycle Bin."

Inside were thousands of files. Recordings, videos, documents.

Each one was a truth that had been strangled by the system.

Chen Mo clicked on one of the videos.

In the video, Wang Wu was hiding in a derelict factory, speaking directly into the camera. His face was covered in blood, and he held a chemical laboratory report.

**"They’re filtering... everything... every negative word is being redirected..."**

**"It’s a massive... Filter Net..."**

**"They don't just delete the posts. They rewrite the data. They turn 'Toxic Pollution' into 'Grade A Air Quality.' They turn 'Cancer Rates' into 'Average Life Expectancy Increase'."**

**"They don't want to solve the problem. They want to solve the person pointing at the problem."**

At the end of the video, a frantic hammering came from the door.

Then, a single gunshot.

The screen went black.

Chen Mo removed his headphones.

His hands were shaking.

"This isn't just a PR firm," he said. "This is a **Clean-up Company**."

"Vision PR is just the storefront. Behind them is a much larger interest group."

"That plant, that pollution, that buried truth... it touches too much money. Too many people."

"So, they have to build the Filter Net."

"This net doesn't just filter data. It filters the human conscience."

Su Huai stared at the folder.

"These files..." she asked. "Can we send them out?"

"We can't."

Chen Mo shook his head.

"We opened a hole in the server, but it’s one-way. We can see, but we can't take. If we attempt a download or a transfer, the system triggers an alarm and executes a self-destruct sequence."

"Then what?"

"We go around it."

Chen Mo pointed to the "Manual Intervention" node on the chart.

"The system can filter data, but it can't filter the **Human**."

"The auditor. The person who actually clicks the 'Delete' button. He saw the truth."

"He’s a person. He has a conscience."

"We find him."

Chen Mo pulled up the local "Heat Map."

Inside the Vision PR building, hundreds of light-points were flickering. Those were the auditors on the clock.

"We strike the heart," Chen Mo said.

He opened the "SMS Bomber."

"This time, we don't send ads. We don't send SOS signals."

"We send **Interrogations**."

---

A few minutes later.

Inside the Great Hall of Auditors at Vision PR.

A young man sat at his terminal, mechanically clicking his mouse.

*Delete.*
*Delete.*
*Delete.*

His eyes were glazed. He didn't see the blood or the tears on the screen; he only saw tasks that needed to be finalized.

Suddenly, his phone vibrated.

**[HOW MANY LIVES DID YOU DELETE TODAY?]**

The auditor froze.

He thought it was a prank. He moved to delete it, but another message appeared instantly.

**[WANG WU’S FINAL WORDS. DID YOU HEAR THEM BEFORE YOU STRANGLED THE AUDIO?]**

His hand shook.

He *had* heard them.

As a "High Risk" auditor, he was required to verify the content of every file. He’d heard the gunshot. He’d seen the bloodied face.

But he had deleted it anyway.

Because it was his job. Because those were the rules. Because he didn't want to lose his salary.

**[THE BLOOD OF OTHERS IS ON YOUR DINNER PLATE.]**

The third message.

The auditor slammed his phone face-down on the desk. His breathing became erratic.

He looked around.

His colleagues were all staring at their own phones. Their expressions mirrored his own: terror, uncertainty, guilt.

A heavy, unnatural silence filled the hall.

It was the sound of consciences awakening.

Zhao’s chest locked away the reports, but it couldn't lock the human heart.
Vision’s firewall blocked the data, but it didn't block the interrogation.

The Filter Net had a leak.
