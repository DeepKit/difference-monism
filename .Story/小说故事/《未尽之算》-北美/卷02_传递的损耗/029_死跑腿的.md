# 第29集：死跑腿的

> **《未尽之算》·第29集·死跑腿的**
> **本卷进度：5/24 | Li Si's Stamina: 30% | Time Remaining: 1 Hour**
> *[SYSTEM_LOG]: NODE: JIMING POST STATION. THROUGHPUT: SATURATED. BUFFERING...*

---

The wind cut like a blade across his face.

Li Si lay flat against the horse’s back, his body pressing into the coarse mane. White frost coated his eyebrows and lashes. The steam from his breath was snatched away by the northern gale before it could even dissipate.

"Yah!"

He snapped the whip, bringing it down hard on the horse’s rump.

The chestnut post-horse let out a shrill whinny, its hooves thunderous against the frozen road of the ancient path.

This was the pass outside Juyong. On either side were rolling, desolate mountains buried under heavy snow. No trees, no birds, not even a ghost in sight.

Only the wind.

The howling wind sounded like the collective wailing of a thousand restless souls.

Li Si didn't feel the cold.

Because a fire was burning inside him. It was a mixture of terror and fury.

In his shirt, he carried the blood-stained strip of cloth—the legacy of the courier who had died at the gates.

**"SEND REINFORCEMENTS IMMEDIATELY."**

Those words burned against his chest like a branding iron.

He had to get this message to the Ministry of War. But he had no formal documents, no permits, not even the black box—it had been turned to ash.

He had only one identity: **Post Messenger of the Express Courier Station.**

In the Great Ming Code, a messenger wasn't called a person. He was called a "Walking Transmission."

Their lives were measured in miles.

Run three hundred li in a day, and you're rewarded with three cents of silver. Late by fifteen minutes? Eighty strokes of the staff. Late by thirty? Two years of hard labor. Late by half a day? Execution.

This was a road with no return. Once you stepped onto it, you either ran yourself to death or died on the execution ground.

---

"Whoa—"

The flicker of a station lamp appeared ahead.

**Jiming Post.**

Li Si yanked the reins. Before the horse had even fully stopped, he rolled off its back, crashing into the muddy slush.

"Change horse!"

He scrambled up, ignoring the jagged pain in his knees, and screamed at the station gates.

"Fast! Change my horse!"

The gate remained shut.

A long moment passed before an old stationmaster, draped in a padded cotton robe, hobbled out. He held a lantern, his eyes heavy with sleep as he squinted at Li Si.

"What’s all the shouting? Is there a funeral?"

"Military business!" Li Si flashed his waist-tag. "800-li Express! Change my horse, now!"

The stationmaster glanced at the tag, then at the sundial.

"What’s the rush? There’s still an hour before the gates lock."

He let out a slow, lazy yawn and gestured toward the trough.

"Horses are over there. Take your pick. But I’ll tell you now—the snow’s heavy today, and the officers took all the good mounts. What’s left are the old, the weak, and the broken."

Li Si rushed to the trough.

He was right.

There were only three horses. One was blind in one eye, one was skin and bones, and the last... had a visible limp.

"How am I supposed to ride these?" Li Si’s eyes turned red. "Can these things run three hundred li?"

"Whether a horse can run is the horse's business," the stationmaster sneered, revealing a row of yellow, tobacco-stained teeth. "Whether you ride it or not is yours."

"Li Si, your mentor was always too much in a hurry. And what happened? He died in the capital."

"Take some advice from your uncle: A dead horse belongs to the state, but a dead man belongs to himself. Who are you trying to impress with all this effort?"

Li Si froze.

He looked at the numbed, indifferent face of the old man.

In this system, if you were too serious, you were an anomaly. If you wanted to save people, you were a fool.

The smart ones had learned how to "muddle through."

The horse can't run fast? That’s the horse’s problem. The message didn't arrive? That’s the will of Heaven.

As long as *I* am not late, as long as I can shift the blame to the horse, to the sky, to the road, then I am not at fault.

"I won't muddle through," Li Si gritted his teeth.

He untied the blind horse. It was one-eyed, but the frame looked sturdy enough.

"He might be blind, but he still has legs."

Li Si vaulted into the saddle.

The skin on his inner thighs had long since been rubbed raw; blood had fused his trousers to his flesh. Every movement felt like skin being peeled from his body.

But he didn't make a sound.

He just squeezed the horse’s flanks tight.

"Yah!"

The blind horse lunged into the storm.

---

The stationmaster watched Li Si vanish into the white. He spat on the ground.

"Fool."

He turned and went inside to sleep.

Li Si couldn't hear the mockery.

He heard only the wind.

This road was the Great Ming’s artery. And he was a single red blood cell within it.

The non-existent box on his back (containing only a scrap of cloth) was the nation’s "Oxygen."

If he stopped, the artery would clog. If the artery clogged, the nation would sicken.

This was the logic his mentor had taught him.

His mentor had been an old messenger. He’d run his entire life, only to die at the gates of the capital. When they found him, his hand was still clutching the message he hadn't quite delivered.

*"We might be low-class, but we’re essential,"* his mentor used to say. *"Whether the Emperor sleeps soundly depends entirely on how fast we run."*

Li Si believed it.

So he ran until his legs broke and his horses died.

But what he didn't know was that at the end of the artery—in the capital—there was a man named Registrar Zhao waiting with a pair of scissors to cut the thread.

He was bleeding for the very man who wanted to sever the connection.

---

The storm intensified.

Visibility dropped to a few feet.

Li Si felt his hands and feet turn to lead. He relied on pure instinct to grip the reins.

Ahead lay a steep downhill slope.

The surface was coated in a thin layer of black ice, hidden under fresh snow. The most dangerous condition possible.

*"Slow... slow down..."* Li Si whispered to himself.

But he couldn't slow down.

Because time was the only currency that mattered.

Suddenly, a hoof slipped.

Black ice.

The blind horse couldn't see the patch, and the slip sent it into a total loss of balance.

*NEEEIGH—*

The horse shrieked as its front legs buckled. The momentum sent Li Si flying like a sack of grain.

*CRACK.*

Li Si hit the ground hard.

He tumbled across the snow until he slammed into a jagged rock.

Agony.

His left arm was snapped. Two ribs were broken.

But he didn't check his bones. His first instinct was to reach for his chest.

The cloth was still there.

He let out a shallow breath of relief.

But then he noticed something far worse.

Hanging from his belt was the spare, empty box he carried to look like an official courier. The impact had snapped the lid open.

The wax seal... was cracked.

Li Si’s face turned even whiter than the snow.

Under the Ming Code, a broken wax seal was equivalent to "Unauthorized Opening of Government Dispatches."

A capital offense.

Even if there was no letter inside, a broken seal meant death.

---

Snowflakes landed on Li Si’s face.

He lay on the ground, staring at the grey, oppressive sky.

The horse couldn't get up. Its legs were shattered, and it was letting out a series of low, wet moans.

The man was broken. The horse was broken. The seal was broken.

Was this the end?

Was this the reward for a man who ran his heart out?

Li Si wanted to laugh.

He thought of the two acres of land back home. He thought of Xiao Hua in the next village—she was still waiting for him to save enough silver to marry her.

"I can't die," Li Si muttered.

"I can't die here."

Gritting his teeth, he used his one good hand to push himself up from the slush.

He stared at the cracked wax seal.

The red wax looked like a wound slashed through the imperial dragon emblem.

If he presented this, it would be his death warrant.

"I have to fix it," Li Si thought.

But he had no fire, no wax, no seal.

How?

His gaze fell on his hand.

The fall had shredded his knuckles against the rocks. Blood was oozing from the cuts.

Bright, vivid red. Dripping onto the white snow.

Blood...

It was red.

Just as red as the wax.

A mad thought flashed through his mind.

His mentor had once said: *"Rules are dead, people are alive. To survive the courier service, you have to learn how to turn a dead rule into a living path."*

Li Si hadn't understood then. He understood now.

The system demanded "Integrity."

As long as it *looked* whole, it *was* whole.

The system didn't care what you used for the patch. It only cared if the red circle was a circle.

Li Si pulled a small knife from his boot.

A dull blade used for cutting hay.

Gritting his teeth, he took the knife to his bleeding finger and carved a deep, fresh gash.

Blood surged.

Thick, warm, and dark.

He pressed his finger into the crack of the wax seal.
