Li Si didn't die.

He collapsed at the gates of the capital and was saved by the city guards.

The letter—his true burden—had been used as kindling to light the signal fires by his rescuers. Ironically, while the message itself was consumed by the flames, the smoke alerted the capital. The Tatars, seeing the signal and fearing a massive ambush, retreated.

Juyong Pass was saved.

Registrar Zhao, credited for "Uncanny Foresight and Prompt Warning" (however accidental), was lauded by the Emperor and promoted.

And Li Si?

Because he had "Rendered Merit through Delivery" (even if he delivered a cartload of stones, it successfully confused the enemy), he was appointed as the **Postmaster of Jiming Station**.

---

Twenty years later.

Li Si was an old man. He sat on the threshold of the post station, a whip in his hand, watching the new generation of young couriers.

A young private was preparing to depart, but he stopped, terrified. "Chief, the wax seal on this dispatch... it’s cracked."

The boy was close to tears. He knew the penalty.

Li Si stood up and walked over.

He didn't scream. He didn't curse.

He reached into his boot, pulled out a small, sharp knife, and made a practiced slice across his own thumb.

With a master’s touch, he let the warm blood drip onto the dry wax, smoothing it over with his calloused hand, patching the broken stamp.

"Did you learn?" Li Si asked, his voice rasping like dry sand.

The private nodded, trembling.

"If you learned it, then get moving. One minute late, eighty lashes."

Li Si watched the boy gallop away into the dust.

He had finally lived long enough to become the man who once told him: *"A dead horse is the state's problem; a dead courier is his own."*

**The hero became the dragon?**
No. It was simpler than that. **The victim had become the enforcer.**

This was the ultimate power of the System. It didn't just filter the signals; it grew into the very marrow of the messengers.
