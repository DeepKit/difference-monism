# 第26集：优先级的暴政

> **《未尽之算》·第26集·优先级的暴政**
> **本卷进度：26/48 | 网络拥堵率：99% | 系统倒计时：983集**
> *[SYSTEM_LOG]: GLOBAL BROADCAST INITIATED. PRIORITY: SSS. CHANNEL OCCUPANCY: 82%. ALL LOWER-TIER SERVICES SUSPENDED.*

---

3:00 AM.

The city should have been asleep. But tonight, the city was wide awake with insomnia.

Chen Mo was lying on a cot in the basement, just beginning to close his eyes when the phone on his pillow began to vibrate with violent, frantic intensity.

*BZZZZT—BZZZZT—BZZZZT—*

Suddenly, the dim motion-sensor light above flared to its maximum brightness, letting out a sharp, electrical whine.

The air vent in the basement—the only link to the outside world—was flooded with a sudden, rhythmic strobing light of deep crimson.

Alarms wailed across the entire city.

*WAAAAA—WAAAAA—*

It didn't sound like an air-raid siren. It sounded like an electronically synthesized beast letting out a mechanical roar.

"Air raid?" Su Huai bolted upright, grabbing her jacket and preparing to dive under the desk.

"No. Not an air raid."

Chen Mo sat up and grabbed his phone.

The screen didn't show an air-raid warning, nor an earthquake alert.

It showed a single photograph.

A cat.

A pure white, fluffy Persian cat with a gaze that looked down upon the human race with profound indifference. Around its neck was a pink diamond collar that sparkled enough to blind the viewer.

Beneath the photo, bold black text occupied the entire screen:

**[CITYWIDE URGENT SEARCH: "MIMI," THE PET OF BILLIONAIRE’S DAUGHTER, IS MISSING. REWARD: $5 MILLION.]**

**[STATUS: EMERGENCY (SSS-CLASS).]**

**[COVERAGE: ALL NETWORKED DEVICES.]**

---

Chen Mo walked out of the basement to the main lobby of the Bureau.

He was stunned by the sight.

The city had been conquered by a cat.

The display screens on street lamps, the massive LED billboards on mall facades, the digital signs on taxi roofs—even the tiny display on A-K’s cleaning machine—were all flashing in unison.

The cat was everywhere.

Its face had been magnified ten thousand times, appearing like a massive, floating deity hovering over the city, peering down at the mortal ants below.

The red glare turned the night into a garish day. The sheer intensity of the light made it impossible to look directly at the skyline.

"Five million dollars..." A-K stood beside his sweeper, staring at the string of zeros on the screen, swallowing hard. "I could sweep for ten lifetimes and not make that much."

"The cat is worth more than I am," Fang Yu said, adjusting his glasses. "The collar is a Tiffany custom. Twenty million."

Chen Mo didn't speak. He simply felt cold.

A cold that reached into his marrow.

He took out his chipped abacus and synced it with the audio file he’d scavenged from the trash bin.

**"Help... help... me..."**

The plea from the dying man was only 2KB.

It had crawled through the network for three days and hadn't been able to make it past a single post-station transmitter. It had been treated like garbage—discarded, ignored, filtered.

But the data for this cat—high-definition video, holographic projections, real-time location tracking—exceeded 500MB.

Yet, in three seconds, it had reached the retinas of twenty million people.

To ensure the delivery of this message, the system had forcibly severed all "Lower-Priority" data transmissions.

Delivery orders were canned. Uber navigations were cut. Even the emergency dispatch system for ambulances had been throttled.

Why?

Because the cat had been tagged "SSS-Class."

And the dying man was "Unlabeled."

In the System's algorithm, the transmission speed for SSS-Class data is the speed of light. Unlabeled data? Get in line. Wait for your next life.

---

"Fu Mu," Chen Mo called, his voice tight. "Calculate something for me."

"What?" Fu Mu sounded exhausted. He’d clearly been woken by the cat’s electronic meowing.

"Calculate the bandwidth this cat is consuming."

The sound of keyboard clacking came from the other side.

"Based on the citywide scale... it’s occupying roughly 82% of the backbone’s capacity. It’s a forced preemption."

"The network is in a state of extreme congestion. Latency for ordinary users has surpassed ten minutes. Video calls are failing globally across the city."

"Meaning," Chen Mo looked up at the ginormous cat, "because of this pet, thousands of 'Help Me' messages are likely stuck in the pipes?"

"Theoretically, yes," Fu Mu’s voice was cold. "This is the end of 'Net Neutrality.' The road is public, but the right-of-way has been privatized."

"We used to think the Internet was equal. Now we know it has a class system."

"If you can pay enough, you can buy the whole road and force the ambulances to pull over for you."

"In fact, you can make an entire city stop just to look at your cat."

Chen Mo hung up.

He checked his phone.

The "Express Reach Logistics" group—the救援 action that had been bubbling with activity—had suddenly gone silent.

The blue dots representing the drivers were frozen.

"What’s happening?" Chen Mo asked.

"Network’s out," Fang Yu checked his gear. "Not a physical outage. It’s channel preemption. The cat’s data stream is so massive it’s washed out our rescue signal."

"The drivers can't receive coordinates anymore."

Chen Mo clenched his fist.

Li Si was still out there in the No-Man’s Land. Every second was the difference between life and death.

But now, his life was being blocked by a cat.

"Clear the way," Chen Mo growled.

"What?"

"Get this cat out of my sight."

"How?" Fang Yu gave a bitter laugh. "This is a SSS-Class broadcast from the Kernel. Unless the owner retracts the reward, no one can shut it down."

"Then we take the back roads."

Chen Mo tucked the abacus into his shirt, his gaze turning as fierce as a wolf’s.

"If the main road is blocked by a cat, we use the sewers."

"We’re going to the Dark Net."

---

The Safe House.

Fang Yu activated a black server—his own custom build, disconnected from the public grid and linked only via a private satellite uplink.

"Welcome to the Underworld," Fang Yu hit enter.

The screen turned a bottomless black. Emerald code flickered like ghost-lights in the void.

This was the Dark Net. The reverse side of the internet.

There were no laws here, no morality—only the raw, naked trade of currency for effect.

Drugs, weapons, forged passports, hitmen... as long as you had the Bitcoin, anything was available.

"What are we buying?" A-K asked, wide-eyed.

"A path," Chen Mo said.

He typed a line into the search bar: **`Traffic_Hijack`**.

Dozens of listings appeared.

*   **Seller A**: DDoS services. 1 BTC per hour.
*   **Seller B**: Botnet control. 100k+ nodes.
*   **Seller C**: Carrier backdoors. Temporary signal hijacking.

"That one," Chen Mo pointed to Seller C.

"You’re going to hijack a carrier station?" Fang Yu gasped. "That’s a felony! Homeland Security will be all over us!"

"I don't care."

Chen Mo contacted the seller.

**CHEN MO**: I want control of the towers around Jiayu Pass. Ten minutes.
**SELLER C**: Not for sale. That’s a military zone.
**CHEN MO**: Double the price.
**SELLER C**: It’s not about the money. That’s a Red Line. Touch it and you disappear.

Chen Mo went silent.

Even the outlaws of the Dark Net wouldn't touch that line.

Was there really no way?

Suddenly, a new message popped up.

**SELLER C**: But, if you just want to deliver a message, I have a dirty way.
**CHEN MO**: Explain.
**SELLER C**: You can rent my 'SMS Bomber.'
**CHEN MO**: A bomber?
**SELLER C**: Yeah. I have a quota of one million phantom-station spam messages. Usually used for gambling ads. The priority is low, but the volume is massive.
**SELLER C**: If you send enough of them, one of them will find a crack in that cat’s broadcast.

Chen Mo froze.

Using spam to fight a SSS-Class broadcast?

Using quantity to fight quality?

It was like using a billion ants to topple an elephant.

"Will it work?" Su Huai asked.

"Yes," Chen Mo’s eyes flared. "It’s the only way."

"The system will filter 99% of the 'junk,' but there’s always a leak in the pipe."

"If we flood it, the coordinates will get through."

Chen Mo turned to Fang Yu.

"Fang Yu, dismantle the audio file. Chop it into a million fragments."

"Disguise every single one as a piece of spam."

"Titles like: **'EASY MONEY,' 'YOU WON THE LOTTERY,' 'FREE EGGS AT THE SUPERMARKET.'**"

Fang Yu’s jaw dropped. "Are you serious?"

"Deadly serious."

Chen Mo stared at the arrogant cat on the screen.

"Since this cat likes to hog the highway, we’ll crawl through the storm drains."

"We’re going to drown this high-class cat in a sea of garbage."

---

Ten minutes later.

Phones across the city began to vibrate again.

This time, it wasn't a unified alarm. It was a cacophony of individual pings.

*Ping. Ping. Ping.*

People irritably reached for their devices.

*More spam?*

"CONGRATULATIONS! Your number has been selected for the grand prize..."
"MGM CASINO NOW ONLINE..."
"LONELY WIDOWS IN YOUR AREA..."

Common reactions were to curse and hit delete.

But just as fingers reached for the button, some began to notice something strange.

Beneath the tawdry marketing text, there was a tiny line of scrambled code.

If you copied the code and used a basic translator—like the auto-translate feature in WeChat—a single line emerged:

**[SOS. 300KM OUTSIDE JIAYU PASS. LI SI.]**

This wasn't an ad.

This was a cry for help buried in the trash.

It was humble, it was filthy, and it moved like a rat through the gutters.

But it was alive.

It crawled beneath the paws of the billionaire’s cat and bit into the retinas of the common people.

The delivery rider at the red light saw it.
The courier loading the truck saw it.
The long-haul driver saw it.

They smiled.

A shared, silent understanding passed through the city.

"RECEIVED."
"RECEIVED."
"RECEIVED."

The word flooded the chat groups.

On the map, the blue dots began to move again.

They ignored the giant, glowing cat and moved like a swarm of silent ants toward the Northwest.

Chen Mo watched the screen.

He let out a long, shuddering breath.

"We did it."

He hadn't just beaten the cat.

He had proven something else:

**In a world ruled by algorithms, garbage has its own power.**

**Sometimes, the humble will to survive is more tenacious than the highest privilege.**
