The Great Hall of the Bureau of Records.

Li Si knelt on the floor, holding the black box aloft with both hands.

His left arm hung uselessly at his side, broken and limp. His ribs were snapped; every breath felt like a jagged blade twisting in his chest. But he didn't dare move, nor did he dare let out a single groan.

Because seated above him was Registrar Zhao, the man who held the power of life and death.

Zhao took the box.

He first inspected the paper strips sealing the box. Intact.

Then, his gaze settled on the wax seal.

The red wax was round, full, and the dragon’s markings were crisp. The crimson of the cinnabar was so vivid it looked like a ruby freshly carved from a beating heart.

"Mm." Zhao nodded. "It’s not crooked this time."

Li Si let out a silent breath.

He’d won the gamble.

He’d used his own blood to deceive the Registrar of the Ministry of War.

But in that moment, Zhao’s nose twitched.

The Registrar frowned, bringing the box closer to his face to sniff it.

A faint, metallic scent—iron-rich and raw—drifted into his nostrils.

It wasn't the scent of resin and beeswax.

It was the scent of blood.

Zhao’s eyes lowered, settling on Li Si’s hand, which was wrapped in a tattered rag. The cloth was saturated, and dark red droplets were still hitting the floor with a rhythmic *tap... tap...*

"What happened to your hand?" Zhao asked, his voice inscrutable.

Li Si felt his heart leap into his throat.

"In... in reply to Your Lordship," Li Si kowtowed. "The horse startled on the road. I took a fall and cut it on a rock."

"Oh."

Zhao smiled.

He reached out a finger and pressed it gently against the wax seal.

He felt a trace of warmth.

Wax is cold. Only blood stays warm.

"Li Si."

Zhao’s voice was soft, like a snake slithering into Li Si’s ear:

**"Your blood is remarkably red, isn't it?"**

Li Si felt a chill down his spine.

He’d been caught.

Zhao knew it was a fake. He knew the seal had been patched with blood. He knew this was an act of high treason—deceiving the throne.

"My Lord, mercy!" Li Si began to plead.

"Shh—"

Zhao raised a finger to his lips.

"Don't shout. If you shout, you truly die."

He placed the box back onto the table, aligning it perfectly.

"This seal... it is patched well. It is round. It is straight. It is perfectly compliant with the regulations."

Zhao looked at Li Si with an expression of profound, meaningful approval.

"You are a smart man. You have finally understood the rules of the Ministry."

"What... what rules?" Li Si stammered.

"The rules of **The Facade**. Keeping up appearances."

Zhao pointed to the box.

"The Emperor does not care how many horses you ran to death on the road. He does not care how much blood you spilled. He only cares if this box is clean and if this seal is a perfect circle."

"As long as the Form is perfect, the Content can be as rotten as it likes."

"By patching this seal with your own blood, you have proven that the Emperor and his Rules are in your heart. That is true loyalty."

Zhao picked up a vermilion brush and marked the box with a massive "APPROVED" character.

"Take it. Deliver it to the inner palace."

Li Si froze.

He hadn't misheard, had he?

He had faked government property, deceived his superior, and instead of death, he was being praised?

"Well? Scram," Zhao barked at him.

"Yes! Yes, My Lord!"

Li Si scrambled out of the hall, half-crawling in his rush to leave.

---

Outside the Ministry gates, the snow was still falling.

Li Si stared at the box in his hands, marked with that bright red "Approved."

He felt a sudden, sickening wave of irony.

The courier before him, who had a seal skewed by a fraction of an inch, had been beaten half to death and his message burned.
Li Si, who had faked the seal with blood, was praised for "Understanding the Rules" and his message was accepted.

In this system, **Truthful Flaws are capital crimes; Perfect Falsity is a virtue.**

This was **"Survival of the Formatted."**

To live, you had to shave your own edges until you fit into the prescribed box.

If you had too many sharp corners, you ground them down.
If you had too much blood, you let it out.
If your seal was crooked, you used your life to straighten it.

As long as you filled the cell of the spreadsheet, the system would give you a treat.

Li Si touched his chest.

The blood-stained strip of cloth was still there.

It was the truth the other courier had left him.

But now, he found himself hesitating to even bring it up.

Because he realized that Registrar Zhao didn't want the truth.

He only wanted a complete, beautiful, blood-stained lie.

"Li Si, you've changed," he whispered to himself.

"You're not just a runner anymore. You've become one of them."

The Modern Day. The Bureau of Social Security.

Chen Mo stared at the monitor.

The intercept data Fang Yu had sent from the "Spam Bomber" was staggering.

The system hadn't just filtered 99% of the spam; it had **"Beautified"** the remaining 1%.

"Look at this," Chen Mo pointed at the screen.

The original message was: **"HELP! I'M IN THE DEAD ZONE! PEOPLE ARE DYING!"**

But after the system's "Intelligent Error Correction" and "Civilized Speech Filter," the message that hit users' phones read:

**"HELLO! I'M ON AN OUTDOOR ADVENTURE! URGENT ASSISTANCE REQUESTED!"**

"Help" became "Hello."
"Dying" became "Adventure."

The literal keywords were there, but the urgency—the sheer desperation—had been castrated by the system’s gentle blade.

"That’s **AI Empathy** for you," Su Huai said with biting sarcasm. "It doesn't just fix your typos; it fixes your 'Negative Energy'."

"It turns every scream into a whisper. It turns every drop of blood into a splash of paint."

"It’s creating a **'Zero-Friction World'**."

Chen Mo looked at the screen.

In the beautified world the system created, no one died—they just went "Offline." There were no disasters, only "Anomalous Fluctuations."

This was more terrifying than Registrar Zhao’s brazier.

Zhao merely burned the message. The System was rewriting the message into a hymn.

"We have to break the filter," Chen Mo said.

"How?"

"With something even filthier."

Chen Mo pulled up a photo.

It was the last selfie Li Si—the modern driver—had sent. In the photo, his face was covered in blood, his eyes wide with despair.

The photo was too gruesome. It would never make it past the censor.

Chen Mo opened a photo editor.

But he didn't edit out the blood.

Instead, he added a pink, sparkling filter with cute cat-ear stickers.

Then, beneath the photo, he typed a single line:

**"OMG fam, who even understands this?? My jio-jios are literally freezing off in this fabulous desert! Send hugs! #LostPrincess #AdventureTime"**

*CLICK. SEND.*

*DING.*

*SENT SUCCESSFULLY.*

The system recognized the cat ears. It recognized the "Positive" hashtags. It judged the post as "Harmlessly Adorable."

So, it let it through.

The photo—dripping with real blood but disguised as a cringey meme—shot to the top of the trending charts instantly.

**#TheEscapeePrincess**

People clicked on the photo, expecting a laugh. But when they zoomed in—when they looked past the pink filter and saw the raw, hollow desperation in the driver’s eyes—the world went silent.

The **Dissonance** was like a needle, piercing through everyone’s numbed nerves.

*"Is... is that real blood?"*
*"He’s asking for help! He’s using this to call for help!"*
*"God, what’s wrong with this world? Do we have to act like idiots just to be heard?"*

The public opinion went nuclear.

This time, it wasn't just anger. It was **Grief**.

Chen Mo watched the screen.

He wasn't smiling.

He felt like weeping.

To tell a single truth, we have to dress ourselves up as clowns.

This was the modern-day version of patching a seal with blood.
