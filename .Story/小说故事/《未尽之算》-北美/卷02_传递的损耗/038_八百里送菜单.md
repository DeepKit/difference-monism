# 第38集：八百里送菜单

> **《未尽之算》·第38集·八百里送菜单**
> **本卷进度：14/24 | System Load: 300% | 系统倒计时：972集**
> *[SYSTEM_LOG]: WARNING. MASSIVE REDUNDANT DATA CONSUMING BANDWIDTH. BUFFER OVERFLOW IMMINENT.*

---

Juyong Pass.

The blizzard had sealed the mountain passes.

Commander Wang Deren stood atop the beacon tower, watching Li Si guide a grain wagon slowly out of the main gate.

It was a standard transport cart, but instead of grain, it was piled high with black official dispatch boxes. One hundred of them. Exactly one hundred.

Every box was sealed, and every box was adorned with three vibrant red pheasant feathers. This was the Ministry of War’s highest-level military intelligence designation—the "Red Feather Express."

In the swirling snow, those red feathers looked like flickers of burning flame—stark, sharp, and resolute.

"Commander," the lieutenant whispered at his side. "Is it true... is it really all just garbage?"

"Not entirely," Wang Deren exhaled a plume of white frost. "Box Number One contains the sheep bones from my dinner last night. Number Two is a heavy stone we used to weigh down the command tent. Number Three is the mess hall menu."

"And the rest?"

"The rest... are letters home from the men."

Wang Deren watched the receding wagon, his gaze softening for a fleeting moment.

"They’re all going to die. The least I can do is let them leave a last word for their families."

"But Commander, according to the Laws of Great Ming, the courier system is strictly for military intelligence, not private mail. This is treason. Defrauding the Emperor."

"Defrauding the Emperor?" Wang Deren smiled grimly. "If fraud saves a soul, I’ll defraud him a hundred times over."

He turned back to the soldiers behind him—men with hollow cheeks and gaunt faces.

"Pass the order! Prepare for the final stand!"

"We buy that wagon three days."

---

On the Post Road.

Li Si cracked his horsewhip.

"YAH!"

The two horses pulling the wagon were already foaming at the mouth. Usually, they hauled a few hundred pounds of grain; now, they were dragging a hundred heavy mahogany boxes over ice-slicked roads at a breakneck pace.

Li Si’s heart was racing just as fast.

He knew what this trip cost.

It was a grand gamble. He was betting on Registrar Zhao’s patience, betting on the Ministry’s protocols, betting on the very fate of the Empire.

"WHOA—!"

Up ahead was the **Jiming Post Station**.

Li Si yanked the reins. The wagon slid several yards across the snow before coming to a shaky halt.

The old Postmaster rushed out, clutching a padded coat around his shoulders.

"You again? Why a wagon this time?"

"Fresh horses!" Li Si leapt down, tossing his wooden badge at the man. "One hundred Red Feather Express dispatches! Move!"

The Postmaster caught the badge, glanced at the mountain of boxes on the wagon, and nearly dropped his lantern in shock.

"One hundred?! Has the sky fallen?"

"Pretty much," Li Si shouted as he began unfastening the harnesses. "The Tatars are coming to slaughter the city. These are requests for aid."

"But... this is too many." The Postmaster’s scalp tingled at the sight of the red feathers. "The station only has twenty horses in total. That wagon needs four of the best just to move on this ice. What about the next station? And the one after that?"

"That’s your problem!" Li Si roared. "Ming Law states: Red Feather Express, take the horse if there’s a horse, take the man if there’s a man! Anyone who obstructs dies!"

The Postmaster cowed under the intensity.

"Fine! Fine! Take them! Take them all!"

He led out the four finest horses from the stables—the ones usually reserved for the Provincial Governor.

Li Si deftly swapped the teams, tucking the hundred-and-first box—the one with the real message—at the very bottom of the pile.

"Thanks, Uncle!"

He vaulted onto the seat and snapped the reins.

"HYAH!"

The wagon charged back into the blizzard.

The Postmaster watched his empty stables, close to tears.

"Done. It’s all over."

What he didn't know was that it wasn't just Jiming Station.

Across the six hundred miles of post road from Juyong Pass to Beijing, every station was experiencing the same catastrophe.

To relay these hundred boxes, thousands of taels of silver were being consumed on the road. Thousands of pounds of premium fodder were eaten. Hundreds of couriers were dragged from their warm beds into the sub-zero cold to push wagons and clear roads.

This was a **Resource Exhaustion Attack**.

We don't fight you. We exhaust you.

Li Si was like a virus, racing through the veins of Great Ming, sucking every bit of nutrient from the system as he went.

---

Three days later. The Ministry of War, Beijing.

Registrar Zhao was sipping tea.

He had just finished a draft of his *Report on the Stability of the Border Regions*, ready to present it to the Minister.

His mood was excellent.

Suddenly, a cacophony erupted outside his doors.

"REPORT—! Juyong Pass Express!"

"Bring it in," Zhao set down his cup, annoyance bubbling in his chest. Was that Li Si character back again? Hadn't he learned his lesson?

"Sir... we can't bring it in."

"What do you mean?"

"There’s too much of it."

The guard pointed toward the courtyard.

Zhao walked to the entrance.

A massive wagon sat in the center of the Ministry courtyard. A mountain of black boxes towered over it, each adorned with those piercing red feathers, now dusted with a thick layer of snow.

Zhao’s teacup slipped from his fingers.

*CRASH.*

Shards of porcelain sprayed across the floor.

"What... is this?" his voice trembled.

"Intelligence," Li Si shouted, kneeling in the slush. His face was covered in frostbite, his lips cracked and bleeding, looking like a ghost from the front lines.

"One hundred dispatches! For your eyes, My Lord!"

"One hundred?!" Zhao shrieked, his voice hitting a shrill, eunuch-like pitch. "Is Wang Deren insane? Has he written every letter he intended for the next fifty years?"

"The Commander said the matter is of grave importance, and fearing omission, he has reported in batches. He insists that you, My Lord, must open and read every single one. **Not a single character is to be missed.**"

Li Si looked up, his eyes like those of a starving wolf.

"If, due to your negligence, a critical piece of intelligence is missed and the pass falls... well, My Lord, can you bear that weight?"

---

Zhao stared at the mountain of wood.

He knew he’d been played.

This was a trap. Maybe only one was real. Maybe they were all fake. Maybe they were filled with manure.

But he didn't dare gamble.

What if one *was* real? What if that one contained the words "The Emperor must flee"?

If he didn't open them, the Censors would impeach him for "Gross Negligence" by tomorrow morning. After all, a hundred Red Feather dispatches sitting in a courtyard were impossible to hide.

If he *did* open them...

Simply checking the seals, opening, logging, reading, and filing a hundred boxes would take him three days and nights of non-stop work.

During those three days, he could do nothing else.

The Ministry of War would be paralyzed. Every other military report would be backlogged. The entire neural center of the Empire would be clogged by a pile of scrap paper.

"Open them!" Zhao hissed through gritted teeth. "Open them all!"

He and a few clerks dragged tables into the courtyard and began the work in the bitter cold.

**Box Number One.**

Seal intact. Label intact.

Empty.

No—there was a... bone?

A sheep bone. Still bearing tooth marks.

Zhao’s hand shook with rage. "What is this?!"

"My Lord," Li Si said tonelessly. "That is the border guards' lunch. The Commander said, please look—this is what we eat."

"Preposterous!"

Zhao threw the bone to the ground.

**Box Number Two.**

A heavy stone.

"And this?!"

"My Lord, that is a brick from the ramparts. The Commander said the wall has collapsed and there are no funds for repair. He asks for an allocation."

"..."

**Box Number Three.**

A mess hall menu.

**Box Number Four.**

A letter home: "Mother, your son is failing his duty..."

Zhao opened one after another.

His hands went numb. His eyes grew blurry from the flash of red feathers. His patience was being ground into dust, grain by grain.

The courtyard became a literal landfill. Bones, stones, rags, scrap paper.

The grand hall of the Ministry of War had become a dump.

More and more officials gathered to watch. They pointed and whispered.

"Has Registrar Zhao lost his mind? Scavenging in the courtyard?"
"Shh, they say it’s an express dispatch from the border."
"An express dispatch of stones? What kind of protocol is that?"

Zhao felt as if his face were being slapped repeatedly in public.

Not just his face—his authority, his dignity, his very soul was being buried under this mountain of garbage.

"ENOUGH!"

He hit Box Fifty and finally snapped.

He slammed the box he was holding into the slush.

"No more! Burn them! Burn all of it! The garbage, the boxes, and that madman too! Burn it all!"

"But My Lord, what if..." a clerk whispered cautiously. "What if there’s a real one further down?"

Zhao’s eyes were bloodshot, like a cornered beast.

"There is no 'what if'! It’s all fake! They’re all liars!"

He grabbed a handful of red feathers and tossed them into a nearby brazier.

Flame erupted.

Li Si knelt in the snow, watching the roaring fire.

He smiled.

The real message—the one hidden in his shirt—felt like it was burning against his skin.

The brighter Zhao’s fire burned, the more successful his plan became.

Because when a fire this big starts, everyone’s eyes are drawn to the smoke.

Including the Emperor’s.
