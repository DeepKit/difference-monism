# 第28集：古代的死信

> **《未尽之算》·第28集·古代的死信**

The City of Beijing.

Feather-light snow drifted from a leaden sky. The flakes landed on the vermilion palace walls, melting instantly into clear streaks of water.

Inside the Great Hall of the Bureau of Records, Ministry of War, it was warm and still.

Four massive bronze braziers burned brightly. The coal was top-grade "Silver-thread Charcoal"—smokeless, emitting only a faint, elegant scent of pine.

Registrar Zhao sat in a heavy mahogany chair, cradling a cup of hot tea with his eyes closed. He was swathed in official robes lined with sable, his feet resting on a heater. He was as comfortable as if he were drifting on a cloud.

"My Lord."

A guard entered softly and kowtowed.

"What is it?" Zhao asked, eyes remaining shut.

"An urgent dispatch from the border has arrived."

"Present it."

The guard rose and led a man into the hall.

It was a courier.

He wore the tattered uniform of a Post Messenger, caked in mud and dried blood. His face was a bruised violet from the cold, and icicles clung to his eyebrows and beard. He hobbled forward, leaving a black, muddy footprint with every step.

It was mud mixed with blood.

*THUD.*

The courier collapsed to his knees, his hands raised high above his head. He held a black box wrapped in oilcloth.

"Urgent dispatch from Juyong Pass! 800-li Express!"

His voice was a rasp, as if squeezed from a dry throat.

Zhao opened his eyes.

He glanced at the courier and frowned slightly.

"Filthy."

He waved a hand, and a guard immediately brought a silk handkerchief, using it to shield his hands as he took the black box and presented it to Zhao.

Zhao took the box.

Three red feathers were stuck to the lid—the mark of the highest-tier military intelligence.

He inspected the wax seal.

The sealer was bright vermilion cinnabar, bearing the official stamp of the Juyong Pass commander.

However...

Zhao’s gaze stopped at the seal.

"It’s crooked," he said flatly.

"What?" The courier hadn't heard clearly.

"I said, the seal is crooked."

Zhao pointed to the wax.

"By the laws of the Ming Great Code: Urgent dispatches must have the wax seal centered, and the impression must be perfectly legible. Your seal is off by a third of an inch. This is an act of disrespect toward His Majesty."

"My Lord!" the courier snapped his head up, his eyes shot through with red capillaries. "This is military intelligence! The Tatar cavalry has reached the walls! Fifty thousand riders are at the gates! Commander Wang lost three scouts just to get this message out! I ran three horses to death!"

"And?"

Zhao tossed the box back at the courier.

"Rules are rules."

"If I accept this 'disrespectful' dispatch and present it to the Emperor, and His Majesty takes offense and takes my head... who will save *my* life then?"

The courier froze.

He looked at the box on the floor. Then he looked up at the elevated, immaculate Registrar Zhao.

He didn't understand.

He was a simple runner. He thought that as long as he ran fast enough, he could save people. He thought that delivering the message would bring reinforcements.

But he didn't know that in the capital's bureaucracy, **running fast meant nothing. Running 'Correct' was everything.**

The value of a message didn't lie in its content, but in its **Format**.

If the format was wrong, even if it said the heavens were falling, it was nothing but scrap paper.

"Take it back," Zhao waved a hand dismissively. "Rewrite it. Reseal it. Run it again."

"Run it again?" the courier cried in despair. "Two thousand li there and back! It will take half a month! By the time I return, Juyong Pass will have fallen! Three thousand brothers will all be dead!"

"Then the general was incompetent," Zhao said, lifting his tea and blowing on the floating leaves. "What does that have to do with the Ministry?"

"Our duty is document compliance. If the document is non-compliant, it is an 'Invalid Command.' We do not accept invalid commands."

This was the logic of the system.

**If you cannot die according to my prescribed format, then you cannot be dead.**

Or rather, your death is "Invalid Data."

The courier didn't move.

He stared at Zhao with a haunting intensity.

Suddenly, he lunged toward Zhao’s desk, trying to shove the box into the Registrar’s robes.

"My Lord! Just look at it! Just one look!"

"Insolence!"

Guards lunged from both sides, slamming a boot into the courier’s back.

The courier let out a cry of agony, flattened against the floor. But he still gripped the box with white-knuckled fingers, refusing to let go.

"Peasant," Zhao said, dusting off his robes. "Daring to run wild in the halls of the Ministry of War."

He picked up the box.

"Since you refuse to run it back, I’ll take care of it for you."

He opened his hand.

The black box—carrying the lives of three thousand men—fell into the brazier beside him.

*HIIIISSSS—*

The flames licked at the oilcloth. The red feathers curled; the black wood turned glowing red.

The courier let out a shriek of visceral agony.

The sound was more painful than anything they could have done to his body.

Because he had run for three days and three nights, and what he had bought wasn't reinforcements. It was a pile of ash.

He watched the message turn to dust in the fire.

"Drag him out," Zhao said coldly. "Eighty strokes with the heavy staff."

The courier was dragged away.

He left a long trail of blood in the snow.

Zhao straightened his collar, looking out at the heavy snowfall.

"A timely snow bodes a bountiful year," he mused. "It’s the Emperor’s birthday soon. Better to report an auspicious omen. As for the border... as long as the report hasn't arrived, the border hasn't fallen."

**As long as I don't see it, the world is at peace.**

---

In the snow outside.

The courier lay prone, his lower back and thighs a mess of shredded flesh.

He felt his life draining away.

"The message..." he whispered.

A young Post Messenger walked over.

He was roughly eighteen or nineteen—lean, with a wiry strength, holding a horsewhip in his hand.

He was a newcomer. His name was **Li Si**.

Li Si knelt and looked at the fallen courier.

"Brother, you okay?"

The courier raised his head. His eyes were barely open.

"The message..."

He pulled a blood-stained strip of cloth from his shirt.

It was a piece he had managed to tear from the report just before the box was thrown into the fire. On it were only a few words: **"SEND REINFORCEMENTS IMMEDIATELY."**

"Send... send it out..."

The courier gripped Li Si’s hand, pressing the cloth into his palm.

"To who?" Li Si asked.

"To... to anyone who can save a life..."

The courier’s hand went limp.

He was dead.

Li Si looked at the cloth. Then at the dead man who wouldn't close his eyes.

He didn't know the full story.

But he knew this cloth was heavy.

Heavier than any official document he had ever carried.

Li Si stood up.

He tucked the cloth into an inner pocket, close to his skin.

He glanced at the gates of the Ministry of War. Those crimson doors were shut tight, looking like a maw that swallowed people whole.

"I’ll get it there," Li Si said to the corpse.

He turned and walked into the wind and snow.

He was the new messenger.

He was the new Zhang San.
