**HISTORICAL ARCHIVAL REPLAY: CHENGHUA ERA, MING DYNASTY.**
**LOCATION: JIMING POST STATION.**

Li Si returned to Jiming Post.

He’d expected to see fresh horses waiting, couriers ready for their leap of faith. He had even fantasized about the Postmaster waiting at the gate with a bowl of hot wine, clapping him on the shoulder and saying, "Well done, brother."

Instead, he saw flies.

Legions of green-headed flies swarming around the horse troughs, their buzzing wings forming a thick, black haze.

There were no horses in the troughs.

There were only corpses.

A dozen post-horses lay on the ground, foaming at the mouth, their bellies bloated like drums. Their eyes were wide, glazed, reflecting the murky gray of the winter sky.

They had been run to death.

To relay those hundred boxes of stones and menus, they had covered three months' worth of distance in three days. They had no rest, no water—only the crack of the whip and the roars of the couriers.

The Postmaster sat collapsed on the threshold, a broken whip in his hand, his eyes vacant.

"Where are the horses?" Li Si asked. His voice was soft, as if he were afraid of waking the dead.

"Dead." The Postmaster pointed at the ground. "All of them."

"And the horses at the next station?"

"Dead too." The Postmaster gave a hollow laugh. "To keep the chain moving, every horse was mobilized. Huailai, Xuanfu, Datong... every animal on the line is spent."

"On this post-road now, you couldn't find a single donkey if your life depended on it."

Li Si froze.

He touched the real request for aid tucked into his shirt.

This was the 101st letter the Commander had given him.

The Commander had said: "The first hundred are to clog the road. This one is to save the soul."

"Wait until Registrar Zhao is driven mad by the waste paper. Wait until the Ministry's system is paralyzed. Then, and only then, you deliver this true letter."

"In that moment, this letter will be the only Truth left in the world."

The Commander’s calculation had been precise.

But he had missed one thing.

**Systems can be clogged, but resources can also be exhausted.**

Those hundred junk dispatches hadn't just drained Registrar Zhao’s patience; they had drained the lifeblood of the courier network. They were like a swarm of locusts, devouring all the vitality of the road.

Now, the road was open—largely because no one was left to send a message—but the vehicles were gone.

This was the **Cost of Saturated Attack**.

In your eagerness to kill the enemy, you had burned your own granaries to the ground.

---

Li Si watched the dead horses.

He recognized one. It was his favorite bay, called "Windchaser." Every time he passed through, he would feed it a handful of black beans.

Now, Windchaser lay in the mud, covered in whip-welts.

The animal hadn't known why it was running. It hadn't known if it carried stones or the fate of an empire.

It just ran. Until it died.

Just like him.

"How much further?" Li Si asked.

"Three hundred miles to Beijing," the Postmaster said. "Without a horse, it’ll take you three days on foot."

"Three days..."

Li Si closed his eyes.

In three days, Juyong Pass would already be a stable for Tatar horses.

The Commander would be dead. His brothers would be dead.

And I should be dead, too.

He sank to the ground, feeling the strength drain from his legs as if a plug had been pulled.

"Give it up," the Postmaster handed him a waterskin. "Drink. We did our best. This is just Fate."

Li Si took the skin.

The water was cold.

But he didn't drink.

He remembered the look in the Commander’s eyes when he’d handed over the letter.

"Brother, the lives of three thousand men are on your shoulders."

That look was like a thorn, driven deep into his heart.

"I can't stop," Li Si whispered to himself.

He crouched down, unfastened his leggings, and pulled them tight again. His fingers were swollen with frostbite; every movement was a jolt of white-hot pain.

He tucked the letter into the innermost pocket of his coat, right against his chest.

"What are you doing?" the Postmaster asked.

"Running."

"How can you run with no horse?"

"I have legs."

Li Si stood up and looked toward the capital.

The snow was still falling. The post-road was a white serpent extending into an invisible distance.

"**I am a courier. As long as my legs aren't broken, the road isn't dead.**"

He tossed the waterskin back to the Postmaster.

"Keep it. And... bury the horses."

With that, he charged out of the station.

On a post-road littered with dead horses, a tiny, solitary figure began to sprint.

One step. Two. Three.

His lungs felt like they were going to burst. His legs felt like they were cast in lead.

But he didn't stop.

He was like a **stubborn red blood cell**, desperately trying to pump one last breath of oxygen into a body that was already clinically dead.

---

The Modern Northwest. The Dead Zone.

The wind howled like a wounded beast.

A sandstorm like a wall of solid yellow pressed in. Visibility was less than five meters.

The rescue convoy was forced to a halt.

"We can't do it!" the lead driver’s voice crackled over the radio. "The wind is too strong! If we keep going, the trucks are going to flip!"

"The roads are buried! GPS is dead!"

Fang Yu stared at his laptop.

The screen was static. The satellite link was severed.

"Chen Mo, we’ve lost him," Fang Yu said in despair. "Li Si’s signal is gone."

Chen Mo stared out the window.

Yellow sand everywhere. Nothing but sand and the roar of the gale.

This was **"Physical Denial of Service."**

In the face of the absolute power of nature, human technology was a fragile toy.

"We did our best," A-K patted Chen Mo’s shoulder. "No one survives a storm like this out here."

Chen Mo said nothing.

He opened the truck door and stepped out.

Dust instantly filled his mouth and nostrils. Sand stung his face like a hail of needles.

He stood in the wind and roared at the top of his lungs:

"LI SI!!"

The wind swallowed his voice instantly.

But he kept screaming.

"LI SI!! HOLD ON!!"

He didn't know if Li Si could hear him.

But he had to scream.

Because a thousand years ago, a man named Li Si had been running through the snow just like this.

Even without a horse, even without a road, with nothing but two legs.

"**As long as his legs weren't broken, the road wasn't dead.**"
