Li Si returned to Juyong Pass.

Or rather, he returned to the ruins of Juyong Pass.

He had been running for seven days without closing his eyes. He had tucked the official receipt—the one bearing the Ministry of War’s grand seal—into his shirt as if it were an Imperial Decree, a magic charm that could save lives.

He’d thought: *As long as I bring this back, as long as I tell the Commander the court knows, everything will be fine.*

He was wrong.

Standing before the once-might fortress, he saw nothing but fire.

A sea of roaring flame.

The ramparts had half-collapsed, looking like a giant with a broken spine. Plumes of black smoke rose everywhere, mixed with the cacophony of slaughter.

The tracks of Tatar horses were a tangled mess in the snow, churned with blood that hadn't yet frozen.

Li Si stood frozen. His horse had finally given out, collapsing at the roadside in a spray of white froth. He held the receipt in his hand, and it felt as if it weighed a thousand pounds.

"How... how can this be?" he whispered. "The court said... they said there was no war."

---

He stumbled into the pass, one leaden foot after another.

Dead men were everywhere. Defenders in their red surcoats, Tatars in their thick furs—their corpses were entwined, a landscape of slaughter where it was impossible to tell friend from foe.

A wounded soldier crawled from a pile of bodies, grabbing Li Si’s hem.

The left side of the man’s face had been hacked away, exposing the stark white of the bone. But his remaining eye was wide, flickering with a final, desperate light.

"The reinforcements?" the soldier’s voice sounded like it was being dragged from the bottom of a well. "Did you deliver the message? When is the Imperial Army coming?"

Li Si opened his mouth.

He wanted to say, "I delivered it." He wanted to say, "The court knows."

But he looked down at the receipt in his hand.

The paper, marked with that bright vermilion seal, bore eight bold characters:

**"NO EVIDENCE OF CONFLICT. STAND BY FOR ORDERS."**

Those eight characters felt like eight mountains, crushing the breath from his lungs.

"The court says..." Li Si’s voice was a tremor. "The court says there is no war."

"What?" The soldier blinked.

He pointed at the wall of fire around them, at the mountains of corpses, at his own ruined face.

"No war? Then what is this? Are we fighting ghosts?"

*PFT!*

A stray arrow sang through the air, piercing the soldier’s throat.

He collapsed, his eye still fixed on the receipt in Li Si’s hand.

Asking: **Why?**

*Why did we spill all this blood, only for the court to say they can't see it?*
*Why did so many of us die, only to leave no names behind?*

---

Li Si sank to his knees.

He was a runner. He’d spent his whole life running.

His mentor had said: *Run fast, and you’ll save lives.*
Registrar Zhao had said: *Follow the format, and you’ll save the state.*

He’d outrun the wind, the snow, even the cracked wax seal. He had turned himself into a perfect part, slotted into the great machine.

But he had lost to the paper.

The paper said "Peace," so this place *had* to be at peace.
If this place wasn't at peace, then it was the people here who were wrong.

**They hadn't just died. They had died "Inconveniently."**

Their deaths were a stain on the Golden Age. A disruption of the Emperor’s mood. A challenge to Registrar Zhao’s performance review.

Therefore, they had to be "A market brawl."

Li Si let out a sudden laugh.

It was a sound more harrowing than a sob.

He pulled the receipt from his shirt and held it to a smoldering beam.

The flames licked the paper. The vermilion seal twisted, turned black, and vanished into ash.

"Fake," Li Si whispered, tossing the remains into the fire. "It’s all fake."

"The road is fake. The seal is fake. I am fake."

"Only the dead are real."

---

He stood up.

He was no longer the submissive, cowering Post Messenger.

He reached down and picked up a notched waist-blade. A common soldier's blade, the hilt still wet with the dead man’s blood.

He didn't know how to fight. He only knew how to run.

But he didn't want to run anymore.

In front of him, a Tatar rider was circling, swinging his scimitar at a group of fleeing women and children.

Li Si gripped the blade tight.

He thought of the two acres back home. He thought of Xiao Hua. He thought of his mentor’s words about the Emperor sleeping soundly.

"To hell with the Emperor."

Li Si roared.

He charged the rider.

"ARGHHH!!!"

A flash of steel.

Li Si collapsed in the slush, his chest carved open by the scimitar.

But he smiled.

Because his blade had found the horse’s belly.

The horse went down. The rider fell into the snow and was instantly swarmed by the desperate women, who crushed him under a rain of jagged stones.

Li Si lay on the ground, staring up at the sky.

An eagle was circling high above.

It flew so high. It must have seen everything. The fire, the blood, even the tea in Registrar Zhao’s cup.

But it said nothing. It just watched.

Just like the System, cold and distant.

"Next life..." Li Si closed his eyes. "No more running."

"I want to be the eagle."
