# 第5集：验尸格目

> **《未尽之算》·第5集·验尸格目**
> **本卷进度：5/24 | 张三的KPI：100% (暂) | 系统倒计时：1005集**

Chen Mo was dreaming.

In the dream, he was no longer the middle-aged office drone sitting in the Social Security basement. He had become a man from the distant past, kneeling in the deep snow.

The snow beneath his knees had melted into slush, soaking through his padded trousers. A bone-chilling cold crawled up through the gaps in his joints.

But he didn't dare move.

Before him lay a young girl’s corpse and a sheet of paper even whiter than the body.

That paper was called the **"Yan Shi Ge Mu"—The Ledger of Death**.

In the Great Song Dynasty, death wasn't simply called death; it was called "clearing the account" (销账). And this ledger was the receipt for that clearance.

"Zhang San," a lazy voice drifted down from above. "Why are you dazing off? Get on with it."

Chen Mo—or rather, Zhang San—looked up.

Seated in a grand carved chair was Registrar Zhou. He was wrapped in a thick fox-fur robe, cradling an exquisite copper hand-warmer. Faint trails of blue smoke drifted from the intricate patterns of the lid.

It was high-grade agarwood.

In this morgue filled with the stench of rot and mildew, that fragrance was jarring—and profoundly arrogant.

"My Lord," Zhang San’s voice sounded like a frozen stone. "It is too cold. The body has frozen solid. It is difficult to examine."

"Frozen solid?" Master Zhou took a sip of hot tea. "Then douse it with wine and vinegar. Thaw it with charcoal. Do I really have to teach you your own trade?"

He pointed to the mountain of corpses piled nearby.

"These one hundred and eight bodies must be processed tonight. If even one is missing, tomorrow’s report won't add up. The entire Kaifeng Prefecture’s performance review for this month will be rated 'Inadequate' (下下)."

An 'Inadequate' rating meant the rice rations for over a hundred officials in the prefecture would be cut in half.

Zhang San said nothing.

He reached into his robe and pulled out a suanpan.

It was missing a corner, polished smooth by years of use. Under the flickering oil lamp, the beads glinted with a cold light.

"Do not worry, My Lord," Zhang San whispered. "The dead do not lie. Neither does the ledger."

He placed the suanpan on the ground and began to grind the ink.

The ink swirled in the inkstone, emitting a nauseating, fishy stench. It was mixed with pig bile to prevent it from freezing.

Chen Mo felt his consciousness anchored to Zhang San, both vivid and blurred. He could feel the chilblains on Zhang San’s hands; he could smell the bile. But he could not control Zhang San’s movements.

He could only watch.

Watching this colleague from a thousand years ago, under the crushing weight of the 'system,' turn human lives into rows of data.

---

Many people think ancient autopsies were as simple as stabbing a throat with a silver needle.

They are wrong.

In the Song Dynasty, forensic examination was a precise science of mathematics.

The *Xi Yuan Ji Lu* (Collected Cases of Injustice Rectified) dictated a "Four Records, Three Verifications" process: initial, secondary, tertiary, and final oversight. Every step required different witnesses to prevent collusion.

And the most critical part was filling out the **Yan Shi Ge Mu**.

The body was divided into thirty-six sections: crown, forehead, brow, eyes, nose, mouth, ears... all the way down to the soles of the feet.

Each part had seven possible states of injury: swelling, bruising, blade wounds, charring, broken bone, poisoning, or no injury.

The *wuzuo* had to measure the length, width, and depth of every wound, accurate to the *fen* (1/10th of an inch), and enter them into the corresponding grid on the ledger.

It was, for all intents and purposes, a prehistoric Excel spreadsheet.

As long as you filled all those hundreds of boxes, the cause of death would arrive automatically.

The system's designers meant well: prevent falsification, ensure justice.

But on the execution layer, the system had a different flavor.

Because the men filling those boxes also had to eat.

Zhang San was a natural talent. His eyes were calipers.

"First body. Male, approximately forty years old."

Zhang San used a bamboo sliver to lift the corpse’s eyelid.

"Left eyebrow: blue swelling, length three *fen*."

The clerk beside him immediately marked the ledger.

"Right ribs: blunt impact, two inches wide."

"Left calf: fracture, bone protruding."

As Zhang San called out the numbers, he flicked the beads of the suanpan on the ground.

*Click, clack.*

The sound of the beads colliding in the dead silence of the night felt like a macabre rhythm.

What was he calculating?

He was calculating the "Value Point" of the corpse.

According to the unwritten rules of Kaifeng Prefecture, for every "unclaimed body," if it was marked as "Death by Natural Causes," the government only had to pay 500 *wen* for burial. If it was marked as "Homicide," the government had to open a case, investigate, and pursue suspects—a cost of at least 50 *taels* of silver.

500 *wen* versus 50 *taels*.

A hundredfold difference.

So, Master Zhou’s hint was clear: **If you can mark it as natural, don't you dare mark it as murder.**

Zhang San’s fingers moved across body after body.

Most were refugees. Frozen to death, starved to death, or beaten to death over a piece of bread.

These were easy to handle.

"Right leg ulcers, bone deep. Cause of death: Exposure."

"Abdomen sunken, stomach empty. Cause of death: Starvation."

The clerk’s brush danced across the paper. Ledgers were filled, stamped with bright red seals, and tossed into the "Closed Cases" basket.

In this assembly line, a corpse was no longer a person; it was a batch of pending data.

Zhang San never thought anything was wrong with that.

He was a cog in the system. Cogs do not think; they only turn.

Until he reached the seventh body.

---

It was the body of a young girl. She looked no more than fifteen or sixteen.

She wore a red padded coat—old and frayed, but washed very clean.

Zhang San’s fingers moved across her cold skin.

She was very clean.

No blade wounds, no ligature marks, no dark spots of poison.

Not even a single chilblain.

According to the ledger, this should be marked as "Sudden Death by Unknown Illness."

But Zhang San’s hand stopped.

Over the girl’s heart, he felt a slight anomaly.

It wasn't a wound.

It was a... microscopic collapse.

This depression was so hidden it was invisible to the naked eye. Only the most experienced *wuzuo*, pressing inch by inch with his fingers, could feel that the ribs there had slumped downward by a single *fen*.

She had been struck by someone using extreme force, through a thick blanket, shattering her internal organs and severing her heart’s pulse.

It was a highly sophisticated technique of murder.

No blood, no external signs.

And more importantly—**it was not in the ledger**.

Zhang San looked up at Registrar Zhou.

Master Zhou was resting with his eyes closed, his fingers tapping the table in a light, rhythmic beat. It was the tune of *Qing Ping Yue*.

To him, this girl was merely a jarring note in that melody, easily flicked away by his fingertips.

"Why have you stopped?" Master Zhou opened his eyes, a flash of annoyance in them.

"My Lord," Zhang San pointed to the girl’s chest. "This person was murdered."

"Where is the wound?"

"Internal heart vessels severed. No external trace."

Master Zhou laughed.

He stood up, fur-clad like a sleek, arrogant cat, and walked over to Zhang San.

He looked down at the ledger.

"Zhang San, you've been in this trade for ten years. Tell me: is there a column in the ledger for 'Internal heart vessels severed'?"

Zhang San froze.

He looked down at the form.

There wasn't.

The ledger only had "Blade Wound," "Blunt Force," "Manual Strike," "Thermal Damage"...

It even had columns for "Lightning Strike" and "Animal Attack."

But there was no entry for "Internal Force Shattering the Heart."

Because the high officials who designed these forms didn't believe in the existence of martial arts masters. Or rather, they didn't want to offend the masters of the powerful families.

"Beyond the ledger, there are no wounds." Master Zhou’s voice was soft, yet as cold as the snow. "If it cannot be entered, it does not exist."

"But My Lord, a human life..."

"A human life?" Master Zhou cut him off, pointing to the Blizzard outside. "All one hundred and eight of these are refugees. Freezing, starving, or dying of illness is reasonable. But 'killed by a martial master' is unreasonable."

"Why?"

"Because that implies the killer is an expert. And experts usually serve as guards for the nobility."

Master Zhou leaned down, his mouth near Zhang San’s ear.

In that instant, Chen Mo smelled the thick, heavy agarwood on Registrar Zhou.

It was the scent of power.

"Zhang San, do not invite trouble for the system. Write 'Exposure.' It is easier for everyone."

"Furthermore," Master Zhou pulled a silver voucher from his sleeve, placing it lightly on the girl’s chest. "As long as you write 'Exposure,' this fifty *taels* of silver is her family’s compensation."

"If you write 'Homicide,' the case becomes an 'Unresolved Case.' Unresolved cases yield no compensation, and her family will be dragged into the dungeons for interrogation."

"Do you want her family to live well with the money, or do you want to destroy them?"

Zhang San gripped the brush. His hand was shaking.

That fifty-tael voucher was like a glowing coal, pressing down on the corpse’s heart.

Inside Zhang San’s body, Chen Mo felt it all.

This was Old Zhao’s logic.

**"Sacrifice one to save a thousand."**

Master Zhou’s logic was: **"Tell one lie to save a family."**

How merciful it sounded.

To secure compensation for the family, we must bury the truth of the murder.

To maintain the system's stability, we must discard the "unquantifiable" anomalies.

Zhang San looked at the girl.

Her eyes were closed, and her eyelashes were still tipped with frost. She looked like she was sleeping peacefully.

But inside her chest, her heart had been shattered into pieces.

If he wrote "Exposure," her grievance would be sunk into the depths of the ocean forever. The killer would walk free, using the same technique on the next girl.

And if he didn't...

Zhang San thought of his mother. His blind mother, waiting at home for him to bring back his rice rations for the New Year.

If he offended Registrar Zhou, his uniform would be stripped. He, too, would become a refugee.

"Zhang San," Master Zhou patted his shoulder. "Don't be too rigid. Stones with sharp edges don't roll far."

"I am a *wuzuo*."

Zhang San suddenly spoke.

His voice was hoarse, as if squeezed from his very marrow.

"I only know how to examine bodies. I do not know how to 'be a man' in your world."

He picked up the brush.

Master Zhou smiled, lifting his teacup, waiting for the ink to fall.

Zhang San wrote.

But he didn't write in the "Cause of Death" column.

He moved the tip of his brush to the "Remarks" section at the bottom—the place usually left blank.

The brush tip pierced the paper. The ink seeped through.

He wrote three characters:

**“受暗力” (受暗力—Subjected to Hidden Force)**.

It was a loophole.

He didn't say "murdered"; he said "subjected to force." He hadn't lied, but he hadn't fully torn down the curtain either.

This was the survival wisdom he had learned from ten years in the system: **If you cannot smash the wall, walk alongside it.**

*Crash!*

The teacup shattered at Zhang San’s feet. Scalding tea splashed everywhere, making his knees jerk.

Master Zhou snatched the ledger, glanced at it, and tore it in half.

"Subjected to hidden force?"

He threw the shredded paper into Zhang San’s face.

"Of the seventy-two causes of death in the Song Dynasty law code, which one is called 'Hidden Force'? Are you trying to give the Ministry of Justice a riddle to solve?"

"My Lord, I..."

"Rewrite it."

Master Zhou threw down a fresh sheet.

"No more clever tricks. Either write 'Exposure' or 'Natural Causes.' If you dare invent a word again, I will charge you with 'Subverting the Law' (乱法)."

Zhang San looked at the pristine white paper.

His "survival wisdom" had failed.

The system had evolved. It no longer permitted ambiguity. It required you to pick a side.

You were either its component or its waste. There was no middle ground.

Zhang San turned his head toward the corpse.

Suddenly, he noticed the girl was clutching something in her hand.

He pried her fingers open.

It was a piece of candy.

Half a bit of maltose, melted and covered in dirt.

In that instant, Chen Mo’s soul and Zhang San’s soul merged.

In the modern basement, Lin Xiaocao had handed Chen Mo a piece of candy.
In the ancient morgue, Zhang San pulled a piece of candy from a corpse's hand.

It was the same **Irreducible Variable**.

Zhang San squeezed the candy in his palm.

The sticky sensation made him feel sick, but it also made him feel furious.

"My Lord," Zhang San looked up, and for the first time, there was light in his eyes. "This ledger... I cannot fill it."

"Why not?"

"Because this form is too small. It cannot hold a human life."

Master Zhou’s face darkened.

"Fine. Very well."

"Since you cannot fill it, then don't."

"Guard!"

Two laborers rushed in.

"Take this body away. Since the *wuzuo* cannot find a cause of death, process it as an unclaimed corpse. Send it to the crematorium. Burn it."

"Burn it?!" Zhang San stood up abruptly. "The ledger isn't finished! It’s against the regulations!"

"Regulations?" Master Zhou sneered. "I am the Registrar. My words are the regulations."

With a wave of his hand, the guards pushed Zhang San aside and hauled the body away.

Zhang San watched as the girl was carried out the door.

The wind and snow swirled in, blowing out the oil lamp.

In the darkness, Zhang San gripped the candy, hearing the distant sound of the watchman’s drum.

It was **Zi Shi**—midnight.

A new day had begun.

But in Zhang San’s heart, the long night had only just arrived.

---

Chen Mo woke up with a start.

He found himself slumped over his desk at the Social Security Bureau, his hand still tightly clutching the candy Lin Xiaocao had given him.

A massive headache felt like an axe splitting his skull.

"Ugh..."

Chen Mo covered his nose, feeling a warm liquid streaming out.

It was a nosebleed.

He wiped it away messily. It was a heavy flow, staining his sleeve.

These were the early symptoms of radiation sickness. To sever those fiber optic cables, he had exposed himself to high radiation for five full minutes.

Beyond the blood, he felt a wave of intense nausea. His stomach churned, but having not eaten for two days, there was nothing to bring up.

"Zhang San..." he panted, looking at the blood on his hand.

In that moment, he couldn't tell if the blood was his own or the girl's from Zhang San's dream.

He pulled the suanpan from his pocket.

The chipped corner was exceptionally jarring under the light.

"The work you didn't finish back then," Chen Mo whispered to the suanpan, "I will finish it for you."

"You couldn't save the girl in the red coat."

"But I will save Lin Xiaocao."

The countdown on the wall ticked once.
