> **《未尽之算》·第1集·多余的人**

> [!NOTE]
> **[SYSTEM_NOTE: CULTURAL ANCHOR]**
> This narrative operates in a near-future Chinese metropolis where social benefits are dictated by AI. Here, a **"Credit Score"** is more than financial—it is a measure of your worth to the collective. To drop below the threshold is to vanish.

---

Thanks for the invite. I'm at the Social Security Bureau, just got off the night shift.

My name is Chen Mo. If I had to define my job, I’d say I run the shop beside the crematorium. Every day, I watch people coming and going—some crying, some laughing—but in the end, everyone just turns into a wisp of smoke.

My official title is "Exceptional Disposition Specialist," a cog in the **Department of Exceptional Disposition**—the human overflow valve for when the bureaucracy's logic hits a wall. It sounds high-end, but in reality, I’m just a trash collector for "System Bugs."

In this hyper-algorithmic age, 99% of social security audits are handled by AI. You get sick, you’re hospitalized, you die, how much you’re paid—the system calculates that in a millisecond. Precise, efficient, and cold-blooded.

The remaining 1% is what we call the "Exceptions."

Take, for example, the little girl sitting in front of me right now.

---

The basement of the Social Security Bureau has no windows. To save electricity, only a single fluorescent light on the ceiling stays on, emitting an irritating buzz. That sound feels like a swarm of flies rubbing their legs together right next to your ear; it makes your brain throb.

The air is thick with the smell of old archival paper, mixed with the sour bitterness of cheap instant coffee. This is the smell I’ve lived with for five whole years.

For five years, I’ve sat in this seat and processed 3,642 "Exceptions."

I’ve seen elderly people trying to scam insurance to buy a house for their sons, workers on the verge of jumping off buildings because their disability claims were stuck, and mothers kneeling on the floor, begging me for help because they couldn't afford medical treatment.

I’ve seen too much despair. To the point where my heart has become as hard and cold as the concrete floor of this basement.

But today’s case is a bit different.

The girl sits on a peeling plastic chair.

She looks about seven or eight, wearing a blue-and-white striped hospital gown that’s far too big for her. It’s clearly an adult version resized—the sleeves are rolled up several times, and the collar reveals collarbones as thin as twigs.

Her head is bald, without a single hair, and several unhealed needle marks are visible on her scalp. Those are the traces left by long-term infusions.

She clutches a crumpled registration slip in her hand, her feet shoved into a pair of pink plastic slippers with worn edges. The slippers feature a rabbit with only one ear.

"Uncle," she looks up, her voice so faint it’s as if she’s afraid of disturbing the dust here, "why did my Green Code turn red?"

I look at the screen in front of me.

On the monitor, her personal file is flashing a piercing red light.

**姓名：林小草**
**年龄：7岁**
**状态：已核销（死亡）**
**死因：系统优化**

My hand, holding the mouse, tightens.

"The system made a mistake," I lie to her, my voice a bit raspy. "Uncle will change it back for you."

I tap the keyboard and enter my administrator password. I want to overwrite that red status, to turn it back into even a yellow "Pending Review."

`错误：权限不足。`
`错误：操作违规。`

A cold, black dialog box pops up on the screen, looking like a black coffin:

**【警告：该样本已被标记为“负资产”。强制复活需消耗管理员信用分。是否继续？】**

I freeze.

My finger hovers over the Enter key, but I just can't press it down.

My Credit Score is down to 60. That's the passing line.

If it drops any further, I’ll be rated as an "Inadequate Employee." According to the bottom-out elimination system, I’ll be packing my bags and leaving by next month.

I’m thirty-five years old this year.

Five years ago, to get married, I bought a apartment in this city. The down payment drained my parents' life savings, and I took on a **2.8 million yuan mortgage—roughly $400,000, two decades of blood and sweat on an average salary**.

In the end, the wedding never happened. My fiancée thought my work was too depressing—not only was I broke, but I was also full of negative energy, like a dead man. She left, leaving me the hollow shell of a home and a 12,000 yuan monthly payment that felt more like a ransom.

The year before last, my father passed away. A brain hemorrhage. It was sudden; he didn't even leave a word.

Last year, my mother developed Alzheimer’s. I sent her to a nursing home. Even the cheapest one costs 6,000 yuan a month for caregiver fees.

Twelve thousand plus six thousand equals eighteen thousand.

And my salary, after taxes and benefits, is exactly 18,500 yuan.

Every month, I only have 500 yuan left for food.

I don’t dare get sick, I don’t dare take time off, and I don't dare be late. Even going to the supermarket to buy discounted vegetables requires meticulous calculation.

I live like a dog. A dog on a chain held by the system.

But I don’t dare let go of that chain. Because once I do, I’ll become a stray.

In this system, unemployment doesn't just mean having no money; it means your social credit rating will instantly plummet to the bottom. You won't be able to buy high-speed rail tickets, you won't be able to get loans, and you'll even be placed at the very end of the line at the hospital.

I, too, am a "Negative Asset" in waiting.

I look at the girl.

She is very quiet. She doesn't cry or make a scene; she just lowers her head, playing with a piece of candy in her hand.

It’s the cheapest kind of fruit drop, orange-flavored. The wrapper has already melted from the sweat of her palm, and the sticky sugar liquid clings to her fingertips.

She carefully licks her finger, as if tasting some rare delicacy.

That is the only sweetness in her entire world.

She is like a lost parcel, unclaimed and with nowhere to go.

In this massive, precise, unquestionable system, she is redundant.

And so am I.

---

"Chen Mo,"

The automatic door behind me slides open with a faint hiss of released air pressure.

Old Zhao walks in.

He’s wearing that faded grey jacket with frayed cuffs, holding that thermos that never quite screws shut. Steam rises from the lid—his only source of warmth in this freezing basement.

Old Zhao is our department director. In reality, he’s just a bigger trash collector.

He’s fifty-five this year. He’s lost most of his hair, but his belly keeps growing. He often says it’s an "occupational injury." Calculating other people's fates in the office every day makes his chest feel tight, and he only manages to suppress it with tea.

"Don't waste your energy. This case was specially authorized from above."

Old Zhao walks up behind me, glances at the red light on the screen, his tone as flat as if he were talking about what's for dinner.

"Above?" I turn my chair. "Which 'above'? Those geezers at the main office who only know how to read reports?"

"Higher than that." Old Zhao points to the ceiling, where thick data cables coil like giant pythons. "The 'Algorithm Optimization Group'."

My heart skips a beat.

The Algorithm Optimization Group. A department that exists only in legends. They aren't responsible for specific business; they are responsible for setting the rules. If we are the system's janitors, they are its architects.

Or rather, its gods.

"They’re testing a new model. Codename: 'Human Optimization Initiative'—though some of the geeks call it the 'Human Instrumentality Project'." Old Zhao blows on the tea leaves floating at the rim of his cup. "This girl is Test Subject Zero."

"Testing what?"

"Testing **'The limit of social resource pressure capacity'**."

Old Zhao takes a sip of tea and places the thermos on the desk with a heavy thud.

"Chen Mo, you haven't been in this business for just a day. You should know about 'Quality-Adjusted Life Years' (QALYs), right?"

Of course I do. That's the formula the social security system uses to put a price on life.

A healthy young person living for a year counts as 1 point.
A paralyzed elderly person living for a year counts as 0.5 points.
A person in a vegetative state counts as 0 points.

No matter who you are, if your treatment cost divided by your QALY score exceeds that invisible "cost-effectiveness threshold" (say, three times the per capita GDP), the system shows a red light.

This isn't just economics; it's a digital version of the Trolley Problem.

"This girl," Old Zhao points at that small back, "suffers from an extremely rare genetic defect. If we treat her, she needs a bone marrow transplant, and follow-up anti-rejection treatment for at least another five years. The total cost is roughly 850,000 yuan."

"Furthermore, the cure rate is only 5%."

"In other words, there's a 95% probability we’re just throwing 850,000 yuan down the drain."

Old Zhao turns around and looks at me, his eyes filled with the weariness and helplessness of a veteran.

"Chen Mo, 850,000 yuan. If we save this money, it's enough to buy two dialysis machines for a community hospital. Enough to give a thousand healthy children flu vaccines."

"Would you rather save this one child destined to die, or would you rather save those thousand future hopes?"

"The system has already calculated it. The optimal solution is: **Stop losses**."

---

He was right.

Every word was true. Every logic was impeccable.

This is the terrifying thing about the system. It doesn't talk to you about feelings; it talks to you about mathematics.

Before mathematics, a conscience is like a remainder that won't divide evenly—both an eyesore and redundant.

If I were a rational manager, I would make the same choice.

To sacrifice the interests of a tiny minority for the sake of the majority. This is called the "Big Picture."

"But Old Zhao," I look at the girl, who is currently putting that sticky candy into her mouth, "she's still alive. She's right here. She's not a number."

"She has a name. Her name is Lin Xiaocao. She still eats candy."

"And," I point at the screen, "her survival instinct index is 100%. She wants to live."

Old Zhao sighs.

He pulls a printed A4 sheet from his pocket and places it in front of me.

**《强制出院确认单》**

"Chen Mo, don't be too rigid. Hard stones don't roll far." Old Zhao pats my shoulder. "Just sign it. Let her go. Send her to the hospice ward; they have free painkillers there. At least she can go without suffering."

"This, too, is a form of mercy."

I look at that paper.

The signature line is just a black horizontal stroke. Like a blade.

As long as I sign my name on it, my mission is complete.

The red light will turn green. That piercing "Warning" will disappear. My Credit Score will bounce back to 65. The mortgage for next month will be covered. My mother’s caregiver fees will be covered.

The girl will be quietly transported away. Then, on some late night, she will die quietly.

The world will keep spinning. The stock market will rise, the subways will run, and everyone will keep laughing at short videos.

No one will remember a girl named Lin Xiaocao who was "optimized" away because the system wouldn't pay for her treatment.

It’s very reasonable.

It’s so reasonable it makes me want to vomit.

I pick up the pen. It’s a cheap ballpoint issued by the unit, the barrel warmed by my hand.

My hand is shaking.

Two voices are fighting in my head.

One voice says: "Sign it. You’re just a small clerk; you can’t save her. You need to survive, too."
The other voice says: "Don’t sign. If you sign, you’re truly dead."

"Uncle."

Just then, the girl suddenly turns around.

She hasn't eaten the candy. She holds out that sticky, dust-and-fingerprint-covered piece of candy toward me.

"Uncle, eat."

Her eyes are large, with clear whites and blacks. Because of the chemotherapy, her eye sockets are sunken, making those eyes seem even larger.

There is no fear, no resentment, not even sadness.

Only a kind of innocence that makes my heart tremble.

"Mom said, you won't hurt anymore after eating candy."

"Uncle, does something hurt? I saw you shaking all the time."

I look at the candy.

It’s half of an orange-flavored hard drop. In this basement filled with the smell of disinfectant and mildew, it gives off a faint, sweet aroma.

It is her only possession in the whole world.

It is something the system cannot calculate.

The system can calculate cost, it can calculate KPIs, but it can never calculate how heavy a piece of candy is.

In that instant, I suddenly feel a wave of intense vertigo.

That dizziness doesn't come from anemia, but from... a displacement of time and space.

The sound of the fan in my ears fades away.

In its place is a **"Click, Clack"** sound.

Crisp. Dry.

Like an abacus bead being flicked hard by a very heavy hand.

Strange images flash before my eyes.

---

**[系统日志：检测到记忆碎片注入。来源：未知。]**

I see a pair of hands.

They aren't mine. They are rough, covered in chilblains. Black dirt is packed under the fingernails.

These hands are holding a chipped, old abacus, flicking beads amidst a heavy snowfall.

The background isn't the Social Security Bureau, but a dilapidated ancient government office.

It is **Song Dynasty · Kaifeng Prefecture**.

I hear a man's voice. Raspy, exhausted, yet carrying a stubborn grit.

"My Lord, this account doesn't add up."

The man is kneeling in the snow, speaking to a high-ranking official seated in a grand chair.

"One hundred and eight corpses mean one hundred and eight lives. Every life must be examined. Every wound must be entered into the ledger."

"The system can calculate cost, it can calculate KPIs, but it can never calculate how heavy a piece of candy is."

In the image, that man is also clutching a piece of candy.

It is his weakness, but also his weapon.

---

I snap back to reality.

Cold sweat trickles down my forehead, dripping onto the *Mandatory Discharge Confirmation Form*, blurring a small spot of ink.

That voice... where have I heard it before?

Zhang San.

The lowly coroner who tried to use an abacus to fight the entire Song Dynasty officialdom a thousand years ago.

When did I learn about his story?

Maybe from those old scrolls Su Huai showed me? Maybe... this is the curse of this position?

Every "Exceptional Disposition Specialist" hears the sound of that abacus.

"Chen Mo?" Old Zhao sees something is wrong and leans in. "What’s the matter? Did your blood sugar drop?"

I take a deep breath.

The air is mixed with dust, making my lungs sting.

But I’m awake now.

"I’m not signing."

I throw the pen onto the desk. It bounces once and rolls onto the floor.

"Old Zhao, tell the 'above.' This 'trash'—I’m keeping it."

Old Zhao is stunned. He looks at me as if I’ve gone mad.

"Chen Mo, are you crazy? This is a violation of procedure! Do you know the consequences?"

"I know."

I stand up. My legs are weak, but I remain standing.

"The consequences are score deductions, demotion, even termination."

"But I don’t care anymore."

I point at the avatar on the screen that has already started to turn grey:

"The system says she’s dead? The system says she’s a negative asset?"

"Then I’ll let the system see how a dead person comes back to life."

I pull something from my pocket and slam it onto the desk.

It isn't some high-tech device. 

It’s an "antique" Su Huai gave me yesterday—a **suanpan, the ancient abacus of merchants and mandarins**, its wood chipped and darkened by a thousand years of grime.

"What are you going to do?" Old Zhao stares at the instrument—a pre-modern tool in a room full of silicon. His expression shifts from surprise to a flicker of something that looks like ancient, ancestral fear.

"I'm going to settle an account," I say, my hand gripping the wood. "Since the system uses algorithms to kill, I’ll use the suanpan to save."

A strange icy sensation travels from my fingertips throughout my entire body.

"Since the system uses algorithms to kill, I’ll use an abacus to save."

"I’m going to add a variable to this ledger."

I pull out that thick *Social Security System Operating Manual* from my coat.

It’s a book no one reads. Because it’s too thick, too boring, filled with dense clauses and disclaimers.

But I’ve read it. I’ve read it a hundred times.

I turn to Article 42, Paragraph 3.

I point it out to Old Zhao:

**“任何对系统判定有异议的案例，可申请‘人工复核’。复核期间，原判定冻结，不得执行。”**

Old Zhao freezes for a moment, then laughs:

"Chen Mo, have you lost it? A manual review requires the joint signatures of three senior specialists and must be completed within 72 hours. This girl can't wait 72 hours! Besides, the final result will definitely still be a rejection."

"That's why *she* can't wait."

I point at the screen, my eyes becoming as sharp as a scalpel:

"But the *system* can."

I pull the keyboard over, my fingers flying as I type the command:

**【申请人工复核。理由：系统计算模型存在逻辑悖论。】**

A red dialog box instantly pops up on the screen:

**【系统警告：请输入悖论详情。若判定无效，申请将被驳回，并扣除操作者双倍信用分。】**

"Don't do anything stupid!" Old Zhao tries to reach out and pull my network cable. "Is it worth throwing yourself away for a dead person?"

I block his hand.

"It's worth it."

I type a line of text into the dialog box.

It isn't code; it’s logic.

**“根据QALYs（质量调整生命年）模型，健康人价值 > 病人。但每个人终将生病，生病后价值归零。因此，模型隐含‘所有人最终价值为零’的结论。”**

**“此结论与‘社保系统保障公民生命权’的底层宪法逻辑冲突。”**

**“请系统解释：如果所有人的终局都是零，那么系统的存在意义是什么？”**

Enter.

Send.

Old Zhao’s eyes widen, looking at me like I’m a monster: "You... you're talking philosophy with the system?"

"No," I lean back in my chair, watching the screen, "I'm talking rules to it."

"Since it only recognizes logic, I’ll use its own logic to attack its logic."

The screen goes still.

The red warning line disappears. In its place is a frantically spinning "Processing" icon.

One second. Two seconds. Three seconds.

The sound of the fan in the basement suddenly grows louder. That’s the sound of the servers running at full capacity.

The system is frantically calling upon all its computing power, trying to untie this dead knot.

It must answer this question. Because according to Article 42, it must respond to every review request.

But it can’t answer.

Because it’s a **Möbius strip**.

If it admits the model is correct, it denies its own reason for existence (the constitutional logic).
If it admits the model is wrong, it must overturn all its previous rulings (including Lin Xiaocao’s death sentence).

The red light on the screen begins to flash frantically, as if crying for help.

**【警告：检测到逻辑死循环。算力占用率：99%。】**
**【警告：无法收敛。】**
**【建议：人工介入。】**

"Beep—"

A long drone.

The screen goes black.

Then it lights up again.

That red "Processed/Account Cleared" turns into a yellow question mark.

**状态：待定（系统无法判定）**
**操作：冻结（直至人工复核完成）**

I smile.

I didn't hack into the system.

I just **used the system's own rules to let the system choke itself to death**.

"You..." Old Zhao points at me, his finger trembling, "You crashed the main office's server?"

"No." I pick up that *Mandatory Discharge Confirmation Form* and tear it to shreds. "I just let it 'think' about its life for a little while."

"In this gap, Lin Xiaocao doesn't have to die."

Old Zhao looks at the shreds on the floor, then at the still-spinning yellow question mark.

He suddenly sighs, and for the first time, his eyes don't hold that high-and-mighty pity; instead, they hold a deep fear.

"Chen Mo, you won. You won this round."

"But you have to know—you've enraged it."

Old Zhao points toward the ceiling:

"The Algorithm Optimization Group won't let you go. Your Credit Score might be safe for now, but your name is already on their blacklist."

"It doesn't matter."

I pull that chipped abacus from my pocket and place it on the desk.

"Anyway, my abacus was already missing a corner."

"As long as this one account can be settled, the rest can wait until later."

Old Zhao shakes his head and walks out.

He pauses at the door, looks back at me once:

"By the way, Fu Mu just sent a message. He found the girl's true genetic map."

"What does that mean?"

"Xiaocao isn't an ordinary person—she is **Test Subject Zero**, the **source code provider** for the social security system’s 'Human Optimization Algorithm'."

Old Zhao's voice is soft, yet it echoes in the basement like a thunderclap:

"Chen Mo, you haven't just saved a child."

"You've saved the system's **mother**."

"And its **first victim**."

The door closes.

I look at the girl, who is still eating her candy.

She doesn't know who she is. She doesn't know she nearly died just now.

She only knows that the candy is very sweet.

The digital clock on the wall ticks once.

**`1008`**

The countdown has begun.

I look at the abacus in my hand. In that chipped corner, a faint red light seems to flicker.

Like an eye opening.

**"Hello, Zhang San."**

I say to myself in my heart.

**"The game is on."**

---

I saved a person.

But the price is: although my Credit Score wasn't deducted, I've been targeted by the system. My mortgage next month might truly go into default.

The system didn't crash. It just... **paused for a moment**, then kept on running.

I want to ask you all:

**If saving one person requires destroying your entire family's life, would you save them?**

**Don't be in a hurry to choose. Think about your bank balance first.**
