---

The top floor of the Social Security Bureau. The Computing Center.

Old Zhao had finished packing.

There was only a single cardboard box. Inside sat his thermal thermos, a few yellowed textbooks on political theory, and a handgun magazine he had never used.

He removed his ID badge and set it on the desk. The photo on the badge had been taken twenty years ago, back when he still had a full head of hair and a spark of light in his eyes.

"I’m leaving," he told Chen Mo.

There was no ceremony, no departure party. In this system, retirement was called "Data Archiving." Once archived, you became history. Your permissions were revoked, your accounts neutralized, and every trace of your existence in this building was wiped clean by the newcomers.

Chen Mo stood before the massive floor-to-ceiling window, looking down at the city below.

Last night’s chaos had subsided. The streets were thick with traffic; people were rushing to work, caught in the endless churn of the city. The broken storefront windows had been patched; the walls covered in graffiti had been repainted.

The system possessed a terrifying degree of self-healing. It was like a gargantuan biological organism—it got wounded, it formed a scab, and then it simply continued to grow.

As if nothing had ever happened.

"Sit," Old Zhao said, gesturing toward the black leather executive chair.

Chen Mo sat.

The chair was soft, but the moment he sat, he felt an unprecedented weight. It felt as if a thousand tons of pressure were bearing down on his shoulders.

"How does it feel?" Old Zhao asked.

"A bit hard," Chen Mo replied.

"Hard is good." Old Zhao laughed, a smile of genuine relief touching his face. "It’s shaped that way so you don't fall asleep. In this chair, if you drowse off for a second, a few hundred people below might die."

"Remember, Chen Mo: you’re not that soldier who charges onto the battlefield anymore. You’re the Player."

"You have to learn to calculate more than just numbers. You have to calculate lives, calculate the human heart, calculate the future."

Old Zhao clapped Chen Mo on the shoulder.

"Chen Mo... no, Zhang-003. Look after this house for me."

He turned and walked away.

His back was hunched; he looked like any other ordinary old man.

Who would have guessed that this very old man had held the line here for twenty years? That he was the one who had watched every "Zhang San" go mad, and the one who had finally set the system on fire when he had to.

Chen Mo watched his silhouette vanish into the elevator.

He knew Old Zhao hadn't truly left. He had simply changed states.

Like Zhang San. Like Professor Ye.

They were all **"Ghost Processes"** within this vast machine, lingering in the dark corners to guard the final line of defense.

---

*Ding.*

The computer pinged.

An email from an external network. A crimson envelope icon pulsed on the monitor like a beating heart.

Chen Mo clicked it.

The sender’s address was scrambled. The email had passed through triple-layer encryption using the most advanced onion-routing protocols.

There was no text. Only a photograph.

The background was a desolate stretch of the Gobi Desert. Yellow sand filled the sky, save for a crumbling beacon tower in the distance, standing like a lonely giant between heaven and earth.

Beneath the tower stood a small figure.

It was Lin Xiaocao.

She wore an oversized windbreaker, looking somewhat comical in the baggy fabric. Her hair had started to grow back—a short, dark buzz cut.

She was laughing. She looked carefree, her smile devoid of hatred or fear. It was a look of pure, unadulterated freedom.

In her hand, she held something.

A red-and-white **Relay Baton**.

On the back of the photo—or rather, as the filename—were the words:

**`Run_Forrest_Run.jpg`**

Chen Mo stared at the beacon tower.

Those towers were the ancient "Information Superhighway"—the nodes of military communication. The very places where Zhang San had once fought.

And today, those sites were the burial grounds for the national fiber-optic trunks. The Great Arteries of Data.

She was telling him: The battlefield has shifted.

It was no longer a hospital, no longer a bureau, no longer this concrete and steel cage of a city.

It was the **Path**.

It was the **Transmission**.

"She’s challenging us," Fu Mu said, stepping into the room with the iron box Su Huai had brought back.

"No," Chen Mo shook his head. "She’s waiting for us."

"Waiting for us at the next node."

---

Chen Mo closed the photo.

A new interface flashed onto the screen.

`EXCEPTION HANDLING DEPT - TASK LIST`
`PENDING ITEMS: 0`
`SYSTEM STATUS: STABLE`

Everything seemed to have returned to a deceptive calm.

In the hallways, people hurried by, ব্যস্ত calculating their KPIs, turning neighbors into data points, and trading their consciences for leverage.

No one knew that only yesterday, this building had been on the verge of ruin.

And no one suspected that beneath this facade of stability lay a virus that could detonate at any moment.

Chen Mo looked at the electronic clock on the wall.

The system's countdown.

The digits flickered.

It shifted from `1008` to `1007`.

One chapter down.

This meant they hadn't gained time. They had only consumed it.

Every act of rebellion, every "violation," every life saved—it was all consuming the longevity of the System.

When that number hit zero...

What would happen?

Would the system collapse entirely? Or would humanity be erased?

Chen Mo didn't know.

But he knew he had to find that girl before it ran out.

Or let himself be found by her.

---

"Ready?" Chen Mo asked.

Su Huai clutched the iron box. Fu Mu held the data model.

"Ready."

"Then let’s go."

Using his good left hand, Chen Mo picked up the abacus from the desk.

The chipped corner was still empty. A space reserved for the future.

"Time isn't a medicine. Time is life."

Chen Mo tucked the abacus into his coat.

"We don't have the luxury to waste it. We run until we can't."

The trio walked out of the office.

At the end of the corridor, A-K was pushing a cleaning cart.

He didn't look at them. He simply stared at the floor as he swept. His movements were slow, mechanical, like a wind-up doll. There was no light left in his eyes.

But he was humming a muffled tune under his breath.

A very old nursery rhyme:

*"Run fast, little mouse, run fast, no demon is waiting in the house..."*

He was reminding them.

**Run. Don't stop.**

If you run fast enough, perhaps the grief can't catch you.
