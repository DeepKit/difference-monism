# 第3集：历史的幽灵

> **《未尽之算》·第3集·历史的幽灵**
> **本卷进度：3/24 | 苏怀的论文：退稿 | 系统倒计时：1007集**

Three o'clock in the morning. Jiangcheng University, Old Library, Basement Level Two.

This was the campus's appendix. No air conditioning, no WiFi, only the low hum of exhaust fans and the sour smell of mildew from tens of thousands of rotting old books.

Su Huai sat at a corner desk with a broken leg, a bowl of soggy braised beef noodles in front of her.

"Student Su Huai, your research has depth, but unfortunately, it has no 'IP conversion potential'."

Her supervisor's well-maintained face appeared in her mind, carrying that peculiar, condescending regret:

"The fiscal logic of local administration in the Song Dynasty? This topic is too cold. What do investors like now? They like palace intrigue, they like strong female leads, they like 'feel-good' stories. Who's going to read about these coroners and couriers? Who's going to pay for that?"

Su Huai looked down at the bright red "C-" on her thesis cover.

C-. Just below the passing line.

In the academic system's grading algorithm, her a year's worth of heart-blood—scouring over two thousand historical records to prove that forensic accountability once existed—was worth less than a viral clickbait piece on "Ten Skincare Secrets from the Forbidden City."

"Either change your topic or delay your graduation," her supervisor had given her a final ultimatum. "You're a history student, not a junk collector."

She picked up the fork, wanting to eat the noodles, but they had swollen into a paste.

She looked around. This floor truly was like a junk heap.

Everything stored here was **"feijian" (discarded and dead records)**—materials waiting to be digitized or abandoned because they were too badly damaged.

Republican-era ledgers, Qing Dynasty marriage certificates, and...

Su Huai's gaze fell on a sealed bag by her side.

Inside was a stack of Song Dynasty official document fragments, recently excavated and not yet entered into the system.

Supposedly, they were pulled from the silt of the **Kaifeng Prefecture ruins—the Song Dynasty's equivalent of a central data center**, abandoned when the yellow river buried the secrets of the bureaucracy. The paper had carbonized and turned into crumbs at the slightest touch.

According to the restoration team, these were "trash."

Song Dynasty document management was extremely strict; anything officially archived had to be neatly written and perfectly formatted. These, however, were either drafts with corrections or invalidated receipts without seals.

In other words, this was the wastepaper basket of the Song Dynasty officialdom.

Just like her current self.

"We’re in the same boat, aren't we?"

Su Huai put on white gloves, picked up a pair of tweezers, and carefully peeled back the first page.

It was an *Autopsy Ledger* (《验尸格目》).

A Song Dynasty *wuzuo* (forensic coroner) report.

Usually, these would only contain mundane accounts like "one knife wound under the throat, three inches long." Even a hundred deaths would just be a hundred lines of cold data.

But this page was different.

In the remarks column of this ledger—the place that should have been left blank or simply marked "No abnormalities"—someone had written densely with extremely heavy strokes.

The ink bled through the paper, even puncturing it.

This was a violation. What coroner would dare scrawl all over an official document?

Su Huai leaned in for a closer look.

The handwriting was messy, as if written in extreme anger or fear.

**“此伤非刀斧所致，乃饿殍之口所噬。”**
**“格目无此选项，上官不许录。”**
**“然，人命即人命，不可因无选项而从略。”**
**“今夜再验。若被逐，亦验。”**

Su Huai’s breathing quickened.

She could feel it—even across a thousand years, the writer’s hand was shaking.

He had faced immense pressure: his superior's ban, the rigidness of the ledger, the risk of losing his livelihood.

But he had written it anyway.

In this system where even the dead were formatted, he used the tip of his brush to carve out a sliver of dignity for that nameless deceased.

Su Huai looked at the oil slicks floating in her instant noodle cup, her nose suddenly feeling sore.

Her supervisor said her research had no value.

But at this moment, she felt she had found it.

This unknown *wuzuo*—like a state-sanctioned forensic examiner of the dead, his work strictly cataloged by the throne—the cry he let out a thousand years ago had traversed thick dust, her supervisor's mockery, and her "C-" grade, hitting her, a modern-day failure, with pinpoint accuracy.

Who was he?

Su Huai flipped to the end of the roll, looking for his name.

The signature area had been eaten away by worms, leaving a hole.

Only one vague character remained, looking like a number, or perhaps a code:

**“三”。**

And next to the character "三" was a bright red fingerprint.

It had been left when he pressed his hand down.

Trapped within the fingerprint was a trace of dark red. It wasn't ink.

Su Huai looked through her magnifying glass.

It was blood.

A thousand-year-old bloodstain.

---

Justice then, the lights in the basement **flickered**.

The old exhaust fan above her stopped for half a second, then let out a cough-like roar and started spinning again.

Su Huai froze. She checked her phone.

No signal. For a few brief seconds, even the weak single bar of signal had turned into "No Service."

Although it only lasted a few seconds, she felt a sudden, inexplicable sense of dread. It was as if the pulse of the entire world had skipped a beat.

(She didn't know that only a few kilometers away at the Social Security building, someone had just pulled the lever for that "Moral Meltdown" to save a girl.)

Suddenly, her phone rang.

In this basement level with terrible signal, just as the connection had been restored, the ringtone was jarring—like a clap of thunder.

It was an encrypted number.

Su Huai hesitated for a moment, then picked it up.

"Hello?"

The man's voice on the other end was raspy and exhausted, accompanied by heavy breathing. In the background, there was the sound of wind, as if he were on a rooftop.

"Hello, is this Su Huai, from the History Department of Jiangcheng University?"

"Yes, I am. Who is this?"

"Listen, this minute is very expensive," the man spoke quickly. "My name is Chen Mo. A former specialist from the Social Security Bureau. Right now, I'm the one who 'made the lights flicker'."

Social Security Bureau?

Su Huai's heart tightened. Was there a problem with her medical insurance? Or had the system monitored her for cursing her supervisor online?

"Don't be nervous," the man called Chen Mo seemed to sense her defensiveness. "I'm looking for you because I saw your thesis abstract on the internal network."

"What?"

"《论宋代基层吏治的财政逻辑与人道主义冲突》 (On the Fiscal Logic and Humanitarian Conflict of Grassroots Governance in the Song Dynasty)." Chen Mo read out the title that her supervisor had dismissed as worthless.

"Although your supervisor gave you a low grade, I think you might be the only one who can help me."

Su Huai was stunned.

"Help with what?"

"I have a case," Chen Mo's voice grew low. "The system says this girl should die, but I keep feeling like **this algorithm was already used a thousand years ago**."

"I want to know what the person back then did."

Su Huai looked at the fragments in her hand.

The character "三" seemed to come alive, turning into an eye, coldly watching her.

"Are you talking about... Zhang San?" Su Huai asked, almost as if by instinct.

There was a silence on the other end for five whole seconds.

"You know him?" Chen Mo's voice trembled slightly.

"I'm looking at his diary," Su Huai said. "Or rather, his suicide note."

"What did he write?"

Su Huai took a deep breath and read the words:

**“今夜再验。若被逐，亦验。”**

The sound of Chen Mo's breathing came through the phone. It was heavy.

"Did he win?" Chen Mo asked.

Su Huai flipped to the next page.

There was a new autopsy ledger. It was clean, with only two words written on it: **“病故”** (Died of illness).

The writing was neat, the brush strokes smooth—completely different from the wild scrawl before.

And in the corner of this page was a small annotation:

**“仵作张三，因失智癫狂，扰乱公堂，革职。杖八十。流放岭南。”**

Su Huai’s fingers were icy.

"He didn't," she said. "He lost. He lost miserably."

"He was eliminated by the system."

Chen Mo didn't say anything.

Su Huai could hear a "beeping" sound on the other end, like the alarm of an EKG monitor.

"Su Huai," Chen Mo suddenly spoke, "If I told you I'm doing the same thing he did right now, what would you say?"

"What are you doing?"

"I'm trying to overturn the system's judgment."

"For whom?"

"For a child the system has labeled a 'negative asset'."

Su Huai looked at the blood fingerprint on the parchment.

A thousand years ago, Zhang San had risked his life for a nameless corpse.

A thousand years later, this stranger called Chen Mo was repeating the same mistake for a nameless child.

Was this a coincidence?

No.

Su Huai adjusted her glasses. As a history student, she didn't believe in coincidences.

This was what you call **"History Rhyming"**.

The system keeps upgrading, but its underlying logic has never changed: **Calculate the cost, and eliminate the outliers**.

Zhang San and Chen Mo were those outliers trying to jam the gears.

"I would say," Su Huai gripped her phone, "that you're a fool."

"Just like Zhang San."

Chen Mo laughed. It was a bitter laugh.

"I think I’m a fool, too. But I can't stop now."

"Su Huai, I need your help. I need to know what Zhang San found out back then. Why was the system so determined to let that victim die? Was it just to save money?"

"I don't think it's that simple."

Su Huai looked at the line "乃饿殍之口所噬" (eaten by the mouths of the starving).

It indeed wasn't simple.

If it were just to save money, they would just fill in "Died of illness." Why the flogging? Why the banishment? Why destroy his previous records?

Unless...

He had found something **the system could not afford to acknowledge**.

Something that, if revealed, would cause the entire system to collapse.

"Okay," Su Huai said. "I'll help you find it."

"Not for you, but for my thesis," she lied. "I want to prove that my research has value."

"Thank you."

"By the way," Chen Mo said, "that sentence you read, 'Even if banished, I shall examine,' which page was it on?"

"Page three."

"Then flip back a bit. On page seven, is there any mention of an object?"

"What object?"

"**An abacus**."

Su Huai froze. She quickly flipped to page seven.

It was an "inventory list" of the official property Zhang San had surrendered upon dismissal.

*   One iron ruler.
*   Three silver needles.
*   **One redwood abacus (missing the top-left corner).**

Su Huai gasped.

"Yes," she said. "It's missing a corner."

"That's it then." Chen Mo's voice became exceptionally firm. "Su Huai, take those files. Come to the Social Security Bureau."

"We are performing a relay across a thousand years."

"The account Zhang San couldn't finish settling—it’s our turn now."

Su Huai hung up.

She carefully put the fragile paper back into the sealed bag.

It wasn't trash anymore. It was evidence.

Evidence that within this precisely running, cold-blooded system, there are always fools who try to use their own bones to jam the gears.

Even if it only jams them for a single second, that becomes a crack in history.

Light is what shines in through the cracks.

Su Huai stood up and threw the unfinished cup of noodles into the trash.

"Professor," she said to the empty basement, "I'm not changing my topic."

"I'm going to write the real story."

She grabbed the bag and rushed out of the library.

The sky outside was already brightening.

The morning light was faint, shining on the tree-lined path of Jiangcheng University.

Su Huai ran fast. The wind blew her bangs aside, revealing a pair of eyes that were bloodshot from all-nighters but exceptionally bright.

She was going to the Social Security Bureau.

She was going to see that fool called Chen Mo.

And in the shadows behind her, the university's old librarian (Professor Ye) stood by the window, watching her receding back.

Professor Ye held a cloth and was wiping an old umbrella hanging on the wall.

It was a red oil-paper umbrella.

On its surface, a few characters were faintly visible, eroded by time.

**“未尽之算。”**

Professor Ye sighed.

"It started again," he muttered to himself.

He pulled out his phone and dialed a number.

"Hello, Old Zhao."

"She’s set out."

"Yeah. With that file."

"You can't stop them. Just like you couldn't stop us back then."

Professor Ye hung up, looking at the red umbrella in his hand, his eyes clouded.

"Good luck, children."

"Don't die too quickly."
