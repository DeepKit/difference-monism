# 第22集：导师的凝视

> **《未尽之算》·第22集·导师的凝视**

Jiangcheng University. Department of History Office.

This was the quietest corner of the campus. Virginia creeper choked the red brick walls, and the window panes were frosted with a decade of dust. Sunlight filtered through the trees, casting skeletal shadows across the corridor.

Su Huai stood before the door to Room 302, clutching a printed withdrawal application.

The paper was already crumpled from the moisture of her palms.

She hesitated for a long time before finally raising a hand to knock on the peeling wooden door.

"Enter."

A thin, ancient voice called from within.

Su Huai pushed the door open.

The office was thick with the scent of old paper, mildewed books, and the acrid tang of cheap tobacco. It was the scent of time itself.

Professor Ye sat behind his desk, his back to her, wiping an object with a soft cloth.

It was a red paper umbrella.

The surface was aged and dull, with faint, charred marks along the edges. But in Professor Ye’s hands, it was treated like a sacred relic. He wiped it slowly, meticulously, as if scouring a buried memory.

"You're here."

Professor Ye didn't turn around. His voice was calm, as if he had anticipated this moment for years.

"Professor," Su Huai took a sharp breath and laid the application on the desk. "I'm here to drop out."

"I know."

Professor Ye set the cloth down and turned.

His eyes remained clouded, like two stagnant wells. But somehow, Su Huai felt that today he looked different.

The habitual sarcasm—the pedantic air of the scholar—was gone. In its place was a deep, marrow-deep exhaustion and a strange sense of... pity.

"Where did the umbrella come from?" Su Huai asked, her eyes fixed on the red paper.

She had seen this umbrella in the fragments of Zhang San’s ledgers. It was the one used for the "Bone Steaming" incident—the evidence he had bought with his life.

"An old friend gave it to me," Professor Ye said, shifting his crippled leg. "His name was Zhang-002."

Su Huai’s stomach dropped.

She remembered the old photograph she’d seen in the archives. A young Professor Ye, standing behind a vibrant Old Zhao.

"You're... an accomplice to the system?"

Her voice trembled.

The mentor she had revered—the man who taught her that "History must have a pulse"—was he a watchdog for the machine?

"An accomplice?" Professor Ye gave a thin, desolate laugh. "Perhaps."

"Why?"

Su Huai slammed the withdrawal form onto the desk, kicking up a cloud of dust.

"You knew the truth. Why did you let me investigate? Why did you watch me slam my head against a brick wall like a fool?"

"You knew exactly how Zhang San ended. Why let me dig up his grave?"

"Were you just watching the show? Did you find it entertaining to watch your students descend into madness?"

Professor Ye looked at her.

There was no anger in his gaze. Only the hollow clarity of a man who had seen too much.

"Because some walls are only hit by fools."

He gestured toward the campus outside the window.

"The smart ones? They're all kneeling at the base of the wall."

---

Professor Ye stood and hobbled to the window.

"Su Huai, do you know what the primary duty of an historian is?"

"To record the truth," she said without hesitation.

"No. It is to **Endure the truth**."

He watched the young students below. They were full of life, utterly ignorant of the clockwork beneath their feet.

"Thirty years ago, I was like you. Full of fire, desperate to change the world. Old Zhao—Little Zhao back then—and I found the flaw in the system."

"We tried to patch it."

"And?" Su Huai asked.

"And Zhao signed the paperwork to save my life. He became the gatekeeper. And me?"

He patted his mangled leg.

"I was broken and cast out. The system gave me the title of 'Observer.' It allowed me to live, so long as I watched generation after generation repeat the same mistakes."

"It’s a fate worse than death."

"I watched you dig through the archives. I watched you cry. I watched you surrender to despair. I wanted to stop you a thousand times, but I couldn't."

"Because the system requires Variables. If I stopped you, you wouldn't be a Variable anymore."

"You used me as cannon fodder?" Su Huai gritted her teeth.

"No. I used you to keep the thread alive."

Professor Ye turned back and pulled a rusted iron box from his drawer.

"Every generation of 'Zhang San' has a 'Su Huai.' Someone to record their story. Someone to pass the spark (火种) along."

"I was Zhao’s recorder. Now, it is your turn."

He pushed the iron box toward her.

"This is the 'Key' you’re looking for."

Su Huai froze.

"A-K had the key."

"A-K had the 'Key to the Lock.' What I have is the **'Key to the Path.'**"

Professor Ye opened the box.

Inside was a yellowed parchment map.

It traced a winding route starting from Kaifeng and extending deep into the desert wastes of the Northwest.

The path was marked with red dots. Beside each dot was a name:

**Jiming Post.**
**Tumu Fortress.**
**Jiayu Pass.**

"What is this?" Su Huai asked.

"These are the **Veins** of the system," Professor Ye’s gaze turned sharp. "The System isn't just in the Bureau. It’s in every river, every post station, every grain of rice."

"The data in this box points to the next battlefield."

"The Northwest."

"It is an information desert. A breeding ground for rumors."

"That girl—Subject Zero—has escaped, but she will head there."

"Because the Northwest is the System’s blind spot."

---

Su Huai stared at the map.

She understood now.

Professor Ye hadn't given up. Over the last thirty years, while he hid in his study with his broken leg, he had been mapping the System’s weaknesses. He had been drawing this map.

He was waiting.

Waiting for a fool like her to take up the mantle.

"Go," Professor Ye said. "Take Chen Mo with you. Don't let him die halfway there."

Su Huai lifted the iron box.

It was heavy. It felt as if she were carrying the weight of history itself.

She reached the door and stopped.

"Professor... what if I never come back?"

Professor Ye picked up the cloth again and resumed wiping the red umbrella. His movements were tender, almost gentle.

"Then die there. Don't come back."

"Even if you turn to ash, make sure you are ash that burns the hands of those who touch it."

Su Huai’s eyes blurred.

She heard him.

It was the only blessing this cold, broken old man could offer.

*Don't come back. Don't become a coward like me.*

"Thank you, Professor."

She gave a deep bow.

Then she sprinted out of the office.

---

After she left, Professor Ye opened the red umbrella.

The sunlight filtered through the red paper, casting a crimson glow onto his wrinkled face.

It was the color of blood.

He looked at the empty room, perhaps seeing himself thirty years ago, and a young Little Zhao full of life.

"Zhao," he whispered to the silence. "Can we win this time?"

There was no reply.

Only the rhythmic, mechanical ticking of the clock on the wall.

**"There are no innocent bystanders in this world."**

**"If you are watching, you are a co-conspirator."**

Professor Ye closed his eyes and buried his face against the red paper of the umbrella.

Finally, he could rest.
