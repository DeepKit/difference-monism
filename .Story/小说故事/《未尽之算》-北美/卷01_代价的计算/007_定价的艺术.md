The Social Security Bureau basement.

Chen Mo stared at the green progress bar on the screen, crawling forward at an agonizing pace.

**Data Recovery: 15%**

It was too slow. Every 1% advance required massive computing power to decrypt sectors that had been overwritten layer upon layer.

It was exactly like Zhang San digging through bone ash.

Chen Mo felt as if two souls lived within his body. One was striking the keyboard; the other was swinging an iron shovel.

"Chen Mo," Fu Mu pushed the door open, holding a freshly printed document. "Old Zhao’s second wave has arrived."

"What is it?"

"Money."

Fu Mu tossed the document onto the desk.

**《关于对0号样本（林小草）的人道主义补偿协议》**
**"Humanitarian Compensation Agreement for Subject Zero (Lin Xiaocao)"**

Chen Mo picked it up.

It was a perfect commercial contract.

*   **Party B (Guardian/Agent for Lin Xiaocao)**: Voluntarily waives all rights to treatment and agrees to cease all litigation against the Social Security Bureau.
*   **Party A (Social Security Bureau)**: Provides a one-time humanitarian subsidy in the amount of **850,000 CNY (approximately $120,000)**.

"850,000," Chen Mo smiled. "Exactly the amount of her treatment."

"No, it’s not just the treatment fee." Fu Mu pointed to the fine print at the bottom. "If Lin Xiaocao dies, this money will automatically be transferred to the 'Lin Family Trust'—a fund the system just authorized today. It’s for her distant relatives, people currently living below the poverty line."

"In other words," Fu Mu looked at Chen Mo, "if she lives, the money goes to the hospital, she suffers through agony, and she likely dies anyway. But if she 'dies' now—if you sign away her rights—this money becomes her family's 'first pot of gold.'"

"It’s enough for her cousin to marry, her older sister to go to college, and for her entire extended family to escape poverty."

Chen Mo’s finger traced the numbers.

850,000.

In the system's eyes, this was the "write-off cost" for a bad debt.
But to a normal person, it was a life-changing fortune.

The system wasn't just killing her; it was murdering her soul.

It placed "life" and "wealth" on opposite ends of a scale and forced the victim to choose.

"If you were in her place, how would you choose?" Fu Mu asked.

Chen Mo didn't answer.

Because a vision was appearing before his eyes once again.

---

The Song Dynasty. Kaifeng Prefecture.

Zhang San was kneeling in the snow, staring at the jar of freshly excavated bone ash.

"Zhang San," a middle-aged man dressed like a head butler approached. He wore a silk robe and leather boots—the picture of a respectable man from a wealthy household.

"I am Head Butler Wang from the Grand Imperial Tutor’s estate."

The man looked down at Zhang San with an air of charitable arrogance.

"I hear you’re looking into the matter of that maidservant?"

"Yes." Zhang San clutched the ash jar tighter.

"Stop." Butler Wang pulled a ledger from his sleeve and dropped it in front of Zhang San. "Look. This is her market value (身价)."

Zhang San opened the ledger.

On it was written the girl’s name: **Xiao Cui**.

Followed by a string of cold, hard numbers:

*   **Year 1 of the Xi Ning Era**: Entered the estate. Market value: **5 taels of silver** (Life Indenture).
*   **Year 2**: Broke a glazed vessel. Deducted **2 taels**.
*   **Year 3, Winter**: Sudden death.

"Do you see?" Butler Wang held up five fingers. "When she was alive, she was only worth five taels. And those five taels? Her parents used them long ago to pay off gambling debts."

"She worked in the estate for a year, and the things she broke cost more than she did."

Butler Wang smiled and pulled a silver voucher from his robe.

"But the Grand Tutor is merciful. As long as you hand over that jar of ash and mark the ledger as 'Natural Causes,' this **fifty-tael voucher** is yours."

Fifty taels.

Zhang San looked at the paper.

In the Great Song, an acre of prime farmland cost only three taels. Fifty taels could buy sixteen acres.

It was enough for his blind mother to eat white wheat buns for the rest of her life.

And it was enough for Xiao Cui’s parents to sell her ten times over.

"Butler Wang," Zhang San’s voice trembled. "Are you saying she is worth more dead than alive?"

"Of course," the Butler said as if explaining a basic law of nature. "Alive, she’s a loss-leader. Dead, she’s a money tree. Isn't this her 'Blessing' (福报)?"

"Her blessing?"

Zhang San felt a wave of nausea.

The snowy field seemed to turn golden before his eyes. The snowflakes drifting in the air weren't snow—they were bits of copper powder.

He saw Xiao Cui’s face in the firelight. She opened her mouth to speak, but instead of a voice, copper coins spilled out.

*Clang, clang.*
*Clink, clink.*

The sound of coins hitting the ground was deafening.

It drowned out her screams; it drowned out the sound of her heart vessels shattering.

Butler Wang’s voice grew distant, then close.

"Too little? Fine, then I'll add another ten taels. Sixty taels for a maid’s life. That is a sky-high price, Zhang San. Don't be ungrateful."

---

"Chen Mo? Chen Mo?"

Fu Mu’s voice pulled him back to reality.

"What’s wrong? You look terrible."

Chen Mo covered his mouth and began to cough violently.

*Hack, wheeze...*

There was something sticky in his palm. Phlegm, with the metallic scent of rust.

He wanted a drink of water to wash it down, but his hand stopped as he reached for the vending machine. His account was frozen. He didn't even have the right to buy a two-yuan bottle of water.

And yet, 850,000 yuan sat right in front of him.

"I’m fine." Chen Mo swallowed the bloody phlegm, his throat feeling like it had been scrubbed with sandpaper. "I just feel... sick."

He looked at the "850,000 Agreement."

On his left was the ancient fifty-tael voucher.
On his right was the modern 850,000 CNY check.

The two pieces of paper, separated by a millennium, stacked perfectly on top of each other.

**The formula hadn't changed.**
**The algorithm hadn't changed.**
**Even the stench of money—the smell of selling human lives like pork—hadn't changed.**

The system had evolved for a thousand years, and its only progress was learning to use the word "Humanitarian" to wrap up its transactions.

"I’m not signing," Chen Mo pushed the agreement back.

"Are you sure?" Fu Mu pushed his glasses up. "If you don't sign, Lin Xiaocao’s distant relatives might come looking for trouble. They’ll say you’re cutting off their livelihood."

"People might even curse you. They’ll say you’re using your 'sense of justice' to rob a poor family of their one chance to escape poverty."

"Then let them curse."

Chen Mo stood up and walked to the computer.

"Fu Mu, do you know why the system dares to set a price like this?"

"Because in its eyes, human life is a **commodity**."

"A commodity that can be depreciated, used up, or liquidated for a premium. To them, Xiao Cui’s death and Lin Xiaocao’s death are just successful 'Asset Restructurings' (资产重组)."

Chen Mo’s finger struck the keyboard hard.

"But I’m going to tell the system something."

"**Life is not a commodity. Life is priceless.**"

"And 'priceless' doesn't mean it's worth a lot. It means **it cannot be priced**."

"The moment you set a price on your conscience, even if it's a fortune, you have already lost."

Chen Mo typed a line of code.

**`Instruction: Reject Agreement. Reason: Subject (Life) is non-tradable.`**

Then, he turned to the slowly crawling progress bar.

"Fu Mu, help me."

"With what?"

"I need more speed."

"Speed for the data steaming?" Fu Mu frowned. "That requires more computing power. We only have these few scrap servers; they’re already at max capacity."

"Borrow it," Chen Mo said.

"Borrow it? From whom?"

"From the whole city."

Chen Mo pulled the suanpan from his pocket.

"The system wants to use money to buy people’s hearts? Fine. Then we’ll use those hearts to fight the money."

"Su Huai," Chen Mo called her. "Publish the story."

"Which story?"

"Zhang San’s story. The story of fifty taels of silver."

"Publish it where?"

"Send it to every phone in the city. To every ordinary person who is struggling for their mortgage, their medical bills, and those few bits of silver."

"Tell them: **'If you sell your conscience today for fifty taels, tomorrow the system will sell your life for five hundred.'**"

Ten minutes later.

An article titled *"Fifty Taels for a Life—Would You Sell?"* began to spread across social networks like a virus.

There were no flowery words, only the bloody experience of Zhang San.

*   *"Alive, she’s a loss-leader. Dead, she’s a money tree."*
*   *"Isn't this her 'Blessing'?"*

Those words were like a whip, lashing across the face of every modern citizen.

Anger.

An anger that had been suppressed for a long time was ignited in every corner of the city.

People began to share. They began to comment.

Every share, every click, coalesced into a tiny stream of computing power.

Chen Mo opened a specialized interface: a **Crowdsourced Computing Platform**.

He broke the "Data Steaming" task into millions of microscopic calculation packets, hidden within the background code of the article.

As long as someone opened the article, their phone automatically contributed a tiny bit of idle computing power to help Chen Mo decrypt the hard drive.

The progress bar began to accelerate.

**15%... 20%... 35%...**

It was **"Computing Crowdfunding."**

On the top floor, Old Zhao watched the monitoring data, his face turning an ashen grey.

"What is he doing?"

"He’s using every phone in the city for mining!" the technician shouted in terror. "No—not mining for coins! He’s mining for graves! He’s unearthing the data we deleted!"

"Cut it off!" Old Zhao roared. "Kill the article link!"

"We can't! It’s decentralized! Every person is a node!"

Old Zhao stared at the rapidly growing percentage on the screen, feeling a fear he had never known before.

He thought money could buy everything.

But he had forgotten that some things cannot be bought.

The thing called **Resonance**.

The thing called **Anger**.

And the **Desire to live like a human being**.

The Ancient Past.

Zhang San threw the fifty-tael silver voucher into the brazier.

The flames instantly swallowed it. Blue tongues of fire flickered, seemingly mocking Butler Wang’s distorted face.

"Are you mad?!" Butler Wang screamed. "That was fifty taels!"

"Yes, fifty taels."

Zhang San clapped the ash from his hands.

"Enough to buy sixteen acres of land. Enough to buy my worthless life ten times over."

"But it can't buy me a good night's sleep."

Zhang San picked up the jar of bone ash and turned to walk into the wind and snow.

"Go back and tell the Grand Tutor: I will steam these bones."

"Even if I cannot steam out the grievance, I will steam out justice."
