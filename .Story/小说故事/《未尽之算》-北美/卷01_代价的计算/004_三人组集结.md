# 第4集：三人组集结

> **《未尽之算》·第4集·三人组集结**

"Old Wang’s Hotpot," right across from the Social Security Bureau.

This was the place Chen Mo picked. He said the mutton here was sliced thick—honest portions for honest work.

But things got awkward the moment he walked in. Chen Mo tried to buy a pack of cigarettes, but his mobile payment failed.

**【账户冻结。信用分不足。】**
**[Account Frozen. Insufficient Credit Score.]**

The red notification box felt like a slap across his face.

Thankfully, Fu Mu arrived just in time.

"I’ve got it."

Fu Mu pulled out a sleek black credit card and gave it a light tap. *Beep.* Transaction successful.

"This meal is on me," Fu Mu said flatly. "Consider it a final bit of humanitarian care for an 'endangered species.'"

Su Huai walked into the private booth, clutching the sealed bag containing Zhang San’s ancient fragments. Seeing this exchange, her gaze became complicated.

The booth was thick with steam and tobacco smoke.

Chen Mo had already taken a seat. He was wearing that same rumpled jacket, busily tossing mutton into the boiling pot. Despite having no money to pay the bill, he ate with the entitlement of a man who owned the place.

Sitting next to him was Fu Mu, the man who had just paid. He wore gold-rimmed glasses and a sharp, tailored suit. He held a tablet in his hand, staring at the plate of raw mutton as if he were calculating the caloric density of every single slice.

"You’re here," Chen Mo beckoned to Su Huai. "Sit. This is Fu Mu. My college classmate, now a 'top pen' at the Policy Research Office. He’s also our financier for the day."

"Hello," Fu Mu looked up, nodding with a polite but distant chill. "I’ve read your thesis. The angle is interesting, but your data models are completely flawed."

Su Huai froze, about to snap back, but Chen Mo dumped a plate of tripe into the pot, cutting her off.

"Enough. Today, we aren't talking academics. We're talking lives."

Chen Mo placed the chipped suanpan in the center of the table.

"Su Huai brought history. Fu Mu brought theory. I brought the suanpan."

"The three of us are going to settle an account today."

---

"If you want to save that girl, someone has to die."

Fu Mu set his tablet down, pointing his chopsticks at the roiling red broth.

"That isn't a threat; it’s math. The logic of the social security system is a zero-sum game. On one side, you have the 850,000 yuan treatment fee; on the other, the system’s budget red line. If one side goes up, the other must be crushed down."

He turned, his gaze sweeping over Chen Mo and Su Huai.

"Who’s going to be the one crushed?"

"Old Zhao told me that if the treatment is forced through, the funds will be deducted from the **Department of Exceptional Disposition’s** annual budget. That means, Chen Mo, your department might be disbanded. Your assistant, A-K, will lose his job. You will be buried under a mountain of debt."

"In fact, if the Algorithm Group pursues responsibility for the **Moral Circuit Breaker**, you could end up in prison."

Fu Mu pushed his glasses up, the lenses fogging over from the steam.

"According to Song Dynasty law, if a *wuzuo* falsified an autopsy report, he was sentenced to three thousand *li* of exile. Chen Mo, are you ready for your own exile?"

Su Huai slammed the roll of ancient paper onto the table, making the wine glasses jump.

"This isn't falsifying! This is error correction! If the rules themselves are wrong, then following them is the real crime!"

"Error correction?" Fu Mu laughed—the kind of laugh an adult gives a child playing with matches. "Miss Su, in a power structure, there is no 'right or wrong.' There is only 'compliance' and 'violation.'"

"The system doesn't care about the truth. The system only cares about stability."

"If you destroy the stability of the rules just to save one person, that is the ultimate evil."

Su Huai’s face flushed with anger.

"That’s Social Darwinism! By your logic, Zhang San shouldn't have examined that body back then? We should just watch that girl die?"

"From the perspective of species optimization? Yes," Fu Mu said coldly. "The girl carries a defective gene. Saving her is like pouring poison into the human gene pool."

---

The air in the booth turned to ice.

The hotpot kept bubbling and popping, but no one moved their chopsticks.

On one side was Su Huai, clutching a thousand-year-old conscience; on the other was Fu Mu, his mind filled with the cold calculations of modern political science.

And caught in the middle was Chen Mo, with a death notice in his pocket that was only valid for seven more days.

"You’re right, Fu Mu," Chen Mo finally spoke.

He fished out a piece of mutton and placed it in Fu Mu’s bowl.

"The system truly only cares about stability. But, Fu Mu, you missed one thing."

"What’s that?"

"**Uncertainty**."

Chen Mo pointed at the suanpan.

"The system thinks it has calculated everything. But it cannot calculate the variables of the human heart."

"Just like Zhang San. Logically, as a lowly *wuzuo*, he never should have dared to offend the powerful. But why did he perform the autopsy anyway?"

"Because he was afraid of ghosts."

"What?" Fu Mu frowned.

"He was afraid that girl’s restless soul would come looking for him in the middle of the night," Chen Mo smiled faintly. "And so am I."

"I’m afraid that if I don't save her, I’ll dream of that piece of candy she gave me every night for the rest of my life."

"That fear is also a cost. And it is an **infinite cost**."

Chen Mo looked at Fu Mu.

"Fu Mu, have you forgotten about your sister?"

Fu Mu’s hand jerked. His chopsticks clattered onto the table.

That was his Achilles' heel.

Ten years ago, Fu Mu's sister died of leukemia. At the time, due to certain healthcare policy restrictions, she wasn't allowed access to the latest imported drugs.

The only reason Fu Mu had fought so hard to get into the Policy Research Office, to study those ice-cold regulations, was to find out one thing: **Who really killed his sister?**

Was it the doctor? Was it the official? Or was it the invisible system?

"I haven't forgotten," Fu Mu’s voice dropped low. "That’s how I know how foolish it is for an individual to fight the system."

"Then are you going to watch the tragedy repeat itself?" Su Huai asked.

Fu Mu fell silent.

He took off his glasses and rubbed the bridge of his nose.

Without the barrier of the lenses, his eyes revealed a deep, soul-crushing exhaustion.

"You guys are insane."

He let out a long sigh.

Then, he picked up his tablet and opened a complex mathematical model.

"If we’re going to save her, brute force won't work."

"We need a change of strategy."

Fu Mu’s finger swiped across the screen.

"The core logic of the system is 'Cost-Benefit.' As long as we can prove that **the cost of 'not saving her' is greater than the cost of 'saving her'**, the system will automatically choose to save her."

"How do we prove that?" Chen Mo asked.

"**By creating a much larger problem**."

A flicker of cunning glinted in Fu Mu’s eyes.

"What if the girl’s death triggers a massive PR tsunami? What if her death causes the Social Security Bureau’s public trust rating to drop by 5%?"

"A 5% drop in trust means the social security contribution rate for next year will plummet. In terms of money, we’re talking billions."

"Billions versus 850,000 yuan."

"Which one will the system choose?"

---

Chen Mo and Su Huai shared a look.

"You mean..."

"I mean, we make this **loud**," Fu Mu put his glasses back on, returning to his cold, elite persona. "We package that girl into an 'untouchable totem.'"

"We make sure everyone knows that if she dies, it is because of the Social Security Bureau’s incompetence and cold-bloodedness."

"It's called **'Asymmetric Warfare.'**"

Su Huai hesitated. "But... using that girl for hype... is that ethical?"

"Ethics?" Fu Mu sneered. "Miss Su, if you want to save lives, don't talk to me about ethics. Didn't Zhang San lie to perform his autopsy?"

"He lied when he wrote 'Even if banished, I shall examine.' He was gambling. He was betting that his superiors wouldn't dare take the heat for 'covering up the truth.'"

"We are gambling, too."

"We’re betting that **the system is more afraid of trouble than it is of spending money**."

Just then, the booth door was pushed open.

A young man wearing a convenience store uniform and a baseball cap walked in.

He was carrying a crate of beer.

It was A-K.

"Boss, soup's getting low."

A-K skillfully topped up the hotpot broth, then glanced at the suanpan on the table.

His gaze lingered on the chipped corner for a second.

"Nice suanpan," A-K said. "Too bad it’s missing a corner. Won't calculate right."

"Better that it doesn't," Chen Mo said. "If it calculated 'right,' the person would be dead."

A-K grinned, revealing a set of yellowed teeth.

"True enough. Just like my street sweeper. The manual says it can sweep away all the dust in the world. But I know there are some spots of grime it can never touch."

"Why?" Su Huai asked.

"Because that grime is **inside the human heart**."

A-K pointed to his own chest.

"The system can sweep the floor clean, but it can never sweep away the greed and fear inside people."

"If you want that cleaned, you have to do it yourself."

With that, A-K set down a bottle of beer and walked out.

"Who was that?" Fu Mu watched A-K leave, frowning. "He seemed... like he understood exactly what we were talking about."

"That’s A-K. The 'Sweeper Monk' of the Social Security Bureau," Chen Mo opened the beer and filled everyone’s glasses. "Don't underestimate him. Sometimes, the guy sweeping the floor sees things more clearly than the guys sitting in the offices."

Chen Mo raised his glass.

"To Zhang San."

Su Huai raised her glass: "To the girl."

Fu Mu hesitated, then raised his own: "To... Probability Theory."

*Clink.*

The three glasses met.

In that crisp sound of impact, a mad plan was born.

They didn't know that this hotpot meal would be the beginning of countless life-and-death struggles they would face together.

And they didn't know that from a dark corner of the city, a pair of eyes was already watching them.
