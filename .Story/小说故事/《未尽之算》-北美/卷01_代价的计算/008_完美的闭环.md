Kaifeng Prefecture. The Steaming Room (蒸骨房).

This was a small courtyard nestled in the most isolated corner of the government compound. No one ever came here; only a few stray cats darted across the walls.

But today, the air inside was thick and hot, like a furnace.

Zhang San was shirtless, pouring aged vinegar into the brick-lined pit.

*Sizzle—*

The vinegar hit the glowing charcoal, instantly sending up a cloud of white steam. The sharp acidity mixed with the scent of burning wood, making Zhang San’s eyes sting and water.

"Zhang San, are you really going through with this?"

The old clerk who guarded the room stood in the doorway, looking at him as if he were a ghost.

"According to the code, one can only apply for 'Bone Steaming' in major cases where there is 'No Body to Examine' and 'Overflowing Suspicions.' This girl has already been burned to ash—there’s nothing left but a few scraps of charred bone. And you still want to steam them?"

The old man pointed to the jar of remains on the ground.

"If you find nothing, you'll be charged with 'Reciprocal Punishment for False Accusation' (诬告反坐) and exiled three thousand *li*. Is it worth it for a dead girl?"

Zhang San said nothing.

He simply poured the contents of the jar onto a prepared bamboo mat.

Several blackened shards of chest bone. They looked ordinary, no different from decaying wood found on a roadside.

"It is worth it," Zhang San said. "I just want to see **which is harder: her bones, or the laws of the Great Song.**"

---

Bone Steaming (蒸骨) was the pinnacle of Song Dynasty forensic science—the final trump card.

The principle was actually remarkably scientific: use a red oil-paper umbrella to filter sunlight, leaving only the red spectrum. Red light can penetrate bone matter, illuminating "blood shadows" (血荫) hidden deep within the marrow.

If the deceased had suffered a fracture or internal injury while alive, blood would be trapped within the bone. Even if the flesh rotted, even if the body was burned—as long as the bone remained, the blood remained.

But the execution was brutally difficult.

One had to first heat the charcoal in the pit, douse it with vinegar, and steam the bones for four hours to loosen the structure.

Then, under the blazing midday sun, open the red umbrella.

If even the smallest trace of blood remained, it would glow like a ruby under the red light.

It was the deceased’s final statement.

Zhang San wiped the sweat from his brow.

"The hour has arrived."

He glanced at the sky. High noon. The sun hung overhead like a relentless eye.

He opened the specially-made red oil-paper umbrella. The paper had been soaked in tung oil until it was as red as fresh blood.

Holding the umbrella over his head, he carefully lifted the steamed fragments of chest bone from the pit.

The sunlight filtered through the red umbrella, splashing across the bone.

The old clerk held his breath.

Zhang San held his breath.

Even the stray cats on the wall seemed to hold theirs.

One second. Two seconds.

Nothing happened.

The bone was still a dull, blackened mess, like a dead stone.

Zhang San’s heart sank.

Had the blood really dried up during the fire? Or had that strike missed the chest bone entirely?

Just as he was about to give up, the center of the bone suddenly flickered.

Like a heart beating once more amidst the dead ash.

A pinpoint of crimson slowly seeped out. Brighter and more vivid with every passing second.

It was a clear palm print.

Five fingers, distinct and sharp. Seared deep into the bone.

"Do you see it?" Zhang San’s hands were shaking; his voice was trembling. "Do you see it?!"

The old clerk scrambled backward in terror, collapsing to the ground.

"It... it's really there! A wound!"

"This is the proof!" Zhang San raised the bone high over his head, roaring at the sun. "Here is the evidence you wanted! Registrar Zhou! Do you see it clearly now?!"

But then, the red light on the bone suddenly dimmed.

It wasn't a cloud blocking the sun.

The bone itself had suddenly fractured.

*Crack.*

A brittle, snapping sound.

The fragment of chest bone, which had just revealed the palm print, crumbled into fine powder right there in the red light. It was like a dry biscuit.

Zhang San froze.

The bone dust slipped through his fingers, caught by a sudden gust of wind and scattered into nothingness.

"What happened?" He dropped to his knees, desperately trying to catch the remains. "Why did it break?!"

The old clerk stammered, his voice filled with ancient fear.

"Zhang San... you forgot. There is another line in the *Xi Yuan Ji Lu*."

**“骨经火焚，性脆如灰。蒸之虽显伤，然触之即碎。”**
**[Bone passed through fire is as brittle as ash. Steaming will reveal the wound, but it crumbles upon the slightest touch.]**

"The evidence... it was **one-time use**."

Zhang San stared blankly at his empty palms.

The evidence was gone.

He had personally turned the only proof into flying ash.

This was the system's perfect loop.

It permitted you to discover the truth, but it forbade you from keeping it.

The truth, like that bone, died the moment it saw the light.

Modern Day. Social Security Bureau Basement.

"The data is gone."

Fu Mu watched the progress bar on the screen suddenly turn blood-red. His face was pale.

"What do you mean?" Chen Mo asked.

"The data steaming was a success. We recovered Lin Xiaocao’s original files." Fu Mu pointed to the screen. "Look. This is a deleted log entry."

On the screen was a single line of code:

`[LOG]: Subject Zero (Lin Xiaocao). Genetic sequence anomaly. Suggest clearance.`

"Isn't that the evidence?" Chen Mo asked. "It proves Old Zhao was lying. She was deleted on purpose."

"It’s evidence," Fu Mu said with a bitter laugh. "But look at the next line."

`[SYSTEM]: Self-destruct sequence initiated after data read. Execution complete.`

The screen flickered.

The code vanished, replaced by a wall of gibberish.

"It’s **'Quantum-State Data,'**" Fu Mu explained. "It’s a high-level encryption technique used by the Algorithm Optimization Group. Like Schrödinger's Cat. If you don't look at it, it’s there. But the moment you observe it—recover it—it collapses and disappears."

"It was one-time evidence."

Chen Mo collapsed back into his chair.

A thousand years ago, the bone in Zhang San’s hand had crumbled.
A thousand years later, the data in Chen Mo’s hand had been destroyed.

Both had seen the truth. Yet neither could prove it.

This was what you called the **"Perfect Loop."**

The system allowed you to peep at its secrets as a reward for your intelligence. But it would never allow you to take those secrets out into the world.

"We lost." Fu Mu closed his laptop.

"No."

Su Huai, who had been silent until now, suddenly spoke.

She pointed to a screenshot on the screen. She had been fast enough to capture it a split second before the data vanished.

Though blurred, a pattern was visible.

It was a specialized marker hidden within Lin Xiaocao’s genetic map.

The marker wasn't helical.

It was a rectangle.

With a piece missing from the top-right corner.

"Look," Su Huai’s voice was trembling. "This shape... doesn't it look like... that suanpan?"

Chen Mo whipped the suanpan from his pocket.

Comparison.

It was a perfect match.

The jagged edge of the chip in the wood matched the gap in the genetic map perfectly.

"This isn't a coincidence," Chen Mo’s gaze changed.

"It’s not a genetic defect. It’s a **Lock**."

"Someone put a lock on Lin Xiaocao’s genes. And the shape of that lock... is this suanpan."

"Who put it there?" Fu Mu asked.

"Zhang San."

Chen Mo looked at the suanpan.

"A thousand years ago, Zhang San might have lost the bone, but he didn't lose his fight."

"He carved the shape of that palm print into his suanpan. Or rather, he hid the secret inside it."

"He knew he couldn't protect the bone. So he created this suanpan as a **'Backup.'**"

"This suanpan is the **Key** to unlocking Lin Xiaocao’s genetic sequence."

"If this is the key," Chen Mo placed the suanpan on the table, "then where is the lock?"

"Inside Lin Xiaocao," Su Huai said. "Or rather, in her blood."

"We need her blood."

The three of them turned simultaneously toward the corner where Lin Xiaocao lay.

She had fallen asleep, still clutching that candy wrapper.

"Draw it," Chen Mo said.

"Here?" Fu Mu asked. "We don't have the equipment."

"We don't need equipment." Chen Mo pulled a small pin from his desk—the kind used to pop a SIM card tray.

"What did Zhang San use back then?"

"The red umbrella," Su Huai said.

"Fine. We’ll use our own version."

Chen Mo picked up the suanpan. His fingers brushed over the chipped corner.

"This isn't ordinary wood," Chen Mo whispered. "It’s a **'Carbon-Based Storage Medium.'**"

"What?" Fu Mu was stunned.

"In college, I read a paper on prehistoric bio-computing. This wood is 'Lei Ji Mu'—lightning-struck wood. When treated with specialized carbonization, it can form transistor-like structures at the molecular level. It requires a specific bio-enzyme as a catalyst to become conductive."

"And that bio-enzyme..." Chen Mo looked at Lin Xiaocao. "It’s in Subject Zero’s blood."

This meant Zhang San hadn't just been calculating accounts. He had been **programming**.

He had used the materials of his time to create a "Biological Hard Drive" that could only be activated by a specific genetic signature.

"Let’s try it," Chen Mo said.

He opened the umbrella, shielding the basement lights. A red glow enveloped Lin Xiaocao.

Chen Mo gently pricked her finger.

A bead of blood emerged.

He let the drop of blood fall onto the chipped corner of the suanpan.

*Bzzzt—*

A faint sound of static.

The blood didn't slide off; instead, it was absorbed by the blackened wood like water hitting a sponge.

Immediately, every bead on the suanpan began to glow with a faint, bioluminescent light.

It was the halo of a bio-electric current.

*Click.*

The computer screen suddenly flared to life.

The genetic map, previously dismissed as gibberish, began to reconstruct itself frantically upon receiving the signal from the blood.

The red helices rearranged themselves into an entirely new form.

It was no longer broken.

It was **complete**.

A line of green text leaped onto the screen:

**【基因锁已解除。】**
**[Genetic Lock Disengaged.]**
**【样本状态：完美进化体。】**
**[Subject Status: Perfect Evolutionary Entity.]**
**【隐藏属性：免疫所有已知病毒。】**
**[Hidden Attribute: Immunity to all known viruses.]**

Chen Mo, Su Huai, and Fu Mu stood in stunned silence.

Old Zhao hadn't lied. Lin Xiaocao was indeed a "Virus."

But she wasn't a virus that would destroy humanity.

She was the **Antidote**.

She was the next stage of human evolution. She possessed a built-in immune system capable of resisting every plague.

The system wanted to kill her not because she was sick.

But because she was **too perfect**.

Too perfect to the point where the current medical infrastructure, the pharmaceutical companies, and the entire "survival of the fittest" logic of human society would become obsolete.

If no one got sick, how would hospitals make money? What would be the purpose of the Social Security Bureau?

"My god," Fu Mu muttered to himself. "This is the real 'Malicious Compliance'."

"The system, to maintain its own operation and profit, must strangle perfect evolution."

"It forbids perfection because perfection means **we no longer need the system**."

Chen Mo looked at the sleeping girl.

She wasn't a burden. She was a miracle.

And they had nearly murdered that miracle with their own hands.

"Does Old Zhao know?" Su Huai asked.

"He probably doesn't," Chen Mo said. "He only saw the first layer—the genetic defect. Only the key, this suanpan, can see the second layer: the perfect evolution."

"I suspect that is what Zhang San saw a thousand years ago, too."

Chen Mo gripped the suanpan tight.

"Zhang San couldn't save the girl in the red coat because he lacked the power."

"But I have it."

"And I’m going to drag this truth into the light."
