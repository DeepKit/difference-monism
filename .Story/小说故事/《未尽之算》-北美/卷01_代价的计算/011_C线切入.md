# 第11集：C线切入

> **《未尽之算》·第11集·C线切入**
> **本卷进度：11/24 | 方屿的数据：加载中 | 系统倒计时：999集**

---

The underground garage.

This was the Social Security Bureau’s blind spot. No signal, no surveillance—only the biting stench of gasoline and the distant, rhythmic wail of sirens. The sound felt like the howl of some unseen beast, echoing through the hollow concrete maze.

Chen Mo, clutching Lin Xiaocao, sprinted toward a black Jinbei van tucked in a far corner.

The body of the van was caked in dust, and the windows were covered in dark, anti-shatter film. It looked like a piece of scrap waiting for the junkyard.

*Slide—CLACK.*

The side door was thrown open with violent force.

A young man with thick-rimmed glasses and a rumpled flannel shirt leaned out. His hair looked like a bird’s nest, his eyes were shot through with red veins, and he was mid-bite into a half-eaten burger.

"Get in! Now!"

It was Fang Yu. An intern at the Social Security Data Center, and one of the underprivileged students Chen Mo had once sponsored.

Chen Mo shoved Lin Xiaocao into the back. The rear of the van was a chaotic heap of electronic hardware, tangled cables, and cases of bottled water.

He carefully placed the girl in a relatively clean corner, covering her with his windbreaker. Then he hauled himself in, slamming the door shut with a heavy thud.

"Hold on!"

Fang Yu stuffed the rest of the burger into his mouth and floored it.

The seemingly dilapidated van let out a roar like a caged predator. The engine had clearly been modified; the G-force pinned Chen Mo against the seat. The van shot out of the garage like a black bullet, merging into the river of nighttime traffic.

"Where are we going?" Chen Mo wheezed. His heart was hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird.

"To my hideout." Fang Yu pushed his glasses up, flicking his gaze to the rearview mirror. "Old Zhao will have every traffic cam in the city on lockdown. But I just backdoored the Transit Authority server. I gave this van the digital 'skin' of a garbage truck. As long as we don't get pulled over face-to-face, we’re invisible to the 'Sky Eye' (天眼)."

Chen Mo let out a long, ragged breath. He collapsed into the seat, feeling as if every bone in his body had been ground to powder.

That metallic taste in his throat was growing heavier. No matter how much he swallowed, it wouldn't go away.

His hands were shaking—not just from nerves, but from the uncontrolled spasms in his muscles.

The radiation was beginning to hollow out his nervous system.

The cabin was thick with the scent of gasoline, old leather, the ozone smell of overheating electronics, and the sour tang of Fang Yu, who clearly hadn't showered in days.

But the stench made him feel safe.

He looked back at Lin Xiaocao. She had fallen asleep, her small hand still gripping the candy wrapper. The shadows of streetlights flickered across her pale face in a rhythmic, strobing pulse.

"Fang Yu, thank you," Chen Mo whispered.

"Don't thank me." Fang Yu shifted gears with practiced ease, his eyes locked on the dashboard. "I'm not doing this to help you, Chen-ge. You know me. I'm a rational egoist."

"Then why?"

"To validate my model."

"What model?"

"**The Human Life Pricing Model**."

---

Fang Yu flicked a switch on the dash.

The speakers didn't play music. Instead, a cold, detached English broadcast filtered through, crackling with static:

*"...Reuters reports that Ford Motor Company has been ordered to pay $3.5 million to victims of the Pinto fuel tank explosions. Ford officials stated they intend to file an appeal..."*

"What is this?" Chen Mo asked.

"History," Fang Yu said. "The foundational logic of the system."

He kept one hand on the wheel and tossed a tablet to Chen Mo with the other.

"Look at this. It’s an internal Ford memo from 1971. I dug it out of a deep-web archive."

Chen Mo took it. On the screen was a grainy scan of a yellowed spreadsheet, stamped with "TOP SECRET."

The report listed two sets of numbers that turned Chen Mo’s stomach:

**PROPOSAL A: RECALL ALL PINTO MODELS. INSTALL SAFETY VALVE.**
*   Part required: A simple rubber gasket.
*   Cost per vehicle: $11.
*   Total recall volume: 12.5 million units.
*   **TOTAL COST: $137 Million.**

**PROPOSAL B: NO RECALL. COMPENSATE VICTIMS.**
*   Estimated casualties (based on crash data): 180 deaths, 180 severe burn injuries.
*   Death compensation (legal standard at the time): $200,000 per person.
*   Burn compensation: $67,000 per person.
*   Vehicle loss: $700 per unit.
*   **TOTAL COMPENSATION: $49.5 Million.**

Chen Mo’s hand twitched.

The numbers felt like needles stitching his eyes shut.

"Do you see?" Fang Yu’s voice was hollow, like he was reciting a funeral dirge. "In the eyes of those Ford executives, these weren't people. It was a math problem."

"137 Million > 49.5 Million."

"The conclusion was simple: **Do not recall**."

"Let it burn. Let those 180 people die. Let them turn into charcoal in their own driveways. Because writing a check is cheaper than fixing a machine."

Fang Yu slapped the steering wheel.

"Chen-ge, do you know how my father died?"

Chen Mo paused. He vaguely remembered Fang Yu’s file mentioning a "single-parent household."

"He was a construction worker. To meet a deadline, the foreman skipped the safety nets. He fell from the twentieth floor."

"What was the payout?"

"600,000 yuan. The maximum for industrial death at the time."

Fang Yu let out a tired, cynical laugh.

"Later, I found out the safety net would have cost a million. Plus another million in penalties for the two-day delay to install it."

"Two million versus 600,000."

"My father’s life wasn't worth the price of the net."

Silence filled the van, punctuated only by the hum of tires on asphalt.

Chen Mo looked at the city blurred outside the window.

Neon lights flickered, dressing the city in a garment of a trillion lights. But behind the grandeur, beneath the foundation of every skyscraper—was there a spreadsheet like this buried in the dark?

"Is the Social Security system calculated this way, too?" Chen Mo asked.

"Precisely," Fang Yu nodded. "QALYs (Quality-Adjusted Life Years) is just Pinto 2.0."

"The system never says 'You aren't worth it.' It says 'Your treatment cost exceeds the threshold for optimal resource allocation'."

"It uses complex formulas and noble excuses about 'The Greater Good' to bury the simple truth: **Your life is not worth the market price.**"

---

"Do you know why the Titanic didn't carry enough lifeboats?" Fang Yu asked suddenly.

"Weight issues?"

"No. Too expensive. And too ugly."

Fang Yu swiped to a new file—a schematic from the White Star Line.

"The original blueprint had enough boats for everyone. But the owner, Ismay, complained they blocked the view for the First Class passengers. It would hurt ticket sales."

"He ran the numbers: the cost of one extra lifeboat—maintenance, space, lost revenue—was roughly 500 pounds."

"The compensation for a drowned Third Class passenger, under the law of 1912? 50 pounds."

"**500 > 50.**"

"So, the boats were cut. They kept only the legal minimum."

Fang Yu swerved to avoid a patrol car. The red-and-blue strobes licked across the windshield, briefly illuminating Chen Mo’s pale, sweat-streaked face.

"Chen Mo, right now, in the eyes of the system, you are that idiot engineer trying to add lifeboats to a ship that 'cannot sink'."

"If you add them, the company loses money. The shareholders curse you."
"If you add them, the ship looks ugly. The First Class guests curse you."
"And if the ship never hits the iceberg, everyone says you wasted their budget. Only if it sinks do they remember you were right."

"But Chen Mo," Fang Yu looked at him through the mirror, "the ship hasn't hit the iceberg yet."

Yes. The ship hadn't sunk.

The pension funds were still flowing, the city was still thriving, and most people were doing just fine.

Only Lin Xiaocao was about to die.

Was he really going to sabotage the entire ship just to save a single stowaway?

Chen Mo looked at the girl.

She was deep in sleep. Her eyelashes trembled slightly—a fragile porcelain doll in a world of industrial concrete.

Perhaps she had seen it all along: this ship was magnificent, but people like her were always in steerage.

No—not even steerage. She was the cost the captain was prepared to pay.

**We are all just 'optimized' costs on someone's balance sheet.**

"No," Chen Mo said suddenly.

"What do you mean, no?"

"The Titanic math was wrong."

Chen Mo tossed the tablet back to Fang Yu.

"White Star only calculated the 'Legal Compensation.' They ignored **'Reputational Bankruptcy.'**"

"After the Titanic sank, White Star's stock plummeted until they were forced into a merger. They missed that in their spreadsheet."

"Ford, too. When the Pinto memo leaked, it cost them billions in market share. And the jury hit them with $125 million in punitive damages—far more than the cost of the recall."

Chen Mo’s eyes sharpened.

He had found the system's blind spot.

The algorithm was based on static data. it assumed that as long as the lid stayed on the pot, there was no extra cost.

But in the information age, there is no lid.

"The system can calculate 'Explicit Costs' like payouts. It cannot calculate 'Implicit Costs' like a crisis of faith."

"If the public finds out the Social Security Bureau is letting children die to save a few pennies..."

"Every person in the city will panic. How much is that worth?"

Fang Yu hesitated. He began to run the mental math at high speed.

"For every 1% drop in social trust, the fund contribution rate drops by 5%... God."

Fang Yu’s hand twitched, nearly sending the van into a guardrail.

"That’s tens of billions."

"Yes. Tens of billions."

Chen Mo smiled. It wasn't a happy smile; it was the look of a man who had finally found the detonator.

"That is our leverage."

"Old Zhao thinks he's saving the system money. He's actually planting a bomb under the foundation."

"If this bomb goes off, the entire system collapses."

"So," Chen Mo pulled out his phone, "we don't need to beg the system to save lives. We just need to tell it: **'If you don't save her, you will die a far more expensive death.'**"

"What are you going to do?" Fang Yu asked.

"I'm opening a short position."

Chen Mo opened his social media app.

He didn't post a picture of Lin Xiaocao. He didn't post a rant against Old Zhao.

He posted a single image.

The photo he had taken in the basement: the **Forced Discharge Confirmation Form**.

It had Old Zhao’s signature. It had that glaring "NEGATIVE ASSET" stamp.

The caption was only one sentence:

**“原来在系统眼里，我们的命是有标价的。你猜你值多少钱？”**
**[So in the eyes of the system, our lives have a price tag. I wonder what you're worth?]**

*Send.*

---

One minute.

Two likes.

Five minutes.

One hundred shares.

Thirty minutes later.

The post was trending.

**#WhatAreYouWorth#**
**#SocialSecurityNegativeAssetList#**

Fear spread like a plague through the network. Everyone was asking the same question: *Am I a negative asset? If I get sick, will the system 'optimize' me too?*

"You're crazy," Fang Yu said, looking at the numbers cascading across his phone screen. "You're manufacturing a panic."

"No. I'm **Shorting** the system."

Chen Mo looked out at the dark road ahead.

"Since the system treats human lives as stock, I’ll short that stock to zero."

"I'm going to make the system's credit hit the floor."

"Only when the 'stock price' hits bottom will they remember that human life was never meant for trade."

The van entered a dark tunnel.

The lights inside were dim, the tires hitting the speed bumps with a rhythmic *thump, thump*.

There was no light at the end yet.

But Chen Mo knew he had lit a fire.

A fire that would burn through the hold of the Titanic—and through the firewall of the Social Security Bureau.
