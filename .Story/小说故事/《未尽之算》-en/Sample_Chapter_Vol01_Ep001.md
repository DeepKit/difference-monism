## Episode 001: The Negative Value

### [Ethan Cole]

The air conditioning in the NewCare processing center is set to exactly 68 degrees.

I’m wearing a sweater I bought at Target, staring at two monitors. On the left screen is a photo of a girl. Her name is Lily. She’s seven. She has Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia, and she’s bald, smiling like she doesn’t know she’s already dead.

On the right screen is a spreadsheet.

**Claim ID**: #8849-K
**Claimant**: Lily Harper
**Treatment**: CAR-T Cell Therapy
**Cost**: $475,000
**Status**: **PENDING**

My job isn’t to approve this. My job is to find a reason not to.

The system calls it "Cost Containment." I call it "Find the Typo." If the doctor misspelled the drug name? Denied. If the date of service is off by one day? Denied. If the parents missed a premium payment by three cents six months ago? Denied.

And if everything is perfect—if the paperwork is flawless, the diagnosis is confirmed, and the treatment is the only thing that will save her life?

Then I look at the algorithm.

The algorithm has flagged Lily as a "High-Risk Asset." Not a patient. An asset.

A notification pings. It’s Brad.

Brad is my Team Lead. He’s twenty-six, wears a Patagonia fleece vest indoors, and drinks kombucha out of a mason jar. He talks about "synergy" and "bandwidth" while people die in our inbox.

He rolls his Herman Miller chair over to my cubicle. The wheels are silent on the gray carpet.

"Ethan, buddy," Brad says, tapping my desk. "You’ve been staring at the Harper file for twelve minutes. Your Average Handling Time is tanking."

"It’s a pediatric leukemia case, Brad. It’s complicated."

"It’s not," Brad says. He points a manicured finger at the screen. "Look at the QALY score."

**QALY**: Quality-Adjusted Life Year.
It’s the math we use to put a price tag on a heartbeat.

"The projection says she has a 40% chance of remission," Brad explains, like he’s teaching a toddler. "Even if we pay the half-million, she likely relapses in five years. Her lifelong contribution to the premium pool is projected to be... negative."

He smiles. It’s a practiced, sympathetic smile. The kind they teach in HR seminars.

"It’s a tragedy, Ethan. Truly. But we have a fiduciary duty to the pool. If we pay for every hopeless case, premiums go up for everyone. Mrs. Johnson can’t afford her insulin. Mr. Smith loses his coverage."

He leans in, whispering the corporate gospel.

"We deny Lily to save the others. It’s the ethical choice."

The Ethical Choice.

I look at Lily’s photo. She’s holding a stuffed bear that’s missing an eye.

I look at the denial code Brad is waiting for me to enter. **Code 34-B: Experimental Treatment Exclusion.** It’s a lie. CAR-T isn’t experimental anymore. But the policy language is vague enough that we can argue it in court for three years.

Lily doesn’t have three years. She has three weeks.

"Just click the button, Ethan," Brad says, checking his Apple Watch. "I need you back above 95% efficiency by lunch. We’re optimizing for Q3."

He rolls away.

I put my hand on the mouse. The plastic is warm.

My rent is due in four days. I have $800 in my checking account. My student loans are eating me alive. If I don't hit my metrics, I lose my bonus. If I lose my bonus, I miss rent.

If I get fired, I lose my own health insurance. And I have that weird lump on my back I haven't checked yet.

The system is perfect. It holds a gun to my head and tells me to pull the trigger on someone else.

I hover the cursor over **[DENY]**.

It’s easy. Just a click. One pixel changes from gray to red. A letter is auto-generated and mailed to Lily’s parents. They’ll cry. They’ll call customer service. They’ll get put on hold for four hours.

And Lily will die.

And I will make rent.

I look at the spreadsheet again. The algorithm is perfect. It has calculated everything: the cost of the drug, the probability of survival, the inflation of medical assets.

But it missed one thing.

I open a second window. I pull up the **NewCare Internal Compliance Manual**. It’s a 4,000-page PDF that nobody reads. It’s designed to be unreadable.

But I’ve read it. I’ve read it six times.

I scroll to **Section 104, Paragraph C**.

*“In the event of a conflict between the Actuarial Model and the Attending Physician’s 'Immediate Life Threat' designation, the claim must be placed in **Mandatory Peer Review Hold**.”*

Peer Review Hold.

It doesn’t mean "Approved."
It means "Freeze."

If I trigger a Peer Review Hold, the claim cannot be denied. It has to be reviewed by a panel of three board-certified oncologists.

"Ethan?" Brad calls from his desk. "Did you clear the queue?"

"Working on it," I say.

I know what happens if I trigger Section 104.
The system hates Peer Review. It costs the company $5,000 per hour to convene the panel. It ruins the efficiency metrics for the entire department. It triggers an automatic audit of *my* workstation.

Brad will come back over. He won’t be smiling.
My efficiency score will drop to zero.
I will definitely lose my bonus.
I might lose my job.

I look at Lily’s missing-eye bear.
I look at my bank balance on my phone.

The cursor trembles.

"Fuck Q3," I whisper.

I don't click **[DENY]**.

I open the command line console (Ctrl+Shift+C). Most adjusters don't even know this exists. I type in the override command that hasn't been used in this building since 2019.

`> INVOKE_PROTOCOL: 104_C`
`> REASON: LOGICAL_DISCREPANCY_DETECTED`

I hit Enter.

The screen freezes. A spinning red circle appears.

Across the room, at Brad’s desk, a red light starts flashing on his dashboard. It’s silent, but it’s screaming.

Brad’s head snaps up. He looks at me. His eyes go wide.

He stands up, knocking his kombucha over. "Ethan! What did you just do?"

I lean back in my cheap chair. The air conditioning suddenly feels very, very cold.

"I just cost us more than the girl's treatment," I say to myself. "And I'm just getting started."
