## 1. Responsibility as Structural Attribution

Responsibility is not a metaphysical property of free will. It is the structural attribution of high-consequence actions to agents whose decision paths can be traced. Responsibility requires: (a) a causal chain connecting agent to consequence, (b) a decision node where alternative paths were structurally available, (c) an attribution mechanism that links consequence back to node.

## 2. The Five-Layer Responsibility Model

Responsibility operates at five distinct layers:
1. **Causal responsibility**: who actually caused, co-caused, or failed to prevent the consequence
2. **Role responsibility**: who was structurally positioned to warn, review, approve, freeze, or compensate
3. **Epistemic responsibility**: who knew, should have known, or was positioned to know
4. **Institutional responsibility**: who has formally traceable, appealable, compensatable interfaces
5. **Normative responsibility**: who should bear heightened blame, condemnation, and justificatory pressure — applied only after the first four layers are clarified

## 3. The Anti-Conflation Principle

These five layers must not be conflated. Institutional traceability is not moral culpability. Causal contribution is not normative blame. Conflating them produces either: (a) morally overburdened individuals who happened to be the last node in the chain, or (b) structurally protected decision-makers who hide behind "collective responsibility."

## 4. From Attribution to Obligation

Once responsibility is structurally attributed, normative obligation follows: the identified agent has a structural duty to participate in repair, compensation, and system correction. This duty is not optional — it is the structural consequence of occupying a decision node in a multi-agent network.
