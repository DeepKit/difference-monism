I am submitting an original research article titled "The Five-Contract Model and the Responsibility Closure Spectrum" for consideration in AI & Society.

**What this paper argues**

When AI systems make or influence high-impact decisions, the question "who is responsible?" becomes structurally ill-defined. Traditional accountability frameworks assume human authorship and traceable decision chains—assumptions that no longer hold. This paper proposes the Accountability Threshold theory (TAT), introducing two innovations: (1) a five-contract model (Subject, Interface, Evidence, Freeze, Compensation) that operationalizes responsibility for AI-augmented systems, and (2) a responsibility closure spectrum that replaces binary "responsible/not responsible" judgments with graded structural requirements.

**What is genuinely new**

1. The five contracts are not ethical aspirations but structural requirements—each specifies what must be in place before a specific type of responsibility gap is closed. A system missing the Freeze contract (independent authority to pause deployment) has a structural vulnerability, not merely a policy gap.

2. The closure spectrum introduces "controlled ambiguity" as a legitimate intermediate state—not all situations require full closure, but all require minimum guardrails (audit trail, trigger threshold, escalation interface, compensation interface).

3. Two worked case studies (autonomous vehicle accident, AI medical advisory error) demonstrate the framework's applicability to real incidents, showing how the five contracts would have identified the structural failure before harm occurred.

**Why AI & Society**

AI & Society publishes work at the intersection of AI systems and their social/governance implications. This paper provides a concrete structural framework—not another set of ethical principles, but a set of operational contracts that can be checked, verified, and enforced. It directly engages the journal's core concern: how AI systems can be governed in ways that preserve accountability.
