# Perception Is Perturbation: The Structural Boundaries of Knowledge

## DM.P06 · Perceptual Perturbation and the Boundaries of Cognition

> **Author**: Yi Fu (付毅, ODDFounder, fuyi.it@live.cn). ORCID: 0009-0008-1251-2632

> **All cognition occurs under perturbation. The question is not whether knowledge is "objective," but under what structural conditions perturbation is minimized and its residue acknowledged.**

---

P00-P05 have established: the world as difference-structures, emergence, organisms with internality. P06 asks:

> **Given that every act of observation perturbs what is observed, what are the structural limits of knowledge?**

---

## 1. Perception as Interaction, Not Passive Reception

A perceiving system does not receive the world — it interacts with it. Every perceptual act is a perturbation event: the perceiving structure and the perceived structure mutually constrain each other's feasible states. There is no "view from nowhere."

## 2. The Perturbation Minimum

While zero-perturbation observation is impossible, perturbation-minimizing observation is achievable. Sciences succeed not by eliminating perturbation but by: (a) making perturbation regular and measurable, (b) designing instruments whose perturbation characteristics are known, (c) building models that account for the perturbation they introduce.

## 3. Three Sources of Cognitive Boundary

Cognition is bounded by three structural facts:
1. **Perturbation ineliminability**: every observation changes the observed
2. **Model underdetermination**: multiple models can fit the same perturbation-affected data
3. **Boundary of the observer**: the observer's own structural limits determine what differences it can register

## 4. Knowledge Without Certainty

This is not relativism. It is the structural condition of all finite knowing systems. Knowledge is possible, improvable, and cumulative — but it is always knowledge-from-a-position, knowledge-under-perturbation. The goal is not certainty but progressively better perturbation accounting.

---

> **We do not see the world as it is. We see it as our structure allows us to be perturbed by it. This is the boundary of cognition — not a failure to be overcome, but a condition to be accounted for.**
