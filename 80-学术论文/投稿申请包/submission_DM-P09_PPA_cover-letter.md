I am submitting an original research article titled "Crisis Compression Must Be Reversible" for consideration in Philosophy & Public Affairs.

**What this paper argues**

When civilizations face existential threats, they compress—concentrating resources, suspending normal procedures, centralizing decisions. This compression is sometimes necessary. But the paper argues that the central criterion for evaluating crisis governance is not whether compression was justified at the moment of decision, but whether the compression is reversible. A crisis response that cannot be reversed is not a survival strategy—it is a civilizational phase transition in which the pre-crisis structure is permanently lost.

The paper identifies "correction capability"—the presence and functioning of institutionalized error-correction mechanisms—as the variable that most consistently distinguishes persisting from collapsing systems, across six historical case studies spanning political institutions, industrial systems, corporate governance, and constitutional design.

**What is genuinely new**

1. The restorability criterion: crisis compression is evaluated not by its immediate necessity but by the structural presence of decompression pathways. Minimum requirements include declared scope/time limits, triggered (not goodwill-dependent) decompression, preserved correction interfaces during compression, and post-compression public accounting.

2. The bearer condition distinction: continuity is a bearer condition of civilization, not a supreme value. "Whatever increases survival rates is therefore most legitimate" is explicitly rejected.

3. A 9+1 factor model of civilizational structure, with the correction factor as the cross-cutting variable—not merely one factor among many, but the structural immune system whose failure explains collapse patterns across domains.

**Why Philosophy & Public Affairs**

This paper makes a direct contribution to political philosophy's treatment of emergency powers, constitutional design, and institutional legitimacy. It engages the same problem space as Ackerman's "emergency constitution," Schmitt's state of exception, and the post-9/11 literature on security vs. liberty—but reframes the question from "when is compression justified?" to "is the compression structurally reversible?" This reframing has concrete institutional implications that the paper develops.
