# The Hard Problem Is Not a Bridge Between Two Substances but the Formation of Internality
*A Minimal Structural Reframing of the Consciousness Problem*

**Status**: English full draft v0.1 for submission package

## Abstract

This paper argues that the hard problem remains stalled not only because it is difficult, but because it is framed too strongly from the outset. Physical process and experience are first separated into two domains, and only then are we asked how they might be bridged. The paper reformulates the issue as the formation of internality: what kind of structure gives rise to states that can exist only from the inside. On this reformulation, consciousness is no longer primarily a problem of connecting two substances, but of identifying the minimal organizational threshold at which stable boundary, internal differentiability, recursive inheritance, and subject-level integration yield internality. The paper does not close qualitative feel, nor does it treat these four thresholds as empirical laws. Its narrower claim is that if first-person and third-person access are two non-interchangeable ways of entering the same structure, then any translation scheme that demands no remainder, structural preservation, and full replacement of first-person access should not be regarded as obtainable. The contribution is not a new mental entity, but a relocation of the dispute to comparative structural thresholds, boundary cases, and explicit failure conditions.

**Keywords**: consciousness; hard problem; internality; organizational threshold; first-person; third-person

## 1. Introduction

The hard problem is often compressed into a single question: why should physical processes be accompanied by subjective experience? The persistence of that formulation comes not only from the difficulty of the issue, but from the fact that it begins by placing the problem into two domains and then demanding a bridge between them. Once that framing is accepted, debate oscillates between two extremes: either experience is re-mystified, or the entire problem is flattened into report, function, or illusion-generation.

This paper withdraws that overly strong starting point. Instead of assuming that the physical is on one side and experience on another, it asks a prior question: what kind of structure yields state-differences that exist only from within the structure itself? On that reformulation, the consciousness problem is no longer primarily about connecting two substances. It becomes a question of what kind of organization forms internality, and under what conditions such internality can support first-person access.

The paper therefore defends a narrower claim. The hard problem should first be rewritten as the problem of the formation of internality. Stronger questions about qualitative feel, detailed neural realization, or value-generation remain for later work.

## 2. Literature Position

This paper locates itself between four strong lines of opposition. It responds to bridge-style formulations of the hard problem associated with Chalmers, but refuses to keep the problem fixed at “how physical process and experience are connected.” It accepts the empirical value of GNW-style accounts associated with Dehaene in matters of accessibility, global broadcast, and task integration, yet denies that global broadcast is identical with internality itself. It accepts that IIT-style accounts associated with Tononi impose a real pressure concerning integration and irreducible organization, while refusing to elevate integration strength directly into first-person access. It also shares the anti-dualist baseline of illusionist positions associated with Dennett and Frankish, while refusing to collapse the problem of experience into report-generation or self-model error.

The point is therefore not to reject empirical science, nor to insist that consciousness can only be carried by an extra entity. The point is to force the dispute back toward structural presuppositions, boundary conditions, evidential interfaces, and explicit failure lines.

## 3. Core Claim: The Hard Problem Should First Be Rewritten as a Problem of Internality

The core claim can be compressed into two sentences. First, consciousness is not primarily the problem of how two substances are bridged, but of what kind of structure gives rise to state-differences that can exist only from the inside. Second, once the problem is reformulated in this way, both crude reduction and crude mystification lose part of their force.

The key is not merely to rename experience. It is to ask what counts as an internally established difference. If a system has only inputs and outputs, but no internal state-differences that enter its own later conditions of existence, then no matter how complex it appears from outside, it should not yet count as a strong candidate for minimal internality. If, by contrast, a structure has boundary and internal differentiability and those differentiations are inherited across time and jointly constrain later processing, then the first-person question no longer appears as an inexplicable add-on. It appears as a problem that begins only after an organizational threshold has been crossed.

## 4. A Minimal Definition of Internality and the Four Thresholds

The paper compresses internality into the following minimal definition:

> Internality is a bounded structure whose partial state-differences asymmetrically enter that structure’s own later conditions of existence.

This definition contains at least three requirements. First, the structure must have a boundary; otherwise there is no such thing as its “own later conditions.” Second, the structure must possess internally distinguishable states; otherwise there are no internal differences to speak of. Third, these differences must feed back into the structure’s own later organization rather than merely altering some external target. Internality is therefore not “inside” in a spatial sense, but in the sense of self-relevant conditions of continuation.

Minimal internality, however, cannot remain at that level of abstraction. The paper expresses its candidate threshold in four jointly required conditions:

1. Stable boundary
2. Internal state-differentiability
3. Recursive inheritance into later organization
4. Subject-level integration

These conditions do not constitute an empirical law. They function as threshold criteria. Their purpose is to exclude two symmetrical errors: the idea that every differential unit is already a micro-experience, and the idea that internality appears as a pure mystery detached from earlier structural conditions.

## 5. Minimum Distinctions from Strong Rival Approaches

If the four thresholds merely rename old disputes, then the paper has not advanced the issue. It therefore fixes at least four minimum points of differentiation.

First, against GNW-style readings, the paper does not deny the importance of broadcast and accessibility, but refuses to identify them with internality itself. Second, against IIT-style readings, it does not deny the relevance of integration or irreducibility, but refuses to treat integration strength as already sufficient for first-person access. Third, against strong HOT-style readings, it refuses to treat higher-order representation as the first threshold rather than as a later reflexive layer. Fourth, against strong illusionism, it refuses to cancel the problem of internally established difference by redescribing it as mere report architecture.

The weaker but more disciplined claim is that these routes may each capture part of the evidential chain. Yet unless stable boundary, internal differentiability, recursive inheritance, and subject-level integration are jointly in place, they do not yet satisfy the paper’s threshold for minimal internality.

## 6. The Specific Pressure from Strong Illusionism

Among rival positions, illusionism generates the sharpest pressure because it shares the anti-dualist baseline while repeatedly forcing any theory that retains the experience question to explain what exactly is being explained: report, judgment, or appearance-differences that matter for the system itself.

To avoid caricature, the paper does not require illusionism to explain every phenomenological detail at once. It asks instead whether strong illusionism can absorb five differences:

1. Stable internal differentiation
2. Memory deposition
3. Binding into later choice
4. No-report and low-report boundary cases
5. The boundary of complete third-person translation

Illusionism does not lose because it can explain some report phenomena. It loses only if it cannot jointly absorb these five lines while remaining structurally thinner. Until that point, the minimal internality hypothesis retains a reason to remain on the table.

## 7. Evidence Interfaces and Update Rules

The paper does not stage the consciousness problem as a purely metaphysical monologue. It requires at least a limited interface with boundary cases. Their role is not to prove the theory in one stroke, but to establish update windows.

The most important cases are:

1. DOC / MCS
2. Propofol anesthesia
3. Split-brain cases
4. No-report paradigms
5. Blindsight

DOC / MCS matters because it blocks the direct equation of no overt report with no internal entry. Propofol matters because it repeatedly compresses loss of consciousness, behavioral suspension, and loss of later recall into a relatively reproducible window. Split-brain cases matter because they pressure the assumption that subject unity must be an indivisible atom. No-report paradigms matter because they detach report from consciousness without eliminating report from the evidential chain. Blindsight matters because it forces a distinction between successful processing and intact first-person entry.

These cases do not by themselves settle the matter. They constrain what counts as an acceptable answer form and where the theory must yield if counter-evidence converges.

## 8. Negative Boundary: Complete Third-Person Translation Is Not Obtainable

The paper’s principal negative claim is that complete third-person translation is not obtainable. This is not a rhetorical escape hatch; it must remain vulnerable to weakening and defeat.

The weakest and clearest version of the claim is this: the paper does not reject every form of third-person modeling, correspondence, or prediction. It rejects a stronger ambition according to which third-person description could achieve no remainder, preserve structure, and fully replace first-person access. If first-person and third-person access remain two non-interchangeable ways of entering the same structure, then such a total translation scheme should not be regarded as obtainable.

This does not amount to a full positive explanation of qualitative feel. It imposes a constraint on what should count as an answer. If a fully remainder-free translation becomes available across cases and paradigms, the paper must retreat.

## 9. Failure Conditions

To prevent the negative claim from becoming unfalsifiable, the paper states three levels of failure:

1. Weakening: third-person models stably predict broad experience-differences across report, memory, and later choice chains
2. Strong weakening: multiple paradigms and laboratories converge on models that absorb subject-level differentials with little residue
3. Defeat: a no-remainder, structure-preserving, fully substitutive third-person translation becomes reproducibly available

The paper also admits local revision triggers:

- If report/task models absorb the internal differential across DOC, no-report, and anesthesia windows, minimal internality must be contracted
- If subject-level integration can be drastically reduced without affecting first-person traces, the integration threshold must be rewritten
- If memory, rollback, and error-correction are largely assembled from outside while experiential structure remains intact, the boundary condition must be weakened

Defeat should not be triggered by one case alone, but by sustained convergence across paradigms.

## 10. Scope Limits

The paper treats only the position of felt entry, not the closure of what is felt. It establishes minimal structural preconditions rather than a full neural realization theory. It requires interface with empirical research but does not attempt to replace empirical research. Its aim is not to end consciousness debate, but to compress the dispute into a narrower and more adjudicable structural layer.

Its success condition is therefore modest. It needs to do at least two things:

1. Rewrite the hard problem from a bridge-style question into a problem of the formation of internality
2. Fix the relevant differences, evidence interfaces, and failure conditions so that the position can be compared, weakened, or defeated

## 11. Conclusion

The paper does not defend the thesis that consciousness has been fully explained. It defends a narrower but firmer claim: the consciousness problem is first a problem of the formation of internality, not of bridging two substances. Once the problem is rewritten that way, both crude reductionism and crude mystification lose force. The former erases internal entry too quickly; the latter re-entityfies it too quickly.

The positive claim retained here is minimal. Internality is a threshold organizational property, not a universally distributed micro-experience, and not a mysteriously inserted second substance. The decisive question is no longer whether physical process is “accompanied” by experience, but which organizational thresholds, boundary cases, and failure lines can reliably separate internal entry from mere report architecture. If a purely third-person route is to win, it must do more than explain report production. It must cross the full set of structural differences and boundary cases. Until then, declaring the hard problem already cancelled remains premature.
