Dear Editor,

I am submitting an original research article titled "The Hard Problem Is Not a Bridge Between Two Substances, But the Formation of Internality" for consideration in the Journal of Consciousness Studies.

**What this paper argues**

The paper reformulates the hard problem of consciousness. Rather than asking "how do physical processes bridge to subjective experience?"—a formulation that implicitly presupposes two substances requiring connection—it asks: "what kind of structure develops internality sufficient to host consciousness?" This reframes the hard problem from a bridging problem to a threshold problem.

**What is genuinely new**

1. Four threshold conditions for minimum internality are identified (stable boundary, internal differentiability, folded-back inheritance, subject-level integration) and defended as necessary conditions through a "minimum anti-collapse argument"—i.e., removing any one condition causes the concept of an "internal access point" to collapse into mere process, parallel chains, or borrowed inheritance.

2. A falsifiable negative boundary claim: complete third-person translation that residuelessly substitutes first-person access is impossible in principle—not because of mystical dualism, but because first-person and third-person occupy different structural access positions to the same organization. Defeat conditions are specified.

3. The framework is explicitly distinguished from IIT (integration alone is insufficient without subject-level folded-back inheritance), GNW (global broadcast is functional availability, not internality), and strong illusionism (which, if it admits internally differentiable self-representational states with downstream binding, has effectively conceded minimum internality).

**Why JCS**

JCS has published the core positions this paper engages: Chalmers' hard problem formulation, Dennett's quining qualia, Frankish's illusionism, and Tononi's IIT. This paper does not merely comment on these positions—it proposes a structural reframing that each of them must address. The paper's commitment to specifying falsification conditions aligns with JCS's tradition of publishable, testable philosophical claims.
