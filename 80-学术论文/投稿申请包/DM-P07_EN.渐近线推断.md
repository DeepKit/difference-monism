# Asymptotic Inference: Knowledge as Progressive Approximation Under Irreducible Perturbation

## DM.P07 · Asymptotic Inference and the Improvability of Knowledge

> **Author**: Yi Fu (付毅, ODDFounder, fuyi.it@live.cn). ORCID: 0009-0008-1251-2632

> **Knowledge is not absolute transparency, nor pure construction. It is asymptotic — approaching but never reaching a perturbation-free limit, yet capable of progressive improvement.**

---

P06 established that all cognition occurs under perturbation. P07 asks:

> **If knowledge is always perturbed, can it still improve? Can we get closer to the truth even if we can never arrive?**

---

## 1. The Asymptotic Model

Knowledge improvement follows an asymptotic curve: each successive model reduces the gap between prediction and observation, but the gap never reaches zero. This is not pessimism — it is the structural condition of finite knowing systems.

## 2. Why Improvement Is Possible Despite Perturbation

Three mechanisms enable progressive improvement:
1. **Cross-validation**: multiple independent perturbation channels converging on the same conclusion reduce perturbation-specific bias
2. **Perturbation accounting**: each generation of models accounts for the perturbation characteristics of its predecessor's instruments
3. **Failure-driven refinement**: when a model fails at a boundary case, that failure reveals previously unaccounted-for perturbation

## 3. Why the Limit Is Never Reached

The asymptotic limit cannot be reached because:
- Every measurement perturbs
- Every model simplifies
- Every observer has structural limits
- The world's complexity exceeds any finite model

## 4. The Practical Consequence

This has an engineering implication: **the goal of inquiry is not "the final answer" but "the next answer that accounts for the perturbation the previous answer introduced."** Science progresses not by eliminating perturbation but by successively better perturbation accounting.

---

> **We approach the truth asymptotically. We never arrive. But the approach is real, measurable, and improvable — and that is enough.**
