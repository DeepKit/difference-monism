# The Accountability Threshold (TAT)

> **Public note**: This English file is a companion summary to the Chinese TAT public review package.
> It is suitable for preprint-style release and public review, but it should not be described as a peer-reviewed final theory or as a completed global legal regime.

> **Author**: Yi Fu (付毅, ODDFounder, fuyi.it@live.cn)

---

## 1. Position

TAT is best described as a **responsibility architecture framework for high-impact systems**.

Built on the upstream path `DM -> ASTO -> ECET`, TAT focuses on a narrower question:

> When does a system cross the threshold at which its capacity to generate consequences exceeds the responsibility structure that can absorb, trace, interrupt, and compensate for those consequences?

TAT is not only about AI. It also applies to finance, automated infrastructure, biotechnology, platform governance, and other systems whose consequences can scale faster than accountability.

---

## 2. Core Claim

TAT proposes that large-scale capability expansion should not be treated as legitimate by default.

Before a high-impact system scales, it should have at least:

- identifiable responsibility subjects
- non-bypassable responsibility interfaces
- evidence preservation mechanisms
- executable suspend/recall authority
- pre-funded compensation capacity

Without these elements, a system may remain operational for some time, but its apparent stability is often purchased by externalizing costs to parties that never authorized those consequences.

---

## 3. Working Formula

TAT uses a working formula:

```text
G × U / L ≤ A₀ · R
```

Where:

- `G` = action generation rate
- `U` = consequence uncertainty
- `L` = regulatory or corrective delay
- `A₀` = absorption and repair capacity
- `R` = responsibility closure

This should be read as a governance and responsibility threshold, not as a fully proven natural law.

---

## 4. Two Types of Closure

TAT distinguishes:

- **physical closure**: collapse, elimination, resource exhaustion, or disaster-driven settlement
- **civilizational closure**: traceable responsibility, preserved evidence, executable interruption, and compensable repair

Nature often enforces physical closure.
Civilization, however, should not rely on collapse itself as its main accountability mechanism.

---

## 5. Practical Meaning

TAT does not promise a world without failure.

Its practical value lies in moving one question forward:

> Who bears the consequences before expansion occurs, not only after failure becomes irreversible?

This is why TAT emphasizes responsibility architecture rather than moral rhetoric alone.
