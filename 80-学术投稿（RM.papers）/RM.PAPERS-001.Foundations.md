# Abstract

Resonance Monism (RM) proposes a minimal ontological framework for the emergence of persistent structures in nonequilibrium systems. Instead of assuming pre-existing objects or spatial structures, RM begins from a primitive condition: the existence of distinguishable differences.

We introduce a **difference amplitude field** Φ as a generic representation of distinguishability and define **resonance closure** as the minimal dynamical condition required for persistence. Under this framework, existence corresponds to the formation of self-sustaining resonance circuits within the difference field.

The framework does not propose new physical laws; rather, it provides an ontological interpretation compatible with established theories of pattern formation and dissipative structures.

---

# 1 Introduction

The emergence of ordered structures from disordered states is a central problem across physics, chemistry, and biology.

Examples include

* Bénard convection
* chemical oscillations
* biological rhythms
* ecological cycles

Existing theories explain these phenomena through **nonequilibrium dynamics and nonlinear feedback**.

However, most theories begin by assuming the existence of objects, spatial coordinates, or specific physical interactions.

Resonance Monism adopts a different starting point.

Instead of beginning with objects, we begin with the minimal observable condition:

**distinction.**

A universe in which nothing can be distinguished from anything else is observationally equivalent to non-existence.

Therefore any observable structure must originate from the emergence and persistence of **distinguishable differences**.

The purpose of this paper is to introduce a minimal ontological framework describing how persistent structures arise from differences.

Subsequent work will connect this framework with dynamical theories of self-organization.

# 2 Chaotic Background

We begin by defining a state in which no distinction exists.

### Definition 2.1 Chaotic background

Let Φ be a scalar field defined on a configuration manifold ℳ.

A chaotic background satisfies

[
\nabla \Phi = 0
]

In this state every region has identical amplitude.

Therefore no local distinction exists.

Such a configuration is observationally indistinguishable.

---

### Definition 2.2 Fluctuations

In realistic systems, microscopic fluctuations occur.

We represent fluctuations as

[
\delta \Phi(x,t)
]

with

[
\langle \delta\Phi \rangle = 0
]

These fluctuations may generate temporary distinctions.

Most fluctuations decay.

Only some evolve into persistent structures.

---

# 3 Difference as Primitive

### Principle 3.1 Distinction principle

Distinction exists.

This principle is minimal: it states only that distinguishable states occur.

---

### Definition 3.1 Difference amplitude field

We introduce the **difference amplitude field**

[
\Phi(x,t)
]

which measures the magnitude of distinguishability.

Properties:

```
Φ ≥ 0
```

Spatial gradients represent local differences.

[
\nabla \Phi \neq 0
]

indicates the presence of distinction.

---

### Definition 3.2 Local distinction

A region contains a local distinction if

[
\Phi(x,t) > \Phi_{bg}
]

where Φ_bg is the background amplitude.

# 4 Resonance Closure

Not all differences persist.

Transient fluctuations decay unless supported by feedback.

---

### Definition 4.1 Positive reinforcement

Let the system evolve under a transformation

[
\Phi(t+\Delta t) = F(\Phi(t))
]

Reinforcement occurs when

[
|F(\Phi)| \ge |\Phi|
]

---

### Definition 4.2 Resonance circuit

A **resonance circuit** is a closed feedback structure satisfying

[
\Phi(t+n\Delta t) \approx \Phi(t)
]

for large n.

Such structures maintain amplitude over time.

---

### Theorem 4.1 Persistence condition

Persistent structures correspond to resonance circuits.

Sketch:

1 fluctuations decay without reinforcement
2 uncontrolled amplification destabilizes the system
3 stable persistence requires closed feedback loops

Therefore persistence corresponds to resonance closure.

---

# 5 Ontological Meaning of Existence

We now define existence in structural terms.

### Definition 5.1 Existence

A structure exists if it forms a stable resonance circuit.

Formally

[
Existence \iff \Phi_{closure} > 0
]

where Φ_closure represents the sustained amplitude.

---

Interpretation:

Existence is not primitive.

Existence emerges from **self-sustaining differences**.

Objects and systems correspond to particular resonance circuits.

# 6 Temporal Ordering

Resonance circuits evolve through ordered states

[
\Phi_1 → \Phi_2 → \Phi_3
]

---

### Definition 6.1 Temporal order

Temporal order is the ordering of states along a resonance trajectory.

Thus time appears as an ordering relation between successive states.

# 7 Spatial Structure

Spatial relationships emerge from gradients in the difference field.

---

### Definition 7.1 Spatial topology

Spatial topology can be represented by the connectivity induced by the gradient field

[
\nabla \Phi
]

Regions connected through gradients form adjacency relations.

Thus spatial structure emerges from the topology of the difference field.

# 8 Multi-Layer Resonance

Complex systems arise from interacting resonance circuits.

Let

```
R1 R2 ... Rn
```

be resonance circuits.

Coupling between circuits forms higher-level structures.

---

### Definition 8.1 Resonance network

A resonance network is a set of interacting circuits that collectively maintain stability.

Such networks appear in many self-organizing systems.

# 9 Epistemic Constraints

Observation is performed by resonance circuits.

Therefore observers detect only differences compatible with their own resonance structure.

Knowledge is therefore **resonance-bounded**.
